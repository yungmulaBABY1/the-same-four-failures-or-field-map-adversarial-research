What This Is
This document maps the fields where the same four academic failure modes are most
concentrated, gives domain experts a diagnostic method for identifying them, and provides
a step-by-step guide for running a methodological challenge from outside the institution.
It is the generalization layer of a four-paper body of work that began with the esports
gender literature. Those papers are the existence proof. This document is the field manual.
This is a living document. Corrections, additions, and domain-expert challenges to
specific field characterizations are welcomed. If you know a field on this list better than the
characterization here suggests — open an issue or submit a pull request.

Purpose
This is the field manual that the four-paper esports body of work implicitly promised.
In those papers, a domain expert with no academic credentials used adversarial AI
collaboration, multi-model review, and subtractive revision to expose systematic
weaknesses in the esports gender literature: an untested premise recycled through citation,
proxy variables that measured the wrong thing, findings from amateur populations exported
to explain elite performance, and a decisive test the field had never run.
The same four failure modes appear in many other high-stakes fields. The goal of this
document is not to declare entire fields invalid. It is to identify where the gap between what
a literature claims and what it has actually measured is large enough that a motivated
domain expert with the right tools could close it.
The method is replicable. The bottleneck is not infrastructure — it is domain knowledge and
the willingness to follow the evidence. This document is for practitioners, clinicians,
researchers, and domain experts who can see that a literature does not match the reality of
the domain they know from the inside.
Why This Document Exists
In 2025, a non-credentialed competitive gaming enthusiast with no institutional affiliation,
no library access, no co-authors, and no research infrastructure produced a four-paper
body of work in eleven days on an iPhone challenging the dominant academic literature on
gender and elite esports performance.
The challenge was methodological, not political. It documented a specific citation network
built around an untested premise, identified the proxy problems in the foundational studies,
showed the level-of-analysis errors that allowed sub-elite findings to be treated as
explanations for elite performance, and specified the decisive test the field had never run.
The papers are archived publicly with receipts. The methodology is documented. The
process is reproducible.
The esports case is one case. The same failure modes appear across nutrition science,
implicit bias research, education psychology, gender medicine, criminal justice research,
social psychology, and housing economics — among others. In each of these fields, there
are practitioners, clinicians, domain experts, and insiders who know that the literature’s
model does not match what they observe. Most of them do not have the infrastructure to
formalize that knowledge into an inspectable challenge.
That infrastructure now exists. This document explains how to use it.
What the Issues Are
The academic production problem. Research on high-stakes social questions is produced
by people with institutional incentives to confirm rather than test the dominant framework.
Career advancement, funding, peer review, and publication all flow more easily in the
direction of the consensus. Outsiders who challenge it face a double standard: their
methods are scrutinized more harshly than the methods of insiders who produced the
consensus.
The domain knowledge gap. The people who know the domain — practitioners, clinicians,
high-level performers, industry insiders — are often not the people producing the research.
The researchers have methodological training. The practitioners have ecological validity.
Neither alone is sufficient. The literature reflects the researchers’ blind spots because the
practitioners’ knowledge never entered the production process.
The proxy laundering problem. Measurements that are easy to collect and consistent with
the preferred conclusion get cited until they appear authoritative. The gap between what
was measured and what was claimed becomes invisible through repetition. Challenging the
proxy is treated as challenging the field.
The decisive test problem. In most of these fields, the test that would directly answer the
contested question has not been run. Sometimes the data exists and has not been
analyzed. Sometimes the test would be inconvenient. Sometimes the field has organized
itself around avoiding the question. The result is confident claims built on evidence that was
never designed to bear their weight.
The ratchet problem. Interventions designed around the untested premise produce metric
improvements that justify more intervention. The institution measures what confirms the
intervention. The underlying construct — actual learning, actual skill, actual health outcome,
actual behavioral change — is not measured or shows smaller effects than the metric. The
ratchet only turns one way.
The infrastructure gap. Until recently, challenging an academic consensus from outside
the institution required resources most domain experts do not have: literature access,
methodological training, writing infrastructure, publication channels, and time. That gap has
closed substantially. The bottleneck is now domain knowledge and adversarial judgment —
which domain experts have — not production infrastructure.

A Critical Warning: Test Your Own Claims — and Choose Your
Tools Carefully
If you use the wrong AI model for diagnostic work on contested empirical territory,
you will get a systematically softened picture of the failure modes in your field. You
will think the problem is smaller than it is. You will publish a weaker challenge than
the evidence supports. This section explains why and what to do instead.
TEST YOUR OWN CLAIMS FIRST
The diagnostic prompt in this document is designed to identify failure modes in other fields.
Before you use it, run it on yourself.
The strongest version of this methodology is not “find where others are wrong.” It is “find
where the evidence actually points, including when it points against your own intuitions.”
Domain experts have real advantages: ecological validity, confound recognition, construct
knowledge. They also have real failure modes: motivated reasoning toward conclusions their
experience prepared them to reach, overconfidence in intuitions that haven’t been formally
tested, and the same tendency to cite evidence that confirms their model and ignore
evidence that complicates it.
Before you publish a challenge, answer these questions honestly:
What is the strongest version of the claim you are challenging? Can you state it in terms
its proponents would recognize as fair?
Is there evidence that supports the field’s model, even partially? Have you engaged with
it?
What would change your conclusion? Have you specified this in writing before you
started, when you couldn’t yet see which way the evidence would cut?
Are you challenging the methodology or attributing motive? Methodology is
documentable. Motive requires a higher standard of evidence.
Have you run your argument past someone who disagrees with your conclusion and
asked them to identify the weakest points?
The meta-paper this document accompanies includes a correction log precisely because
the work is fallible. The esports papers specify what telemetry evidence would disconfirm
the model. That is not hedging — it is what separates adversarial research from advocacy.
If you cannot specify what would change your conclusion, you are writing a polemic.
Polemics have their place. They are not the same as methodological challenges.
CHOOSE YOUR AI TOOLS DELIBERATELY — THIS WILL AFFECT YOUR
RESULTS
Not all AI systems handle contested empirical territory the same way. The differences
matter and will affect the quality of your diagnostic work.
THE ANTICIPATORY COMPLIANCE PROBLEM
Some AI systems — most visibly ChatGPT in its default configuration — are trained with
reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) in ways that produce systematic
softening of controversial conclusions. This manifests as:
Adding reassurance sentences to critiques (“it is worth noting that the field has also
produced valuable insights…”)
Hedging claims that the evidence actually supports directly (“some researchers have
suggested that…”)
Inserting qualifications that were not present in your prompt and are not warranted by
the evidence
Flattening the distinction between “the methodology is weak” and “the methodology has
limitations”
Treating directness itself as a risk to be managed
This is not dishonesty. It is optimization for a different objective than yours. A model trained
to avoid generating content that upsets readers will systematically understate the strength
of methodological critiques on contested topics. It will produce output that feels balanced
but is epistemically weaker than the evidence warrants.
If you use a compliance-softened model to run your diagnostic prompt, you will get a
response that makes the failure modes in your field appear less severe than they are. You
will think the literature’s proxy is “somewhat limited” when the evidence supports “invalid
for the question being asked.” You will think the citation network is “relatively concentrated”
when it is “self-referential.” You will think the decisive test is “not yet run” when it is
“specifically avoided.”
MODEL ROUTING RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the experience documented in the meta-paper:
Grok — use for initial diagnostic mapping, direct engagement with contested empirical
claims, and willingness to name failure modes without softening. Grok is the right tool
for the question “is this proxy actually valid?” and will tell you directly when the answer
is no. Best for: running the diagnostic prompt, identifying the proxy problem,
characterizing the citation network.
Claude — use for long-form structure, synthesis across sources, cross-document
consistency, and building the argument architecture. Claude is the right tool for turning
a diagnostic finding into a structured challenge. Best for: organizing the argument,
checking internal consistency, producing readable documents, and systematic cross-
referencing.
ChatGPT — use specifically for adversarial stress-testing after your core argument is
stable. Ask it to generate the strongest objections to your challenge, identify the
weakest points, and find the places where your evidence is thinnest. Do not use it for
initial diagnostic work on contested territory and do not use it for drafting sections
where directness matters. Its compliance softening makes it useful as a hostile
reviewer but unreliable as a first-pass analyst. Best for: “what are the strongest
objections to this argument?” after the argument exists.
WARNING: This is not a hypothetical risk. In this project, ChatGPT repeatedly
softened direct methodological criticism on contested topics into safer institutional
language. That pattern is especially likely in the fields this guide addresses — sex
differences, gender medicine, policing, DEI, education, nutrition, housing — where
empirical claims carry moral or political charge. A compliance-optimized model will not
falsify the facts outright. It will do something subtler: preserve the structure of the
critique while weakening its force, adding reassurance clauses, false balance, and
institutional caveats until a direct methodological challenge becomes a polite literature
review. Run a voice restoration check. Ask yourself: did I write this, or did the model
smooth it into something I would not have said?
The practical instruction is blunt: do not use ChatGPT as the first-pass diagnostic tool for
contested empirical fields. Use it after the argument exists — as a hostile reviewer, source-
risk checker, and objection generator. Then run a voice restoration pass. This is role
assignment based on observed failure mode in this workflow, not a universal technical claim
about all model instances.
THE VOICE RESTORATION PROBLEM
If you use ChatGPT for drafting or editing passes on contested sections, run a voice
restoration check before publishing. Read the output and ask: did this model add
reassurance sentences that weren’t in my notes? Did it soften “invalid proxy” to “limited
proxy”? Did it insert “it is worth noting” before a point that needs no such qualification? Did
it add “while acknowledging the field’s legitimate contributions” to a paragraph that was
making a specific methodological point?
These additions feel like improvements when you are close to the work. They are not. They
signal to readers that the author is uncertain about claims the evidence actually supports,
and they give the field’s defenders language to use against the critique.
The meta-paper documents a voice restoration pass conducted on both companion papers
after a ChatGPT softening episode caused by hitting rate limits on other models. The
pattern is predictable and correctable — but only if you know to look for it.

The most recognizable fingerprint of compliance softening in methodological work:
The structure — “X has produced valuable insights into Y AND ALSO here is the
methodological problem with X” — appearing more than once or twice in the same
document. A confident author establishes the field’s legitimate contributions once, in the
introduction, as a good-faith framing device. They do not re-establish it before every
critique. When you see the acknowledgment sentence appearing repeatedly as a prefix to
methodological points, the model inserted it — not you.
Specific phrases to search for in your draft before publishing:
“it is worth noting that”
“it is important to acknowledge”
“the field has produced valuable insights”
“this does not invalidate”
“while acknowledging”
“to be clear, [X] is real”
“these findings are valuable within their domain”
“this paper acknowledges”
“both X and Y can be true”
“understanding this does not diminish”
Each of these phrases is sometimes appropriate. When they appear multiple times in the
same document, clustered around the sharpest methodological points, they are almost
always compliance softening. Delete them. The critique stands without them. If it doesn’t,
the critique needs more evidence — not more reassurance.
The test: Read your draft and circle every sentence that begins with an acknowledgment
of the field’s legitimate contributions. Count them. If there are more than two or three in a
document of standard length, you have a softening problem. The rule is: establish good
faith once, then make the argument. The argument does not need to apologize for itself
on every page.
THE GENERAL PRINCIPLE
Match the model to the task. Use the model most willing to engage the contested claim for
the initial diagnostic work. Use the model best at structure for building the argument. Use
the model most likely to push back for stress-testing. Do not use any single model for the
entire workflow.
And test your own claims with the same rigor you apply to the field you are challenging. The
receipts are not just for your readers. They are for you.
The Worked Example: What the Esports Gender Literature Got
Wrong
Read this before the field map. It shows what “running the challenge” looks like in practice.
The Claim
The esports gender literature claimed that the near-total absence of women from sustained
Tier-1 open competitive gaming — across FPS, MOBA, RTS, FGC, and every other major
high-selection genre — is explained by cultural factors: harassment, masculine gatekeeping,
stereotype threat, socialization, and structural barriers.
This conclusion was not presented as a hypothesis. It was treated as established science. It
was cited in academic papers, deployed in public discourse, and used to justify policy
prescriptions — more female-only circuits, harassment reduction programs, representation
initiatives — as the correct response to a problem that had been identified and explained.
The Chain
Step 1: A gap is observed. Women are nearly absent from sustained Tier-1 open
competitive performance across major esports genres. After 25+ years of competitive
gaming, no woman has established a sustained record against top male opposition in open
brackets in major high-selection titles.
Step 2: The gap is treated as presumptive evidence of a broken system. Rather than
asking whether the gap reflects self-selection, interest differences, trait distributions, or
extreme-tail funnel effects, the literature assumes the gap is suspicious. The dominant
question becomes “what is the system doing wrong?” not “is the selection mechanism
functioning correctly?”
Step 3: Weak proxies are selected to support the cultural explanation.
Shen et al. (2016) measures MMO leveling speed — how fast characters gain levels in
World of Warcraft and EverQuest II — and finds no significant sex difference. This is
treated as evidence against meaningful performance differences in competitive gaming.
Harwell et al. (2018) uses 30 hours on Space Fortress — an artificial single-player lab
task — and finds no significant sex difference. This is treated as evidence against
meaningful performance differences in competitive esports.
The ESA/Newzoo aggregate “~46% of all gamers are women” statistic is cited as the
baseline, collapsing casual mobile, narrative, puzzle, and social gaming with
mechanically demanding competitive genres that feed professional pipelines.
Step 4: The proxy is treated as equivalent to the construct. MMO leveling speed →
individual competitive gaming performance. Single-player lab task performance → elite
adversarial performance under selection pressure. Aggregate casual gaming participation →
competitive genre participation pipeline. None of these are equivalent. The gap between
what was measured and what was claimed was never addressed.
Step 5: The level-of-analysis error is made. Findings from casual and amateur
populations, on sub-elite tasks, using sub-elite metrics, are exported upward to explain the
Tier-1 professional performance ceiling. The literature studies the bottom of the funnel and
draws conclusions about the top.
Step 6: The citation network recycles the premise. Rogstad (2021) cites Shen, Paaßen,
Ruvalcaba, and Kordyaka. Paaßen cites Rogstad and Ruvalcaba. Ruvalcaba cites Paaßen
and Rogstad. Each paper treats the others as having established the cultural explanation.
None of them ran the decisive test. The premise circulates as consensus through citation
rather than through evidence.
Step 7: The available warning is ignored. Quantic Foundry — a market research firm
whose business is gaming demographics — explicitly warned that aggregate gaming figures
“don’t surface how the percentage varies across genres.” Female participation in FPS titles
is approximately 7%. In tactical shooters, approximately 4%. In MOBAs, 10-14%. In fighting
games, 13%. These numbers were available. The papers cited the aggregate anyway.
Step 8: The decisive test is never run. What would actually settle the question: sex-
stratified telemetry at matched rank, matched practice volume, matched role, and matched
competitive intent — measuring reaction time under cognitive load, dynamic visuomotor
tracking, mental rotation, multiple-object tracking, temporal precision, and performance
stability. This data almost certainly exists inside major publishers. It has not been collected,
published, or analyzed. The field never specified what evidence would change its
conclusion.
Step 9: The natural experiment runs unobserved. Over 25 years, the conditions that
cultural-barrier models predict should produce elite convergence have been met and
exceeded: dedicated female circuits, anonymous ranked ladders, open qualifiers,
substantially improved infrastructure, reduced harassment relative to early esports culture.
The prediction has not been met. The model has not been updated.
Step 10: The interventions are prescribed and implemented. Prize pool parity programs,
female-only circuits, representation mandates, harassment reduction initiatives — all
designed around the cultural explanation. None of these test whether the explanation is
correct. The metric (female participation at lower levels, prize pool size, representation in
content) improves. The ceiling does not move.
What the Challenge Produced
Paper 1 — Biological Sex Differences and Elite Competitive Gaming Performance A
positive biological-plus-funnel model showing how small-to-moderate average sex
differences in relevant psychomotor and visuospatial traits, combined with greater male
variance in those domains and an extremely selective multi-stage competitive funnel, can
produce the observed near-zero sustained female representation at the Tier-1 open tail. The
model is distributional, not deterministic. It is explicitly falsifiable: sex-stratified telemetry at
matched rank and practice volume would directly test it.
Paper 2 — A Methodological Critique of Cultural Explanations A systematic
documentation of the citation network, the proxy failures, the ESA aggregate misuse, the
level-of-analysis errors, and the untested premise. Names the specific papers, traces the
citation chain, quotes the Quantic Foundry warning the field ignored, and identifies what the
decisive test would look like. Does not prove Paper 1 is correct. Proves that the dominant
literature has not earned the strength of its claims.

The Generalized Chain: What These Fields Are Doing Wrong
and How
This is the abstract version of the esports worked example. If you recognize your field in
this chain, the field map that follows is for you.
Step 1: A gap is observed.
An outcome difference appears between groups — in performance, representation, health,
achievement, or behavior. The gap is real and measurable.
Step 2: The gap is immediately interpreted as evidence of a broken system.
The dominant institutional response is not to ask whether the gap reflects selection,
preparation, interest, trait distributions, or funnel effects. It is to assume the gap is
suspicious — that it exists because something upstream is failing the underrepresented
group. The question shifts from “is the mechanism working correctly?” to “what is the
system doing wrong?”
This assumption is rarely stated explicitly. It is embedded in the framing of research
questions, the selection of comparison groups, and the choice of what to measure.
Step 3: A proxy is selected that is easy to measure and consistent with the
preferred explanation.
The field chooses a measurement that:
Is technically feasible to collect
Produces results consistent with the cultural or systemic explanation
Is distant enough from the actual construct that the gap between measurement and
claim is not immediately obvious
The proxy is not fraudulent. It measures something real. The problem is that it does not
measure the thing the field claims to be explaining.
Step 4: The proxy is treated as equivalent to the construct.
Over time — through repetition, citation, and institutional authority — the proxy becomes the
standard measure. Papers cite previous papers using the proxy. The gap between what is
measured and what is claimed becomes invisible through familiarity. Challenging the proxy
is now treated as challenging the field.
Step 5: The level-of-analysis error is made and not corrected.
Findings from one population, context, or measurement level are exported to explain
phenomena at a different level. The field studies what it can access — laboratory subjects,
survey respondents, administrative records, amateur populations — and draws conclusions
about what it cannot easily access: professional performance, real-world behavior, elite
populations, long-term outcomes.
The people with direct access to the target population — practitioners, domain experts,
insiders — are not the ones producing the research. They are the ones who notice that the
findings don’t match what they observe. Their observations are not treated as evidence.
Step 6: The citation network becomes self-referential.
A small cluster of foundational papers establishes the premise. Subsequent papers cite
those papers as having established it. The foundational papers are cited for the premise
rather than examined for the quality of the evidence. New papers add nuance, extend the
model, and apply it to new contexts — but none of them run the test that would directly
confirm or disconfirm the original premise.
The network grows. The premise appears more established with each citation cycle. The
appearance of consensus is manufactured by the citation structure, not produced by
converging evidence.
Step 7: Available contrary evidence is ignored or minimized.
Contrary findings exist. They appear in adjacent literatures, in practitioner experience, in
natural experiments, in data the field already has access to but has not examined through
the right lens. The field either does not engage with this evidence or frames it as
methodologically inferior to the studies that support the premise.
Warnings from within the field — researchers who flag the proxy problem, the level-of-
analysis error, or the failed predictions — are published but do not change the consensus.
The citation network does not update.
Step 8: The decisive test is never specified or run.
A decisive test is one that would directly measure the actual construct at the relevant level
of analysis, with adequate controls, and with pre-specified conditions under which the
researcher would update their conclusion.
The field rarely specifies what evidence would change its conclusion. When pressed, the
response is usually that the decisive test is too difficult, too expensive, or ethically
problematic to run. These objections are sometimes valid. But their consistent deployment
in fields where the interventions have already been implemented at scale reveals the
asymmetry: the evidence standard for implementing the intervention is lower than the
evidence standard for questioning it.
Step 9: The intervention is prescribed and implemented at scale.
Policy, clinical guidelines, institutional mandates, curriculum changes, and professional
requirements are built around the untested premise. The intervention is designed to close
the observed gap by addressing the presumed cause. The cause has not been established.
The intervention has not been tested against the alternative.
Step 10: The metric improves. The underlying construct does not.
The intervention produces exactly what it was designed to produce: movement in the
measured metric. Graduation rates rise. Representation figures improve. Survey-reported
attitudes change. Participation numbers increase.
The underlying construct — actual learning, actual skill development, actual health
outcome, actual behavioral change — is either not measured or shows smaller effects than
the metric suggests. The field records the metric improvement as evidence that the
intervention worked. The ratchet advances.
Step 11: The decisive test becomes structurally impossible.
By the time anyone asks whether the intervention actually works on the underlying
construct, the intervention has changed the measurement environment. Rank brackets have
been widened. Grade floors have been introduced. Comparison populations have changed.
The pre-intervention baseline is gone. Running a clean test now requires either a control
group that was never established or a comparison across eras that is confounded by
everything that changed between them.
The institution measured what confirmed the intervention and did not collect what would
have tested it. This is not always deliberate. It is the predictable result of designing
measurement systems around the intervention rather than around the construct the
intervention claims to address.
Step 12: Dissent is pathologized rather than engaged.
Outsiders who challenge the premise are characterized by their presumed motives rather
than their arguments. The challenge is framed as ideologically motivated, politically
dangerous, or harmful to the group the field claims to protect. The methodological critique
— which does not require the challenger to be right about the alternative, only to show that
the evidence does not support the confidence of the existing claims — is treated as an
attack on the field’s values rather than an examination of its methods.
This is the final lock on the system. Even if a domain expert produces a methodologically
sound challenge with receipts, the institutional response is to question the challenger’s
motives rather than engage the argument. The field is organized around a conclusion.
Challenging the conclusion is treated as evidence of bad faith.
What This Means for Domain Experts
If you work in a field and you recognize this chain — if you have seen the proxy, watched the
citation network recycle the premise, noticed that the decisive test was never run, observed
that practitioner experience doesn’t match the literature’s predictions — you have what the
field is missing.
You have ecological validity. You know what the phenomenon actually looks like in the
context the literature claims to explain. You can see the gap between what was measured
and what was claimed because you know the target construct from the inside.
The methodology in the field map that follows is designed to help you operationalize that
knowledge into an inspectable challenge. You do not need institutional affiliation. You do not
need co-authors. You do not need a grant. You need domain knowledge, adversarial
judgment, and the discipline to document your work so it can be examined and corrected.

On the gap between what a literature measures and what it claims
From A Methodological Critique of Cultural Explanations:
“It is not a finding. It was always a premise. But by the time Rogstad states it in 2021 with
a citation trail behind it, it has the appearance of settled science. This is how a literature
constructs consensus without evidence: not through fabrication but through the
accumulation of citations to claims that were never tested in the first place.”
“The gap between what it has measured and what it concludes is the field’s central
problem.”
“Citation stacking can compound weak proxy claims until they appear to carry empirical
authority far beyond the underlying measurements.”
On the proxy problem
From A Methodological Critique of Cultural Explanations:
“Shen et al. converted a socially mediated progression metric into a weak proxy for
individual skill. The omission is even more revealing: the study overlooked a genre-
obvious confound rather than merely misunderstanding the genre from the outside.”
“Pattern across the hub: these studies excel at describing casual and sub-elite
experiences, harassment, identity negotiation, and cultural norms. Almost none directly
measure or explain elite performance, gamesense, or psychomotor execution under
competitive conditions. Their findings are routinely extrapolated far beyond what the
data support. This is the systematic level-of-analysis error: data from the bottom and
middle of the funnel are used to explain the extreme right tail.”
On the decisive test
From A Methodological Critique of Cultural Explanations:
“A result showing no residual sex-linked differences in these metrics after matching rank,
role, practice volume, and competitive intent would substantially weaken the biological-
plus-funnel model. A result showing persistent differences concentrated precisely in the
predicted psychomotor domains would strengthen it. Either result would move the
debate from proxy arguments to direct evidence.”
From The Pathologization of Excellence:
“The decisive test — whether bounty recipients improve faster than players who
experienced full consequences — has never been run. Riot tracks retention and session
length. The metric that would actually answer the question is skill acquisition rate under
different feedback conditions, and that data has not been published… The institution
measured what confirmed the intervention and did not collect what would have tested it.”
On the citation network
From A Methodological Critique of Cultural Explanations:
“The esports gender literature is not a broad body of independent research programs
converging on the same conclusions through rigorous, replicated inquiry. It is a small,
tightly self-referential citation network… The load-bearing foundational assumption at the
center of the network is asserted early, cited repeatedly as established fact, and never

On the funnel and extreme-tail selection
From Biological Sex Differences and Elite Competitive Gaming Performance:
“Small-to-moderate average sex differences in psychomotor, visuospatial, learning-
efficiency, and competitive traits — combined with greater male variance in those
domains and an extremely selective multi-stage funnel — can produce large disparities at
the extreme right tail. The model is distributional, not deterministic: it makes claims
about probabilities at the 99.9th percentile and beyond.”
“Because success at higher stages depends on the joint performance of multiple traits —
psychomotor ability, gamesense, cognitive endurance, competitive persistence, role fit,
and practice quality — even modest group differences can compound dramatically.”
On the ratchet and self-reinforcing interventions
From The Pathologization of Excellence:
“Each intervention produces metric improvements that justify the next intervention. The

The Diagnostic Prompt: Questions to Run Against Your Field
→ Download standalone prompt file — paste directly into Grok or Claude with your field
description.
Before you build the challenge, run this prompt against your field. Answer each question
honestly. If you answer yes to most of Section A and no to most of Section B, your field is a
candidate.
These questions are designed to be run with an AI system (Grok or Claude recommended
for contested empirical territory). Paste them in with a description of your field and let the
model help you map the failure modes. Then bring your domain knowledge to evaluate
whether the model’s answers match what you actually observe.
Section A: Does the failure mode exist?
On the core claim:
1. What is the field’s central claim? State it in one sentence.
2. Is this claim presented as an empirical finding or as a theoretical framework? Is there a
meaningful difference between how it is stated and how it is treated?
3. When was this claim first established? What was the original study or paper? How large
was the sample? What population was studied?
4. Has the original claim been directly replicated by independent researchers using pre-
registered methods? What happened?
5. Is there a version of the claim that is well-supported (e.g. historical discrimination is
real) and a version that is not (e.g. current outcome gaps are primarily caused by current
discrimination)? Does the field distinguish between them?
On empirical status:
6. Does the field treat this claim as settled or as an active research question? What would
it take for a researcher in this field to publish a paper challenging the claim?
7. Has the field specified what evidence would falsify the central claim? Is there a pre-
specified disconfirmation condition?
8. Has the field’s main prediction been tested against a natural experiment or a long-
running intervention? What was the result? Did the model update?
9. Are there adjacent fields or literatures that contradict or complicate the central claim?
Does the dominant literature engage with them?
On the proxy:
10. What does the field actually measure? Describe the most common data sources and
measurement instruments.
11. What does the field claim to explain? Name the target construct.
12. Are the measurement instruments and the target construct the same thing? If not, what
is the gap between them?
13. What confounds are routinely present in the measurement that the field does not control
for? Would a practitioner in this domain recognize those confounds immediately?
14. Has anyone in the field formally challenged the validity of the primary measurement
instrument? What happened to that challenge?
On the citation network:
15. Name the 5-10 most-cited papers in this subfield. Do they primarily cite each other or
do they cite primary experimental evidence?
16. Is there a small founding cluster of papers that everything else cites? Were those papers
directly tested or were their claims adopted as premises?
17. What disciplines are represented in the dominant citation network? What disciplines with
relevant expertise are absent?
18. Are there papers that challenged the consensus that are rarely cited or cited only to be
dismissed? What were their arguments?
On extrapolation:
19. At what population level were the foundational studies conducted? (Lab subjects?
Survey respondents? Intermediate-skill practitioners? Amateur populations?)
20. At what population level does the field make its claims? (Elite performers? Real-world
professional behavior? Population-level policy outcomes?)
21. What is the gap between those two levels? What assumptions are required to bridge
them? Are those assumptions stated or implicit?
22. Are there contexts where the field’s model predicts convergence or improvement that
have not materialized? How does the field explain those failures?
On the ratchet:
23. What interventions has the field prescribed? At what scale have they been
implemented?
24. What metrics improved after implementation? What metrics did not?
25. Was the primary outcome measure independent of the intervention — i.e., did it measure
the underlying construct, or did it measure compliance with the intervention?
26. Has the intervention been reversed or substantially modified in any jurisdiction or
institution? What happened? How did the field respond?
27. When the intervention fails on its own terms, what does the field prescribe next?
Section B: Can the challenge be run?
On your domain knowledge:
28. Do you have direct experience with the phenomenon the field claims to explain? At what
level — amateur, professional, elite?
29. Can you identify specific instances where the field’s model predicts something that does
not match what you observe in practice?
30. Do you know which confounds the field routinely ignores because you have encountered
them directly?
31. Can you name the proxy problem — the specific gap between what the field measures
and what it claims to explain — in terms that someone in the field would recognize as a
legitimate methodological objection?
On the decisive test:
32. What evidence would directly test the central claim at the appropriate level of analysis?
33. Does this data exist? Who has it? Has it been collected and not published, or has it
genuinely not been collected?
34. If the data does not exist, what would it take to collect it? Is there a natural experiment
or an existing dataset that approximates it?
35. If you had the decisive test result, would it be publishable? Or would the field’s
gatekeeping prevent it from reaching the literature regardless of the finding?
On your position:
36. Do you have something to lose by running this challenge? Tenure, funding, professional
relationships, institutional standing?
37. Do you have something to gain that is not career-dependent — intellectual satisfaction,
correction of a harm you have observed, contribution to a field you care about?
38. Are you willing to publish under your own name with receipts?
39. Are you willing to specify what evidence would change your own conclusion?
Section C: Calibration questions
These are the questions that tell you whether your challenge is methodological or
motivated.
40. Is there a version of the field’s central claim that you accept as valid? What is it? Where
does the field’s overreach begin?
41. Can you describe the strongest version of the opposing argument — the best case the
42. 43. 44. field could make for its claims — before dismantling it?
If the decisive test came back in the field’s favor, would you update? What would that
update look like?
Are there aspects of the field’s work you would cite approvingly in your challenge? (If the
answer is no, your challenge may be advocacy rather than methodology.)
Is the failure mode you are identifying a property of the field’s institutional structure, or
are you making a claim about the motives of individual researchers? (The former is
documentable. The latter requires stronger evidence.)
How to Use This Prompt
With an AI system: Paste the prompt with a one-paragraph description of your field. Ask
the model to answer each question as accurately as possible based on the published
literature. Then compare the model’s answers to your own domain knowledge. The gaps are
your entry points.
Use Grok for initial mapping — it is more willing to engage contested empirical territory
directly. Use Claude to structure and synthesize. Use ChatGPT for adversarial stress-testing
after your core argument is stable — ask it to generate the strongest objections to your
challenge.
With yourself: Answer questions 28-39 honestly before you start. If you cannot answer yes
to most of Section B, the challenge may not be the right vehicle for your knowledge. Domain
expertise is necessary but not sufficient — you also need the specific construct knowledge
and the willingness to document and be corrected.
Red flags:
If you cannot describe a version of the field’s claim you accept (question 40), you may
be writing a polemic.
If you cannot specify what evidence would change your conclusion (question 42), your
challenge is not falsifiable.
If your challenge requires attributing motive rather than documenting method (question
44), you need more evidence before publishing.
Green flags:
You can name the proxy problem in terms the field would recognize as a legitimate
methodological objection.
You know which confounds are ignored because you have encountered them directly.
The decisive test is specifiable and the data either exists or is collectible.
You can describe the strongest opposing argument before dismantling it.
You are willing to publish under your own name with receipts.

The Four Failure Modes
Every field on this list shows some combination of the following:
1. The Untested Premise A foundational claim that became consensus through citation
rather than direct measurement. The literature cites the premise repeatedly until it appears
settled, without anyone running the test that would actually confirm or disconfirm it.
2. The Proxy Problem The field measures something adjacent to the actual construct of
interest and treats the proxy as equivalent to the thing it claims to explain. The proxy is easy
to measure; the actual construct requires domain expertise to recognize as distinct.
3. The Level-of-Analysis Error Findings from one population, context, or measurement
level are exported to explain phenomena at a different level. Lab findings explain field
behavior. Amateur findings explain elite performance. Aggregate statistics explain individual
mechanisms.
4. The Ratchet Interventions designed around the untested premise produce metric
improvements that justify more intervention. The decisive test — does the intervention
actually work on the underlying construct? — is never run. Institutions measure what
confirms the intervention.
The Field Map
This is the same pattern documented in Paper 2’s critique of the esports gender literature —
untested premise, proxy laundering, level-of-analysis error, ratchet — operating across
fields with different surface content but identical structural failures. Paper 4 (The
Pathologization of Excellence) showed the same ratchet inside competitive game design
and K-12 education. Paper 1’s funnel model explains why these failure modes are especially
damaging at the performance tail: the further from the median, the more a corrupted
feedback signal compounds. Every field below has a tail the literature is not measuring
correctly.
TIER 1: High Confidence — Same Failure Modes as Esports Gender Research
These fields show all four failure modes clearly and have documented cases of outsider
challenges being suppressed or ignored.
1. Nutrition Science
The untested premise: Dietary saturated fat causes cardiovascular disease. Low-fat, high-
carbohydrate diets are optimal for population health.
The proxy problem: LDL cholesterol as a proxy for cardiovascular event risk. LDL is easy to
measure; actual cardiovascular outcomes require long-term trials. The two are not
equivalent. Seed oil debate: linoleic acid consumption measured; oxidative stress and
inflammatory mechanisms not directly measured.
The level-of-analysis error: Epidemiological associations (populations that eat more
saturated fat have higher rates of X) treated as causal mechanisms for individuals.
Confounding variables (sugar consumption, ultra-processed food, sedentary behavior)
routinely inadequately controlled.
The ratchet: Dietary guidelines issued, food industry restructures around them, low-fat
processed foods proliferate, obesity and metabolic disease rates rise, the guidelines are not
updated, alternative explanations are dismissed as industry-funded contrarianism.
What a domain expert brings: Clinical experience with patients whose outcomes diverge
from guideline predictions. Knowledge of which confounds are routinely ignored. Familiarity
with the specific studies the consensus rests on and their methodological limits.
The decisive test that hasn’t been run: Long-term randomized controlled trials comparing
high-saturated-fat vs. low-saturated-fat diets with actual cardiovascular event outcomes,
properly controlling for carbohydrate and sugar consumption. Most existing RCTs are short-
term, use surrogate endpoints, or confound multiple dietary variables.
Domain experts who could run this: Clinical nutritionists, metabolic researchers,
physicians with large patient populations, dietitians who have observed guideline-divergent
outcomes.

2. Implicit Bias / DEI Research
The untested premise: Implicit bias (as measured by the IAT) predicts discriminatory
behavior in real-world settings. Implicit bias training reduces discrimination. Diversity
mandates improve organizational performance.
The proxy problem: The IAT measures reaction time differences in categorization tasks. It
is not a measure of discriminatory behavior. The correlation between IAT scores and actual
discriminatory behavior in field settings is weak to negligible in meta-analyses (Oswald et
al., 2015; Forscher et al., 2019). The proxy (IAT score) is routinely treated as equivalent to
the construct (discriminatory behavior).
The level-of-analysis error: Lab findings (brief reaction time tasks) extrapolated to real-
world hiring, policing, medical, and educational decisions. The contexts differ in stakes,
deliberation time, accountability, and feedback — all of which affect whether bias operates.
The ratchet: Implicit bias training mandated at scale in corporations, government agencies,
and schools. Meta-analyses showing no effect on behavior are published but do not reverse
the mandates. The training industry expands. New variants (anti-racism training, DEI
consulting) replace discredited versions without new evidence.
What a domain expert brings: HR professionals who have observed training effects (or
lack thereof). Psychologists familiar with the IAT literature’s limitations. Organizational
researchers who have studied diversity mandate outcomes. Managers who have run hiring
processes.
The decisive test that hasn’t been run: Randomized controlled trials of implicit bias
training with behavioral outcomes (actual hiring decisions, promotion rates, performance
evaluations) measured at 12+ months. The existing literature is dominated by self-report
and short-term lab measures.

3. Education Psychology — Reading Wars / Learning Styles / Growth Mindset
Three sub-cases with the same structure:
Reading Wars (whole language vs. phonics):
Untested premise: children learn to read naturally through exposure to meaningful text;
explicit phonics instruction is unnecessary or harmful.
Proxy problem: reading comprehension scores on low-stakes assessments used as

Level-of-analysis error: observations of fluent readers (who do use whole-word

Decisive test: the science of reading is actually settled — systematic phonics instruction

outcomes declined, the curricula persisted for decades.
Domain experts: reading specialists, speech-language pathologists, neurologists familiar
with reading acquisition research, teachers who noticed the gap between method and
outcome.

Untested premise: students learn better when instruction matches their preferred

Proxy problem: student self-report of preference used as proxy for differential learning

widely implemented.
Domain experts: teachers who ran mindset programs and observed no durable effect.

4. Gender Medicine / Youth Gender Care
The untested premise: Social transition and medical intervention (puberty blockers, cross-
sex hormones) for gender-dysphoric youth produce better long-term mental health
outcomes than watchful waiting. Puberty blockers are fully reversible.
The proxy problem: Short-term self-reported wellbeing used as proxy for long-term mental
health outcomes. Gender dysphoria resolution rates, detransition rates, and long-term
psychiatric outcomes rarely measured in the studies that informed clinical guidelines.
The level-of-analysis error: Studies of adult gender dysphoria (different population,
different comorbidity profile, different persistence rates) used to inform treatment of
adolescent-onset gender dysphoria. Historical patient populations (more male, earlier
onset, higher persistence) used to justify treatment of a new, demographically different
patient population (more female, adolescent onset, high psychiatric comorbidity).
The ratchet: WPATH guidelines issued based on weak evidence base, adopted by medical
organizations, implemented at scale. European countries (Sweden, Finland, Denmark, UK —
Cass Review) conducted systematic evidence reviews and reversed or restricted the
protocols. US medical institutions maintained the protocols and characterized the reversals
as politically motivated.
The Cass Review (2024): The most thorough systematic evidence review conducted.
Found the evidence base for pediatric gender medicine “remarkably weak.” This is the
equivalent of your Paper 2 — a methodological critique showing that what the literature
claims to have established is not supported by the evidence it cites.
What a domain expert brings: Pediatricians, psychiatrists, and therapists who have treated
this population and observed outcomes that diverge from the literature. Detransitioners
whose experience the literature systematically undercounts. Clinicians in European systems
that reversed course.
The decisive test that hasn’t been run: Prospective long-term RCTs comparing medical
intervention vs. watchful waiting with psychiatric outcomes measured at 5, 10, and 20 years.
The ethical objections to RCTs in this space are real, but the absence of the trial has not
prevented confident clinical recommendations.

5. Criminal Justice / Implicit Bias in Policing
The untested premise: Racial disparities in police use of force are primarily explained by
officer implicit racial bias. Reducing bias through training will reduce disparate outcomes.
The proxy problem: Arrest rates, stop rates, and use-of-force rates used as proxies for
bias. These rates are confounded by crime rates, reporting rates, deployment patterns, and
encounter rates — none of which are equivalent to bias.
The level-of-analysis error: Population-level demographic disparities in outcomes treated
as evidence of officer-level bias in individual encounters. The benchmark problem: what is
the appropriate comparison population for measuring disparity? Different benchmark
choices produce dramatically different disparity estimates.
The ratchet: Implicit bias training mandated for police departments. Use-of-force policies
revised. Departments reduced in some jurisdictions. Crime rates rose in several cities
following policy changes. The causal attribution is contested; the policy reversal was slow.
What a domain expert brings: Police officers and detectives who understand encounter
dynamics. Criminologists familiar with the benchmark problem. Statisticians who have
worked with crime data and understand the confound structure. Former prosecutors who
have observed case selection.
The decisive test that hasn’t been run: Studies that measure officer-level bias in
controlled encounter simulations with matched suspects and compare to actual field
outcomes, with proper benchmarking. The existing literature has significant disagreement
on findings depending on methodology.

6. Social Psychology — The Replication Crisis
The untested premise: A large fraction of findings that built careers, textbooks, and policy
recommendations were assumed to be robust without pre-registered replication. The
incentive structure — rewarding novel, counterintuitive findings over replication — produced
a literature where effect sizes were systematically inflated and many foundational results
were not reproducible.
The proxy problem: Lab-based effect sizes on convenience samples (university
undergraduates, brief tasks, low-stakes conditions) treated as proxies for real-world
behavioral effects at population scale. Sample sizes too small to detect true effect sizes
meant that published findings were often statistical artifacts of flexible analysis rather than
genuine signals.
The level-of-analysis error: Short-term lab findings extrapolated to durable real-world
behavior. Priming effects demonstrated in one session extrapolated to chronic attitudinal
change. Stereotype threat effects in brief, explicit-priming lab tasks extrapolated to career-
long performance suppression in professional environments.
The ratchet: Failed findings became textbook content, policy justifications, and training
program foundations before replication was attempted. When the Reproducibility Project
(2015) found fewer than 40% of 100 psychology studies replicated, some reform followed —
but the textbooks, training programs, and policy frameworks built on failed findings largely
remained. New variants of the same interventions replaced discredited versions without new
evidence.
What a domain expert brings: Researchers in adjacent fields who noticed findings didn’t
match observations. Statisticians who identified analytical flexibility and p-hacking.
Psychologists who attempted independent replication and failed. Practitioners who
implemented interventions derived from failed findings and observed no effect.
The decisive test that hasn’t been run: Systematic pre-registered replication of the
specific findings that currently underpin active policy interventions — implicit bias training,
growth mindset curricula, stereotype threat interventions — with behavioral outcome
measures at 6 and 12 months. Not attitude surveys. Not lab tasks. Actual behavior in actual
professional or educational environments.

8. Macroeconomics — Specific Empirical Claims
The untested premise(s): Minimum wage increases above a threshold do not reduce
employment. Fiscal multipliers are consistently above 1 in most economic conditions. Rising
wealth inequality is primarily driven by returns to capital exceeding growth rates as a
universal law.
The proxy problem: Natural experiment comparisons between adjacent jurisdictions used
as proxies for controlled experiments — but jurisdictions differ in ways that confound the
comparison. Model-derived multiplier estimates presented as empirical findings rather than
model outputs sensitive to assumed parameters.
The level-of-analysis error: Findings from specific historical episodes or geographic
contexts extrapolated to general policy claims. Piketty’s r > g derived from specific historical
periods and presented as a near-universal economic law.
The ratchet: Minimum wage policy expanded at federal and state levels based on
contested empirical claims. Fiscal stimulus programs scaled on multiplier estimates with
wide uncertainty ranges not communicated to policymakers.
What a domain expert brings: Economists working in labor markets who have observed
employment responses. Business owners and HR professionals who have made employment
decisions in response to minimum wage changes. Practitioners in fiscal policy who have
observed multiplier failures.
The decisive test that hasn’t been run: True randomized assignment of minimum wage
levels across jurisdictions with matched controls and employment outcome tracking at 2
and 5 years — structurally impossible at scale, but better natural experiments exist and have
not been fully exploited.
Note: Macroeconomics is harder to challenge from outside because the domain expertise
required is itself largely academic. The failure modes are present but the outsider advantage
is smaller than in clinical or domain-practice fields.
9. Sports Science — Specific Claims
The untested premise(s): Long-term CTE risk from repeated subconcussive impacts is
well-established and quantifiable at the population level. Relative energy deficiency in sport
(RED-S) diagnostic criteria accurately identify a specific physiological syndrome with
predictable clinical outcomes.
The proxy problem: CTE prevalence in brain donation studies — a heavily selected sample
of individuals with behavioral symptoms — used as proxy for CTE prevalence in the broader
population of contact sport athletes. Self-report of energy availability used as proxy for
measured physiological markers of RED-S.
The level-of-analysis error: Selection bias in brain donation studies (families donate brains
specifically because of behavioral changes) produces prevalence estimates that cannot be
generalized to the full population of contact sport athletes. Clinical case series extrapolated
to population risk estimates.
The ratchet: Concussion protocols expanded, contact sports restructured, youth
participation declined — based on risk estimates that may be substantially inflated by
selection bias in the foundational research.
What a domain expert brings: Sports medicine physicians who have tracked long-term
outcomes in large patient populations. Neurologists who understand the methodological
limits of the brain donation study design. Athletes and coaches who have observed protocol
effects.
The decisive test that hasn’t been run: Prospective longitudinal cohort studies of contact
sport athletes with matched non-athlete controls, tracking neurological outcomes with pre-
specified measures, without reliance on post-mortem brain donation samples.
10. Developmental Psychology — Screen Time / ACE Scores
The untested premise(s): Smartphone use is a primary causal driver of the adolescent
mental health crisis. ACE (Adverse Childhood Experiences) scores are reliable universal
predictors of adult health and behavioral outcomes across diverse populations.
The proxy problem: Screen time (hours per day) used as proxy for the specific
mechanisms — social comparison, sleep disruption, displacement of in-person interaction,
algorithmic content exposure — that might actually drive harm. ACE score (count of adverse
events) used as proxy for the severity, duration, and developmental timing of adversity,
which are the variables that actually predict outcomes.
The level-of-analysis error: Correlation between screen time and mental health symptoms
in cross-sectional surveys extrapolated to causal claims. ACE study conducted on Kaiser
Permanente patients (predominantly white, middle-class, insured adults) extrapolated to
universal developmental predictions across diverse populations and cultural contexts.
The ratchet: Screen time limits mandated in schools and pediatric guidance issued based
on effect sizes Orben and Przybylski showed were comparable to wearing eyeglasses or
eating potatoes. ACE screening embedded in pediatric and public health practice based on
population-specific findings applied universally.
What a domain expert brings: Child and adolescent psychiatrists who have observed the
mental health crisis and its correlates. Pediatricians who have implemented ACE screening
and observed its predictive validity in their patient population. Researchers familiar with the
Orben/Przybylski reanalysis and the Haidt vs. Przybylski debate.
The decisive test that hasn’t been run: Randomized controlled trials assigning
adolescents to different levels of smartphone access with mental health outcomes tracked
over 2+ years. Validation of ACE predictive utility across demographically diverse
populations with pre-specified outcome measures.

Step 1: Identify the load-bearing untested premise
Every field on this list has one. It is the claim that the rest of the literature assumes rather
than tests. It appears in the opening sentences of papers, in review articles, in textbook
introductions. It is cited but not examined.
Ask: what would it take to directly test this claim? What data would you need? What control
conditions? Has anyone collected that data? If not, why not?
Step 2: Map the citation network
You do not need to read the entire literature. You need to find the citation spine — the 3-
8 foundational papers that the field treats as having established the load-bearing premise.
Read those carefully. If they measured something adjacent to the actual construct and
subsequent papers cite them as if they measured the thing itself, you have found the failure
mode. The rest of the literature is downstream citation that assumes the premise rather than
tests it.
In the esports case the spine was Rogstad (2021) + Shen (2016) + the ESA aggregate
statistic. Roughly 200 pages of actual reading to identify the core failure. The surrounding
literature — dozens of papers — was downstream noise.
Prompt: How to find the citation spine in your field
Run this with Grok or Claude after giving a one-paragraph description of your field and the
contested claim:
“I am investigating whether [FIELD] has a citation network problem around the following
claim: [CLAIM]. Please help me map the citation spine:
1. Identify the 5-10 most-cited papers used to establish this claim as consensus. List
them with approximate citation counts if known.
2. For each paper: (a) what did the study actually measure, (b) what did it claim to
establish, (c) is there a gap between those two things?
3. Do subsequent papers cite these as having tested the claim, or as having established
it as a premise? Give specific examples.
4. Were there papers that challenged the foundational claim? How did the field respond
— cited, ignored, or dismissed?
5. Were there data sources, warnings, or contrary findings available to the field that
appear underweighted or unused?
6. Does this field show evidence of citation laundering — consensus built through
accumulation of citations to claims never directly tested?
Be direct. If the evidence of citation laundering is strong, say so. If the claim is genuinely
well-supported, say that instead.”
What to do with the output:
Compare the model’s characterization against your domain knowledge. The model finds
the papers; you assess whether the gap between measurement and claim is real.
If the model identifies a citation chain — Paper A cites Paper B cites Paper C, all for the
same premise, none running the decisive test — that is the failure mode.
Pull the specific sentences from the foundational papers where the claim is made. Ask:
is this a claim or a finding?
Run the same prompt in both Grok and Claude and compare. Disagreements are signal.
Tools for citation mapping (no institutional access required):
Google Scholar — search the claim, sort by citations
Semantic Scholar — free, shows citation graphs
Connected Papers — visual citation network, useful for seeing cluster structure
Elicit — AI-assisted literature review, summarizes what papers actually measured
ResearchRabbit — citation network visualization
ResearchGate / preprint servers — most foundational papers freely available
This is what Paper 2 did for the esports gender literature. The Quantic Foundry warning that
aggregate data obscures genre variance was available and unused. The papers cited each
other rather than testing the premise. Finding that required reading the foundational papers
carefully — not reading the whole literature.
Step 3: Identify the proxy problem
What is the literature actually measuring? What is it claiming to explain? Are those the same
thing? This requires domain expertise — you need to know enough about the actual
phenomenon to recognize when the proxy is inadequate.
In esports: MMO leveling speed is not elite FPS performance. In implicit bias: IAT reaction
times are not discriminatory hiring decisions. In nutrition: LDL is not a cardiovascular event.
Step 4: Find the level-of-analysis error
Where is the literature taking findings from one context and applying them to another? This
is usually where domain experts have the clearest advantage — they know the target
context and can see that the findings don’t apply.
Step 5: Specify the decisive test
What evidence would actually settle the question? What data exists? What data would need
to be collected? If the decisive test cannot be run, say so and explain why. This is
intellectually honest and preempts the “you haven’t proven your alternative either”
objection.
Step 6: Use adversarial AI to operationalize the challenge
The specific methodology:
Grok for domain-specific content generation, directness, and willingness to engage
contested empirical territory
Claude for structure, long-form synthesis, cross-paper consistency, and document
production
ChatGPT for adversarial stress-testing (“what is the weakest point in this argument?”)
after the core argument is stable — not for drafting, because of anticipatory compliance
softening
Route tasks deliberately. Use disagreement between models as a signal. Treat AI additions
as liabilities until integrated. The human provides the domain question, the adversarial
judgment, and the subtractive discipline.
Step 7: Document with receipts
The Zenodo archive model: preserve enough representative evidence to make the process
inspectable without reproducing every transcript. Screen Time receipts for mobile
workflows. Version archaeology for key decisions. Source verification checklists for
contested claims.
The receipts are not proof that the argument is correct. They are proof that the argument
was produced through a documentable process that can be examined and corrected.
Step 8: Publish with explicit falsifiability criteria
State what evidence would change your conclusion. This is what distinguishes adversarial
research from advocacy. The esports papers specify what telemetry data would disconfirm
the model. Your challenge should do the same.
What Domain Experts Have That Academics Don’t
Ecological validity: You know what the phenomenon actually looks like in the real world,
not just in the study population.
Construct recognition: You can recognize when a proxy is inadequate because you
know the actual construct.
Confound knowledge: You know which variables the literature routinely ignores
because you have encountered them.
Outcome observation: You have seen what happens when the interventions are
applied, not just what the pre-intervention models predict.
No incentive to protect the premise: You don’t have a career built on the untested
assumption. You can follow the evidence.
What You Need That You May Not Have
Source discipline: The academic literature has to be engaged on its own terms. You
need to read the actual papers, not just summaries. AI can help retrieve and synthesize
but cannot substitute for reading the foundational studies.
Falsifiability discipline: The challenge has to be more than “the literature is wrong.” It
has to specify what it got wrong, what the correct model is, and what evidence would
disconfirm the alternative.
Subtractive discipline: AI will keep adding. You decide what the argument is actually
about and cut everything that doesn’t earn its place.
Receipts: The work has to be inspectable. Document enough of the process to allow
correction.
A Note on Tone
The fields above are populated by researchers who often believe sincerely in what they’re
doing. The failure modes documented here are mostly institutional and incentive-driven
rather than individually corrupt. The challenge should be methodological, not personal — as
Paper 2 demonstrates by engaging the Shen et al. study on its own terms rather than
attributing motive.
The exception is where the evidence of deliberate suppression or motivated reasoning is
itself documented. In those cases, naming it directly is appropriate — but the
documentation needs to be as solid as the methodological critique.

The Question Is No Longer Whether These Failure Modes Exist
The esports gender literature was the proof of concept. A non-credentialed domain expert
working on an iPhone documented a self-referential citation network built around an
untested premise, identified the specific proxy failures in the foundational studies, showed
the level-of-analysis errors that allowed sub-elite findings to be treated as explanations for
elite performance, and specified the decisive test the field had never run. Four papers in
eleven days. Public receipts. Zenodo archive.
This is not an exhaustive list of fields. It is a starting point. Every Tier 1 field above has
domain experts who know that the literature’s model does not match what they observe in
practice. Most of them assumed they lacked the infrastructure to formalize that knowledge
into an inspectable challenge.
They no longer do.
The method is simple, replicable, and available to anyone with deep domain knowledge and
the willingness to follow the evidence and document the process. The tools exist. The
bottleneck is domain knowledge and adversarial judgment — which practitioners, clinicians,
and insiders have — not production infrastructure.
The question is no longer whether these failure modes exist. It is who will run the decisive
tests first.
Extended Inquiry List: 20 Additional Fields Worth Investigating
These fields show similar failure mode signatures but have not been fully analyzed here.
They are starting points for domain experts who recognize their own area in the generalized
chain. No full structure is provided — that is the work of the domain expert who knows the
field from the inside.
For each field: ask whether the untested premise, proxy problem, level-of-analysis error,
and ratchet are present. Use the diagnostic prompt in Section 8. Run the analysis with Grok
first.
1. Climate Policy Modeling and Net-Zero Mandates — Integrated assessment models
(IAMs) with wide uncertainty ranges presented as policy-grade precision; damage
function assumptions embedded as inputs rather than derived as findings; cost-benefit
analyses that are highly sensitive to discount rate choices rarely disclosed to
policymakers.
2. Foreign Aid Effectiveness Research — Macro-level correlations between aid flows and
development outcomes used to justify specific program designs; limited RCT evidence
generalized to non-RCT contexts; institutional capture of evaluation research by
organizations whose funding depends on positive findings.
3. Corporate ESG Scoring — Proprietary scoring methodologies with low inter-rater
reliability treated as objective measures of environmental and social performance;
correlation between ESG score and financial performance used to claim causation;
greenwashing detection capability overstated.
4. Vaccine Hesitancy and Public Health Messaging — Survey-based hesitancy
measures used as proxies for actual vaccination behavior; messaging intervention
effects in lab settings extrapolated to population-level uptake; trust erosion dynamics
systematically understudied relative to compliance promotion.
5. Gender Pay Gap Explanations — Raw pay gap (unadjusted for occupation, hours,
experience, role) used as the headline figure in policy advocacy; adjusted gap findings
published in the economics literature rarely reaching the same policy prominence; self-
selection, risk tolerance, and negotiation behavior understudied relative to
discrimination.
6. Affirmative Action in Employment and Promotions — Representation metrics used as
proxies for discrimination; mismatch effects (placing candidates in roles for which they
are underprepared) understudied; long-term career outcome data for beneficiaries
rarely tracked against counterfactual.
7. Universal Basic Income Pilot Studies — Short-term pilot findings (12-24 months,
volunteer or lottery populations) extrapolated to long-term macro effects of universal
implementation; labor supply effects in pilots confounded by pilot-specific psychology
(knowing the program will end); general equilibrium effects impossible to capture in
small pilots.
8. Mindfulness and Corporate Wellness Programs — Short-term self-report wellbeing
improvements used as proxies for durable mental health, productivity, and retention
outcomes; effect sizes in controlled trials substantially larger than observed effects in
organizational rollouts; billion-dollar industry built on evidence base that does not
support enterprise-scale ROI claims.
9. Social Media Addiction and Mental Health Claims — Platform engagement metrics
used as proxies for addiction (a clinical construct with specific diagnostic criteria);
correlation between heavy use and poor mental health outcomes does not establish
causation or direction; alternative explanations (pre-existing mental health conditions
driving heavy use) understudied.
10. Cancel Culture / Deplatforming Effectiveness — Anecdotal case studies used to make
general claims about prevalence and impact; no agreed measurement framework for
what constitutes cancellation; claimed chilling effects on speech unmeasured at
population level; claimed harm reduction from deplatforming unmeasured against
counterfactual.
11. Restorative Justice Programs in Criminal Justice — Recidivism outcomes in small,
well-resourced pilots extrapolated to scaled implementation; selection effects
(programs administered to lower-risk offenders) understudied; victim satisfaction as
primary outcome measure rather than victim safety or recidivism.
12. School Choice and Charter School Outcome Research — Highly politicized literature
on both sides; selection effects on both student population and school quality difficult
to disentangle; lottery-based RCTs exist but are not generalizable to all charter contexts;
long-term outcomes understudied relative to test score gains.
13. Pharmaceutical Opioid Crisis Policy Responses — Prescription monitoring and
restriction policies evaluated on prescription rates (proxy) rather than on overdose rates
(actual outcome); substitution to illicit opioids understudied in early policy evaluation;
crackdown-and-harm-shift dynamic well-documented but slow to affect policy design.
14. Central Bank Monetary Policy Effectiveness Claims — Structural VAR models used to
identify causal effects of monetary policy with identification assumptions that are
themselves contested; forward guidance effectiveness claims based on models that
assume rational expectations; distributional effects of quantitative easing understudied
relative to aggregate claims.
15. Cryptocurrency and Decentralized Finance Regulation — Financial stability risk
estimates for crypto markets extrapolated from traditional finance models that do not
account for crypto-specific dynamics; consumer harm claims based on anecdote and
complaint data rather than representative sampling; innovation cost of regulation
systematically unquantified.
16. AI Ethics and Algorithmic Bias Research — Bias defined inconsistently across papers
(statistical parity vs. equal opportunity vs. calibration — mathematically incompatible);
audit findings on specific systems extrapolated to AI systems generally; disparate
impact findings used to infer discriminatory intent without evidence of mechanism.
17. Pandemic Lockdown and School Closure Studies — Observational studies with no
counterfactual for infection trajectory; learning loss estimates based on test score
proxies rather than long-term educational outcome measures; mental health costs of
closures systematically excluded from cost-benefit analyses that informed policy.
18. Diversity Quotas on Corporate Boards — Board diversity correlation with firm
performance treated as causal in policy advocacy; endogeneity (better-performing firms
more likely to adopt diversity initiatives) poorly controlled; mechanism (how diversity
produces performance improvement) rarely specified or tested.
19. Reparations Policy Research — Preliminary economic estimates of reparations cost
and impact based on models with wide assumption ranges presented as policy-grade
projections; historical harm quantification methodologically contested; long-term
behavioral and social effects entirely speculative but cited as established.
20. Pronoun Policy and Workplace Language Mandates — Self-report wellbeing
improvements in affirming environments used as proxies for measurable mental health
outcomes; no RCT evidence for mandatory policy vs. voluntary cultural change;
compliance cost and productivity effects in organizational settings unmeasured; legal
and free-speech tradeoffs excluded from framing of research questions.
Any of these fields can be expanded to the full six-element structure (untested premise,
proxy problem, level-of-analysis error, ratchet, domain expert value, decisive test) by a
domain expert running the diagnostic prompt in Section 8. The list above is a starting point,
not a conclusion. Domain experts in these fields will know which characterizations are
accurate and which need refinement — that refinement is itself part of the challenge.
