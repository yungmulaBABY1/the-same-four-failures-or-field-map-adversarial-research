## Abstract

This paper audits a three-document citation pipeline that has functioned as load-bearing evidence in legal briefs, clinical guidelines, and legislative testimony regarding gender-affirming care. The pipeline runs from an advocacy organization’s fact sheet (Human Rights Campaign, “Get the Facts on Gender-Affirming Care”), through a direction-of-finding literature review (Cornell University, What We Know Project, 2018), to an aggregator of institutional endorsements (transequality.org / A4TE, “Medical Organization Statements”). Each document in the chain cites the others as independent corroboration. None constitutes a quality-weighted systematic review. The Cornell document — the most methodologically substantial of the three — applies a coding scheme that grades studies by direction of finding rather than evidence quality, produces a 93% positive rate from heterogeneous uncontrolled clinical samples, and exports a “robust international consensus” summary that downstream documents and policy actors treat as equivalent to a GRADE-quality evidence synthesis. It is not. The narrowest defensible claim the evidence actually supports is: most patients in clinical follow-up samples who completed gender transition treatment and remained in contact with clinical services reported positive outcomes on self-report measures. That is a real finding. It is not “robust international consensus” adequate to settle contested policy questions about population-level guidelines, access for minors, or medical necessity determinations. This paper describes the pipeline mechanically and names the failure mode directly: this is citation laundering operationalized as health policy. The post-2017 evidence base — the Cass Review, NICE systematic reviews, and Scandinavian policy reassessments — demonstrates both what the evidence actually shows when quality-weighted methodology is applied and that the pipeline continued operating after contrary quality-weighted reassessments were available. That continuation is the ratchet signature.

## 1. Introduction

When a policy claim rests on science, the question is not whether studies exist. Studies almost always exist. The question is what tier of evidence the studies occupy, whether the most rigorous available evidence supports the claim, and whether the claim’s deployment preserves or discards the evidence’s stated limitations.

The literature supporting gender-affirming care in policy contexts has developed a specific citation architecture. Three documents recur as foundational references across legal briefs, state legislative testimony, medical organization position statements, and public advocacy:

1. Human Rights Campaign Foundation, “Get the Facts on Gender-Affirming Care” (hrc.org, updated January 2025)
1. Cornell University What We Know Project, “What does the scholarly research say about the effect of gender transition on transgender well-being?” (2018, no subsequent update)
1. transequality.org / A4TE, “Medical Organization Statements”

These documents are presented as independent sources converging on the same conclusion. They are not independent. Each cites or links to the others. The Cornell document provides the appearance of academic authority for the HRC document’s factual claims; the HRC document cites Cornell as its evidentiary anchor; the medical organization statements page is used to establish that the conclusion carries institutional medical endorsement; and all three documents appear in the same citation clusters across policy-facing materials.

One instance of the pipeline in deployment: a February 2026 formal regulatory comment to HHS by the Robert Wood Johnson Foundation — submitted in opposition to a proposed rule restricting Medicaid coverage of gender-affirming care for minors — cites the Cornell document directly in footnote 7, reproducing the “93% of 55 studies” figure as counter-evidence to an HHS review questioning the evidence base. The Cornell document is cited without qualification or methodological caveat. This is the pipeline mechanism operating as described: Cornell’s directional vote count deployed as quality-equivalent rebuttal to a federal agency’s evidence reassessment.

Before proceeding: this paper states explicitly what it does not claim. It does not assert that gender transition causes harm. It does not deny that the clinical follow-up literature contains real positive findings — it does, for the narrower claim those studies can support. It does not attribute bad faith to individual researchers. The methodological critique is structural: a direction-of-finding coding scheme does not produce quality-weighted evidence synthesis regardless of intentions, and institutional endorsements built on the same citation base are not independent corroboration. What this paper claims is that the specific evidentiary architecture deployed to establish “robust scientific consensus” in policy contexts is not what it presents itself as, and that the gap between what the evidence shows and what the pipeline claims is the paper.

The paper’s primary evidentiary anchors are NHS-commissioned systematic reviews, peer-reviewed citation analyses in indexed journals, and published journal corrections — not advocacy-adjacent sources. Where critics of the pipeline are cited (Mason, Abbruzzese et al., Singal), they appear in supporting roles and are identified as such; none of the paper’s load-bearing methodological claims depend on them.

## 3. Document One: HRC “Get the Facts on Gender-Affirming Care”

The Human Rights Campaign is an advocacy organization. “Get the Facts” is advocacy output. This is not itself a methodological criticism — advocacy organizations produce advocacy documents — but it is a categorization that matters when the document is cited in clinical or legal contexts as independent scientific summary.

The document’s evidentiary structure is as follows. Claims about safety and efficacy are supported by hyperlinks to individual studies selected to return positive findings, with no acknowledgment of contrary evidence or methodological limitations. The puberty blocker “fully reversible” claim is stated as settled fact; the contested evidence on bone mineral density, brain development windows, and fertility outcomes — acknowledged in the clinical literature even by researchers sympathetic to gender-affirming care — does not appear. A claimed suicidality reduction exceeding 70% cites papers with small samples, no control groups, and self-report outcomes. The claim that “every major medical organization” supports gender-affirming care is used as a substitute for evidentiary argument: institutional consensus is presented as independent corroboration of the evidence base rather than as a downstream product of the same citation architecture. These are examples of caveats that disappear at the first node of the pipeline.

The Tordoff et al. (2022) study, published in *JAMA Network Open* and cited in the HRC document as positive evidence for youth hormone therapy, illustrates the problem at the individual study level. The study received a formal correction from JAMA Network Open in July 2022 for data errors in supplementary tables (DOI: 10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2022.29031). More substantively, analysis of the study’s own reported data showed that the proportion of youth meeting threshold for moderate-to-severe depression was 57% at baseline and 56% at 12-month follow-up in the puberty blocker/hormone group — no statistically significant improvement. The study’s abstract framing of improvement has been disputed on these grounds (Singal, 2022). The broader foundational literature has also been subject to peer-reviewed methodological critique: Abbruzzese, Levine & Mason (2023) identified three structural biases in the Dutch studies that originated the clinical protocol — selection of only the highest-functioning cases, faulty measurement of gender dysphoria resolution, and inability to separate effects of simultaneous psychotherapy, hormones, and surgery. None of these critiques appear in the HRC document.

The decisive-test question — what would a pre-registered, prospective, controlled study measuring objective long-term outcomes across the full population of treatment-seekers show? — is not raised.

**Verdict: Fatal construct validity failure for its policy use.** The document is advocacy material that deploys the appearance of evidence. Its failure is category error when cited as science.

## 4. Document Two: Cornell “What We Know” (2018)

The Cornell document is more sophisticated and requires more careful treatment. It presents as a systematic literature review: it specifies a search window (1991–2017), identifies 55 studies meeting inclusion criteria, and reports a summary finding. It carries institutional credibility. It is the academic anchor of the pipeline. The page remains in its original 2018 form with no subsequent update.

The methodology, examined carefully, does not support that anchor.

### 4.1 Why 93% Is Not a Meta-Analytic Finding

The 93% positive rate is not a pooled effect size, not a weighted estimate, not a GRADE assessment, not a risk-of-bias-adjusted synthesis, and not a durability finding. It is a directional vote count across heterogeneous studies. A direction-of-finding consensus is not a meta-analytic finding, and the Cornell document’s summary language — “robust international consensus” — does not accurately describe what the methodology produced.

The distinction matters because the five-part failure between direction and valid policy evidence runs as follows:

**Direction ≠ quality.** The coding scheme grades studies by direction of finding rather than evidence quality. A retrospective satisfaction survey of 28 patients with no control group and high loss-to-follow-up is coded identically to a prospective cohort study, because both return a positive directional finding. No formal risk-of-bias tool is applied — no Cochrane RoB-2, no Newcastle-Ottawa scale, no GRADE instrument. Studies are sorted into bins (positive / mixed or null) without any structured scoring of selection bias, measurement bias, confounding, or attrition.

A common response to this critique is that the ethical difficulty of conducting randomized controlled trials in this population — withholding treatment from those who seek it — makes high-tier evidence unavailable through constraint rather than negligence. That constraint is real and is acknowledged by the Cass Review and others. But the ethical difficulty of producing rigorous evidence does not license presenting the resulting low-certainty evidence as robust consensus adequate for population-level policy. The appropriate response to an evidence base constrained by ethical limits is epistemic humility about what can be claimed — not confidence inflation in the summary language.

**Direction ≠ clinical significance.** Even when a study reports a positive direction, the relevant question is whether the effect is clinically meaningful, durable, and attributable to the intervention. A small short-term improvement in self-reported satisfaction does not establish the same thing as large durable improvement across objective and subjective health outcomes. Cornell’s directional count treats them equivalently.

**Direction ≠ durability.** The coding scheme has no category for mixed trajectories — short-term improvement followed by long-term plateau or decline. A study showing early gains that do not persist at 5-year follow-up counts as positive, because the direction of the short-term finding is positive. The long-term trajectory is invisible in the count.

**Direction ≠ attribution.** Many studies cannot isolate what produced any observed improvement: social transition, psychotherapy, hormones, surgery, family support, clinical attention, legal recognition, comorbidity treatment, or selection into care. Cornell’s directional count treats “transition” as a clean intervention, but the underlying studies often measure bundled processes where no individual component can be credited or blamed.

**Direction ≠ population-level policy evidence.** Direction-of-finding in self-selected clinical follow-up samples, with acknowledged loss-to-follow-up and no control conditions, cannot support population-level claims about what happens to the full range of people who seek treatment — including those who are denied, those who detransition, and those who disengage from care.

### 4.2 Additional Methodological Omissions

Beyond the direction-versus-quality problem, the Cornell document has further structural omissions that compound the weakness of the “robust consensus” framing.

**No transparent search protocol.** Cornell specifies a date range and arrives at 55 studies but provides no reproducible search protocol: no database list, no search terms, no formal inclusion/exclusion criteria, no grey-literature handling. The 55-study set cannot be independently replicated or audited for selection bias in inclusion decisions.

**Outcome non-commensurability.** The 55 studies use substantially different endpoints: satisfaction, dysphoria scales, quality of life, suicidality, psychiatric functioning, body image, sexual function, relationship outcomes, and surgical outcomes. These are not interchangeable. Cornell treats them as equivalent “positive findings,” aggregating across fundamentally different constructs into a single directional count.

**No effect-size synthesis.** Cornell never asks how large the effects are, whether they exceed minimum clinically important differences, or what the confidence intervals look like. A tiny non-significant improvement is coded the same as a large robust improvement. The absence of magnitude assessment means the 93% figure says nothing about whether the positive findings are clinically meaningful.

**No sensitivity analyses.** Cornell never tests what happens if the weakest studies are removed — those with greater than 40% attrition, no control group, or sample sizes below 30. A single sensitivity analysis stratifying by study quality could substantially alter the apparent consensus. Its absence means the robustness of the 93% figure is untested.

**No handling of detransition or adverse trajectories.** The coding scheme has no category for adverse events, detransition, discontinuation, or mixed outcomes where one domain improves and another worsens. This is a structural blind spot: the framework cannot register harms unless a study explicitly concludes that “transition causes overall harm” — an unusually stringent and asymmetric standard.

**Asymmetric evidentiary standards for benefit and harm.** Benefits are counted when self-report outcomes move in a positive direction. Harms are discounted unless a study concludes transition causes “overall harm.” That asymmetry matters: harms can appear in bone density, fertility, sexual function, surgical complications, persistent below-population quality of life, psychiatric morbidity, or adverse long-term trajectories without any individual study concluding “overall harm.” The binary coding scheme structurally suppresses adverse signal.

**Temporal obsolescence.** Cornell is locked to 1991–2017 and has never been updated. Cass, NICE, SBU, and PALKO all reassessed overlapping and youth-relevant portions of this evidence base and downgraded it to “very low certainty.” Cornell cannot incorporate these downgrades, yet continues to be cited as if it reflects the current state of evidence.

**Summary-body mismatch.** Cornell’s opening summary claims “robust international consensus,” while its own Finding 7 admits: “It is difficult to conduct prospective studies or randomized control trials.” This contradiction is a framing-level error: the headline contradicts the methodology. Downstream actors cite the headline; the caveat does not travel.

### 4.3 The Lindqvist Case: Direction-of-Finding Inflation in Practice

Cornell places Lindqvist et al. (2017) in its mixed/null category — correctly, on its own terms. But Lindqvist illustrates the broader inflation problem across the 51 “positive” studies regardless of which bin it occupies.

Lindqvist et al. is a prospective cohort study of 190 transgender women undergoing surgery at Karolinska University Hospital — one of the more methodologically substantial studies in the literature. Its findings: transgender women scored lower than the general population on most quality-of-life domains on the SF-36 throughout the follow-up period; gains did not persist at 5 years; and the long-term QoL trajectory was consistent with — not better than — the general population’s age-related decline. The study’s own conclusion was that “transgender women generally have a lower QoL compared to the general population.”

Cornell coded it as mixed/null, which is accurate. But the broader pattern it illustrates is this: if a study of this methodological quality and scope — prospective, 190 patients, 5-year follow-up — is coded as mixed/null while dozens of smaller, shorter, less rigorous studies with positive short-term self-report findings are coded as positive, the 93% rate is a product of the field’s publication and study-design landscape, not a measure of convergent evidence quality. A substantial number of the Cornell-positive studies have sample sizes below 30, follow-up periods under 12 months, or no comparator condition of any kind. They count identically to Lindqvist in the directional tally. That is not a consensus; it is an artifact of the coding scheme.

### 4.4 The Dhejne Omission

Dhejne et al. (2011) — a population-based matched cohort study of all 324 sex-reassigned persons in Sweden from 1973 to 2003, with population controls matched 10:1, and 30 years of follow-up — does not appear in Cornell’s 55-study set at all. The study found that sex-reassigned persons had considerably higher risks for mortality (aHR 2.8), suicide attempts (aHR 4.9), and psychiatric inpatient care (aHR 2.8) than controls of the same birth sex. Its own authors concluded that “sex reassignment, although alleviating gender dysphoria, may not suffice as treatment for transsexualism” and called for improved psychiatric and somatic care after surgery.

Dhejne (2011) falls within Cornell’s 1991–2017 window. One possible explanation for its exclusion is that Cornell’s inclusion criterion — studies that “assess the effect of gender transition on transgender well-being” — may have been applied narrowly to exclude studies whose primary framing was mortality, psychiatric morbidity, and criminality rather than well-being as an explicit endpoint. That exclusion rationale is not stated in the Cornell document. More importantly, it does not hold as a methodological defense: psychiatric morbidity and long-term mortality are directly constitutive of well-being. A review claiming to assess the well-being effects of transition that excludes the most methodologically rigorous long-term controlled outcome data on psychiatric morbidity and survival — on grounds that those outcomes are not “well-being” — is making a scope decision that substantially affects the apparent consensus. The absence is unexplained and consequential regardless of whether it was intentional.

### 4.5 Youth vs. Adult: The Level-of-Analysis Error

The pre-2017 literature in Cornell’s set is predominantly adult or mixed-age. The policy deployments that have most heavily relied on the “robust consensus” framing — restrictions on minors, pediatric clinical guidelines, age-specific access policy — concern a population the underlying literature does not primarily study. The narrowest defensible claim from the Cornell set is about adults in clinical follow-up samples.

Two distinct arguments apply here and should not be conflated. First, the Cass Review, NICE systematic reviews, SBU, and PALKO apply specifically to the youth evidence base — their “very low certainty” findings are directly about the pediatric literature, the population where the policy stakes are highest. Second, the adult clinical follow-up evidence, while not subject to the same pediatric-specific quality reviews, remains subject to the Cornell-level methodological critique independently of Cass: the direction-of-finding coding, the loss-to-follow-up problem, the publication bias, the outcome heterogeneity, and the absence of the decisive test apply to the adult literature as well. The export of the adult evidence into youth policy without flagging the level-of-analysis error compounds both problems — it misapplies adult findings to a younger population, and it carries the adult literature’s own methodological limitations unremarked into the policy arena.

### 4.6 Hub Function

The Cornell document functions in the citation chain not as one study among many but as the premise-laundering hub. Its “robust international consensus” summary language — stronger than the underlying evidence warrants — is what gets cited downstream. The individual studies and their limitations are not independently inspected by policy actors who cite Cornell. The hub is the mechanism by which 55 heterogeneous, mostly uncontrolled, retrospective studies become “settled science.”

**Construct validity verdict: C/D border.** Useful for a narrower claim; invalid for the strong claim.
**Internal validity verdict: D — Suspect.** Direction-of-finding count from uncontrolled studies is not quality-weighted evidence synthesis.
**Citation network verdict: Premise-laundering hub.**

## 5. Document Three: The Medical Organization Statements Aggregator

The transequality.org / A4TE medical organization statements page aggregates position statements from the AMA, AAP, APA, WPATH, and other professional bodies, presenting this as a third independent evidence source. It is not independent.

Professional association statements are interpretations of the literature, not independent evidence. When multiple organizations rely on the same low-certainty studies or the same summary document, their agreement does not multiply the evidence. It multiplies the citation count.

The AAP’s 2018 policy statement (Rafferty, 2018) — the most cited and consequential — rests on a short narrative review of the literature. A 2025 peer-reviewed analysis in *Archives of Sexual Behavior* (McDeavitt et al., PMC12011888) examined that statement’s citations and found that cited references have been documented as misrepresenting the studies they cite — with Cantor (2020) finding that the policy statement’s references “repeatedly said the very opposite of what AAP attributed to them.” The same 2025 paper notes: “The AAP has not acknowledged (as of this Letter’s revision in January 2025) any of the systematic evidence reviews” — meaning Cass, NICE, SBU, and PALKO.

The AAP’s own internal processes document the evidentiary challenge. Resolution 27 (2022), submitted by five AAP members led by Dr. Julia Mason, called for “a rigorous systematic review of evidence and policy update for management of pediatric gender dysphoria.” The AAP changed procedural rules that year; Resolution 27 was the only resolution affected and did not advance (*Medscape Medical News*, November 2022). The AAP Board reaffirmed the 2018 statement in August 2023 while simultaneously authorizing a systematic review — a sequencing that Dr. Gordon Guyatt, credited as a developer of evidence-based medicine, described to the *New York Times* as “very clearly putting the cart before the horse,” adding that based on previous systematic reviews the AAP’s forthcoming review would “most likely find low-quality evidence for pediatric gender care.”

The AMA’s statements cite AAP. The aggregator presents all of them as independent institutional convergence. But if the foundational studies are low-certainty and the hub document overstates what they show, then the organizational statements built on that hub are amplifications of the same weakness, not independent corroboration. That non-acknowledgment of Cass, NICE, SBU, and PALKO — by the organization whose 2018 statement anchors the institutional consensus claim, in a peer-reviewed assessment dated January 2025 — is itself evidence of the ratchet operating at institutional scale.

**Verdict: Citation-laundered premise.** Institutional endorsements built on the same evidence base are not independent corroboration and should not be categorized as such.

## 6. The Pipeline as a Structural Failure Mode

The three documents fail as a system, and the system’s failure is more significant than any individual component’s weakness.

The pipeline structure: advocacy document cites academic hub → academic hub cites clinical follow-up literature → institutional statements cite academic hub → advocacy document cites institutional statements as independent corroboration → policy actors cite all three as converging evidence streams.

Each step adds apparent authority while discarding the previous step’s stated limitations. The formal failure modes present across the pipeline are:

**Proxy substitution.** Self-reported satisfaction in filtered clinical samples substituted for demonstrated population-level well-being outcomes.

**Level-of-analysis error.** Findings from adult clinical populations exported to youth policy; findings from treatment-completers exported to access policy affecting all who might seek treatment.

**Publication bias unaddressed.** Absence of contrary findings treated as positive evidence of safety rather than as a feature of the selection pressures that shaped the literature.

**Loss-to-follow-up ignored.** The systematic absence of dissatisfied, harmed, and detransitioned patients from clinical follow-up samples is not incorporated into the evidence summary or its downstream citations.

**Hub laundering.** Cornell converts heterogeneous uncontrolled studies into “robust consensus” through a coding scheme that does not weight evidence quality, magnitude, durability, or attribution; the hub summary travels without the underlying studies’ caveats.

**Institutional laundering.** Medical organization statements, produced by citing the same literature, are cited as independent corroboration of it.

**Ratchet behavior.** The pipeline continued operating and expanding after contrary quality-weighted reassessments were available.

This is citation laundering operationalized as health policy. The mechanism is not that researchers made errors. The mechanism is that a claim — most patients in clinical follow-up samples reported positive outcomes — was passed through successive institutional nodes, each of which restated it in slightly stronger terms, until it emerged as “robust international consensus supporting medically necessary care” with the underlying evidentiary limitations entirely absent.

### 6.1 Responses to Common Objections

**“But major medical organizations endorse gender-affirming care.”** Medical organization endorsements are downstream interpretations of the same literature audited here. The AMA cites AAP; AAP cites the same clinical follow-up cluster; the aggregator presents this chain as independent convergence. Endorsement by multiple organizations that relied on the same low-certainty base does not constitute independent evidence quality. It constitutes institutional amplification.

**“The Cass Review is politically motivated or methodologically flawed.”** The Cass Review was commissioned by NHS England, led by a former president of the Royal College of Paediatrics and Child Health, applied explicit GRADE-style quality weighting that Cornell lacked, and reached conclusions consistent with independent health technology assessment bodies in Sweden and Finland — countries with very different political contexts from the UK. The methodological standard applied by Cass is the standard that should have been applied from the beginning.

**“The positive studies in Cornell’s set are still real findings.”** Many are. This paper’s claim is not that every positive finding in the pre-2017 literature is false. The claim is that a directional vote count from heterogeneous uncontrolled studies does not constitute quality-weighted consensus adequate for the policy uses to which it has been put — and that the pipeline converting it into “settled science” exhibits the documented failure modes of citation laundering.

## 7. What the Evidence Actually Shows

### 7.1 The Post-2017 Quality-Weighted Reassessments

The Cornell document covers 1991–2017. In the years following its publication, a set of higher-quality systematic evidence reviews was conducted by national health technology assessment bodies in the UK, Sweden, and Finland. These reviews applied quality-weighting criteria that the Cornell document did not. Their conclusions about evidence certainty differ substantially from the “robust consensus” framing.

**The Cass Review (2024)** was commissioned by NHS England and conducted by Dr. Hilary Cass, former president of the Royal College of Paediatrics and Child Health. Its evidence quality assessment is direct: the evidence base for puberty blockers and hormone therapy in youth is “remarkably weak,” characterized by small sample sizes, lack of control groups, high risk of bias, short follow-up periods, and outcome measures that do not capture the full range of relevant health domains. By GRADE standards, certainty of evidence is “very low” for most claimed outcomes. The Cass Review applied quality-weighting criteria to overlapping and youth-relevant portions of the same pre-2017 evidence base that Cornell coded by directional finding. The same literature, different quality filter, produced a different conclusion about what can be claimed.

**NICE systematic reviews (2020)** commissioned by NHS England found that available evidence for puberty blockers and gender-affirming hormones in young people was of “very low certainty” by GRADE criteria — too small, too methodologically limited, and too short in follow-up to support strong efficacy claims.

**Swedish Agency for Health Technology Assessment (SBU) (2022)** concluded that evidence quality for gender-affirming medical treatment in minors was insufficient, with risks including bone density loss, cardiovascular effects, and fertility uncertainty inadequately characterized. Sweden subsequently restricted such treatments for minors to research settings.

**Finnish Council for Choices in Health Care (PALKO/COHERE) (2020, updated 2023)** reached parallel conclusions and similarly restricted pediatric gender medicine to research protocols.

These reviews do not establish that gender transition causes harm. They establish that when quality-weighting criteria are applied to the evidence base, the result is “very low certainty” rather than “robust consensus.” That is the difference between an evidence base that supports population-level clinical guidelines and one that does not.

### 7.2 The Ratchet: Pipeline Operation After the Reassessments

The NICE evidence reviews were published in 2020. The SBU and Finnish assessments were available by 2022. The Cass Review’s interim report was published in 2022 and its final report in April 2024. During this period, the pipeline documents continued to be cited without qualification in the United States.

The HRC document was updated in January 2025 — nine months after the final Cass Report. It continues to describe the evidence as settled, the consensus as robust, and the safety profile as established. It does not mention the Cass Review. It does not mention the NICE evidence reviews. It does not mention the Swedish or Finnish policy reversals. The 2025 peer-reviewed *Archives of Sexual Behavior* analysis establishes the same non-acknowledgment within the AAP’s institutional apparatus.

This is the ratchet. An intervention ratchet occurs when: a field deploys an intervention on the basis of preliminary evidence; stronger contrary quality-weighted reassessments become available; the intervention and its supporting claims expand rather than pause for reassessment; and the original evidentiary framing continues to be deployed without updating.

The ratchet’s mechanism here is structural: the pipeline’s institutional nodes — advocacy fact sheets, position statements, legal citations — do not have built-in mechanisms for downgrading claims when the underlying evidence is reassessed at higher quality standards. The January 2025 HRC update, and the AAP’s non-acknowledgment of any systematic evidence review as of the same date, document the ratchet in operation at both the advocacy and institutional levels.

## 8. The Decisive Test

The decisive test for the strong policy claim — that gender transition reliably improves well-being across the range of people who seek it, in sufficient magnitude and certainty to support population-level clinical guidelines — would require:

A pre-registered, adequately powered prospective study following a representative sample of people presenting with gender dysphoria — including those who received and those who did not receive medical intervention — with a matched comparator group, measuring objective and self-reported outcomes across health domains at minimum five and ten years, with independent replication by researchers not connected to the institutional networks that produced the current literature.

That study has not been conducted. Cornell’s Finding 7 acknowledges this and characterizes it as difficult. What Finding 7 does not say — and what the pipeline as a whole does not acknowledge — is that the absence of the decisive test means the strong policy claim is not established. The Cass Review makes this point directly: clinical confidence in gender-affirming care for youth expanded beyond what the evidence warranted, in part because the field treated the difficulty of rigorous research as a reason to proceed on weaker evidence rather than as a reason to maintain epistemic humility about what that evidence can support.

-----

## 9. Conclusion

Three documents form a pipeline that has functioned as the evidentiary backbone of policy arguments about gender-affirming care. None constitutes a quality-weighted systematic review. The most methodologically substantial — the Cornell document — exports a “robust international consensus” summary from a directional vote count that does not weight evidence quality, magnitude, durability, attribution, or population representativeness. The others are an advocacy fact sheet and an aggregator of institutional endorsements that were themselves produced by citing the same literature. Citing all three as independent corroboration is citing the same evidentiary base three times.

The narrowest defensible claim the evidence supports is: most patients in clinical follow-up samples who completed gender transition treatment and remained in contact with clinical services reported positive outcomes on self-report measures. That is a real finding with real implications. It is not settled science adequate to establish medical necessity across a population, justify clinical guidelines for minors, or foreclose continued inquiry into the evidence base.

The post-2017 quality-weighted reassessments — Cass, NICE, SBU, PALKO — applied to overlapping and youth-relevant portions of the same literature and reached substantially different conclusions about evidence certainty. The pipeline continued operating after those reassessments were available. The January 2025 HRC update, and the AAP’s non-acknowledgment of any systematic evidence review as of the same date, document the ratchet in operation.

A direction-of-finding consensus is not a GRADE-quality consensus. Institutional repetition cannot convert low-certainty evidence into settled science.

The gap between what the evidence shows and how it has been deployed is the paper.
