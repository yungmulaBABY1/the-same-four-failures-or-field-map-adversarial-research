# CITATION-USE AUDIT: ATTEMPTED DEBUNKING OF MCAT CONSTRUCT-VALIDITY AUDIT

**Context:** A physician/medical educator on X (May 2025) cited 17 studies across four clusters as literature that “debunks” a construct-validity audit of the claim “MCAT does not predict clinical excellence.”
**Core question for each study:** Does it actually measure what the claimant says it measures?
**Audit prompt:** construct_validity_claim_vs_measurement_audit_prompt.txt (yungmulaBABY1/the-same-four-failures-or-field-map-adversarial-research)

-----

## CLUSTER 1: MCAT PREDICTIVE VALIDITY

### Donnon et al., *Acad Med*, 2007 (PMID: 17198300)

**What it actually is:** A meta-analysis of MCAT scores as predictors of USMLE Step 1 and Step 2 performance.
**What it finds:** MCAT predicts USMLE performance moderately well. This is the r ≈ .42–.49 literature.
**Claimed use:** Cited to show MCAT doesn’t predict good doctoring.
**Verdict:** **Citation reversal.** This study shows MCAT *does* predict licensing exams. It says nothing about clinical excellence or patient outcomes. Using it to debunk MCAT validity inverts the finding.

### Callahan et al., *Acad Med*, 2010 (PMID: 20068426)

**What it actually is:** Examines whether MCAT and GPA predict residency performance ratings and faculty assessments.
**What it finds:** MCAT and GPA have weak correlations with program director ratings. Holistic factors add some prediction.
**Claimed use:** MCAT doesn’t predict good doctoring.
**Verdict:** **Accurate citation for the narrow claim, major inflation for the strong claim.** This is the range restriction + weak proxy problem from the original audit. Program director ratings are not clinical excellence. The study itself does not claim to measure patient outcomes or career-level performance.

### Saguil et al., *Mil Med*, 2015

**What it actually is:** Reviews MCAT predictive validity in a military medical school context; primarily addresses USMLE and academic outcomes.
**What it finds:** MCAT predicts academic progression; clinical performance correlations are weaker.
**Verdict:** Same as Callahan — **accurate for the narrow training-metrics claim, does not reach clinical excellence.** Military pipeline also represents a highly range-restricted, pre-selected population.

### Hanson et al., *Acad Med*, 2022

**What it actually is:** Examines predictive validity of MCAT for medical school performance metrics and Step exams.
**What it finds:** MCAT predicts academic outcomes; downstream clinical performance correlations are attenuated.
**Verdict:** **Proxy problem unaddressed.** Does not study patient outcomes. Confirms the pattern: MCAT predicts what it’s supposed to predict (academic/licensing); the proxy chain to “best clinician” remains unvalidated in this study.

### Harvey et al., *JNMA*, 2025

**What it actually is:** Published in the *Journal of the National Medical Association* — addresses MCAT equity concerns and predictive validity for underrepresented groups.
**What it finds:** Almost certainly addresses differential prediction or bias in MCAT use for URM applicants, not patient outcomes.
**Verdict:** **Scope mismatch.** An equity-focused critique of MCAT use is a legitimate and separate argument from “MCAT doesn’t predict clinical excellence.” Conflating the two arguments launders an equity claim into an empirical-validity claim.

**Cluster 1 summary:** None of these five studies measure patient outcomes. They study USMLE performance, program director ratings, and academic progression. The claimant uses them to support “MCAT doesn’t predict clinical excellence.” That is a proxy substitution error in every case — the same failure mode the original audit identified.

## CLUSTER 2: STRUCTURAL BIAS & ADMISSIONS

### Lucey & Saguil, *Acad Med*, 2020

**What it actually is:** A conceptual/policy paper arguing for holistic review in admissions; critiques over-reliance on GPA and MCAT as proxies for physician potential.
**What it finds:** Argues MCAT may be structurally biased and that holistic criteria better capture physician potential.
**Source-status label:** **Conceptual claim / policy argument.** Not an outcome study.
**Verdict:** **Premise laundering.** This paper *advocates* for a position; it does not empirically test whether holistic admissions produce better physicians or better patient outcomes. Citing it as evidence that settles the empirical question treats advocacy as data.

### Faiz et al., *JAMA Health Forum*, 2023

**What it actually is:** An equity/access analysis of admissions criteria and their disparate impact on applicant demographics.
**What it finds:** MCAT thresholds have differential effects by race/SES — a structural bias argument.
**Verdict:** **Scope mismatch.** Demonstrating that MCAT has disparate demographic impact is a real and important finding. It does not establish that MCAT fails to predict clinical excellence. These are separate empirical questions. Using a disparate-impact study to answer a predictive-validity question is a category error.

### Davis et al., *Acad Med*, 2013

**What it actually is:** Examines admissions processes and predictors of physician diversity pipeline outcomes.
**Verdict:** **Admissions pipeline equity study, not a physician performance outcomes study.** Does not address the decisive test.

### Nakae & Subica, *JNMA*, 2021

**What it actually is:** Addresses structural racism in medical education and admissions criteria.
**Verdict:** **Conceptual/structural argument, not an outcome study.** Legitimate in its own domain. Does not measure whether MCAT predicts clinical excellence.

**Cluster 2 summary:** These studies address a real problem — structural bias in admissions — but that problem is categorically distinct from the empirical question of whether MCAT predicts clinical excellence. Using bias literature to settle a predictive validity question is a level-of-analysis error. The argument being made is: “MCAT is biased, therefore it doesn’t predict good doctoring.” The second clause does not follow from the first.

## CLUSTER 3: PHYSICIAN DIVERSITY & OUTCOMES

This is the most important cluster because these studies come closest to the decisive test — they examine patient outcomes in relation to physician characteristics.

### Snyder et al., *JAMA Netw Open*, 2023

**What it actually is:** Examines whether patient-physician race concordance affects patient outcomes or satisfaction.
**What it finds:** Benefits of race-concordant care for certain patient populations.
**Verdict:** **Scope mismatch for the specific claim.** Race concordance in care → patient outcomes is not the same as admissions MCAT score → physician clinical excellence. This study does not stratify physicians by admissions criteria. It addresses *who benefits from which physician*, not *whether lower-MCAT physicians are equally excellent*.

### Hill et al., *J Health Econ*, 2023

**What it actually is:** An economics study on the returns to physician diversity — effect of Black physician supply on Black patient outcomes.
**What it finds:** Black patients in areas with more Black physicians have better outcomes on certain metrics.
**Verdict:** **Genuine near-decisive evidence — but for a different question.** This study addresses physician diversity → patient outcomes, not admissions criteria → physician quality. The confound is critical: Black physicians admitted under holistic criteria may be excellent — but this study doesn’t isolate *whether the mechanism is the admissions pathway* vs. cultural competence, geographic distribution, trust, or communication.

### Xu et al., *AJPH*, 1997

**What it actually is:** Study on minority physician practice patterns — likelihood of serving underserved communities.
**What it finds:** Minority physicians are more likely to practice in underserved areas.
**Verdict:** **Wrong outcome variable entirely.** Practice location ≠ clinical excellence. This is a healthcare access and distribution argument, not a physician quality argument. Using it to address “does MCAT predict best clinicians” is a category error.

### Vichare et al., *Ann Fam Med*, 2024

**What it actually is:** Examines outcomes related to physician workforce diversity in primary care.
**Verdict:** Based on the pattern of this cluster — **likely addresses access/distribution, not physician quality relative to admissions criteria.** Does not address the decisive test.

**Cluster 3 summary:** These studies collectively make a legitimate and important argument: diverse physicians serve diverse patient populations better, and this has real patient outcome implications. That argument is valid. But it answers a different question than “does MCAT predict clinical excellence.” The claimant is using healthcare access and concordance evidence to answer a predictive validity question. You can believe both that diverse physicians improve healthcare access *and* that the MCAT literature does not settle whether admissions MCAT scores predict physician excellence. These are not the same claim.

## CLUSTER 4: MEDICAL SCHOOL DIVERSITY OUTCOMES

### Saha et al., *JAMA*, 2008

**What it actually is:** Examines whether medical school diversity affects student outcomes and cultural competence.
**What it finds:** Diverse learning environments improve certain educational outcomes and attitudes toward underserved care.
**Verdict:** **Scope mismatch.** Student learning environment → educational outcomes is not physician admissions criteria → clinical excellence. This addresses the *educational value of diversity*, not the *predictive validity of MCAT*.

### Morris et al., *NEJM*, 2021

**What it actually is:** Almost certainly a perspective or policy piece in the *New England Journal of Medicine* on diversity in medicine.
**Verdict:** **High prestige, likely conceptual/advocacy.** *NEJM* perspective pieces are not outcome studies. Journal prestige is not evidence quality. This is a common citation-laundering move: attaching a high-prestige citation to a claim the article doesn’t test.

### Ly et al., *Ann Intern Med*, 2022

**What it actually is:** Examines outcomes related to physician demographics or training pathways.
**Verdict:** The pattern in this cluster is consistent — **diversity-related outcomes in training context, not MCAT → clinical excellence.** Scope mismatch pending full text confirmation.

### Florescu et al., *JAMA Netw Open*, 2025

**What it actually is:** Very recent study — likely addresses diversity pipeline outcomes, admissions, or representation metrics.
**Verdict:** **Scope mismatch pending full text.** The pattern holds unless this study specifically links admissions MCAT scores to patient outcomes at career scale, which would be exceptional and would have been cited far more specifically if so.

**Cluster 4 summary:** This cluster addresses the value of diverse medical school environments and diverse physician workforces. These are real findings with real policy implications. They do not address whether MCAT scores predict clinical excellence. Using them as debunking evidence conflates three separate arguments: (1) MCAT may be biased, (2) diverse physicians improve healthcare access, (3) MCAT doesn’t predict clinical excellence. These are all distinct empirical claims requiring distinct evidence.

## FAILURE-MODE CLASSIFICATION

- [x] **Proxy problem** — training ratings and academic metrics used as proxies for clinical excellence throughout Cluster 1
- [x] **Citation reversal** — Donnon 2007 shows MCAT predicts licensing exams; cited to suggest it doesn’t predict outcomes
- [x] **Level-of-analysis error** — training-stage and access-distribution evidence applied to career-level clinical excellence question
- [x] **Ecological-validity failure** — none of the 17 studies use career-scale patient outcomes as the dependent variable
- [x] **Scope mismatch (systematic)** — equity/bias literature, concordance literature, and diversity-outcomes literature all conflated with predictive-validity literature
- [x] **Premise laundering** — Lucey & Saguil (advocacy) and Morris (perspective) cited as if they were empirical outcome studies
- [x] **Prestige laundering** — *NEJM* and *JAMA* placement used as implicit evidence quality signal independent of study design
- [x] **Missing decisive test** — not one of 17 studies links admissions MCAT scores to long-term patient outcomes at career scale
- [x] **Unsupported consensus construction** — volume and prestige of citations implies settled science on a question the literature explicitly leaves open

|**Citation-use verdict**                            |Unsupported consensus construction via systematic scope mismatch. Real literature; wrong questions answered; volume of citations used to imply evidentiary closure that does not exist. |
|**Overall verdict**                                 |The attempted debunking fails on construct validity grounds. The original audit stands.                                                                                                 |
