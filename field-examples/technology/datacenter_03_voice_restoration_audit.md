## 3. Hotspot Table

### Hotspot 1 — “Honest concession” label (Claim 3)

**Original:**

> “Honest concession: This is the strongest of the five claims. Water stress in arid regions during drought years is a real documented conflict. Local governments in water-stressed communities have legitimate concerns about allocation.”

**Problem type:** Compliance performance — the author audibly flagging their own good faith before making a methodological point. A confident author does not announce the concession; they make it. The concession itself is doing real evidentiary work. The label is not.

**Why it weakens the argument:** Interrupts the audit format’s clinical tone with a self-conscious parenthetical. Signals uncertainty about claims the evidence actually supports.

**Recommended action:** Rewrite — drop the label, keep the substance, add the denominator punch line.

**Voice-restored replacement:**

> “The water consumption figures are real. Hyperscale facilities in arid regions do create documented stress during drought years, and local governments in those communities have legitimate allocation concerns. The failure is in the jump from local stress to national catastrophe: the denominator was omitted entirely. ‘Towns go dry’ is the conclusion you reach when you report the numerator and erase the denominator.”

-----

### Hotspot 2 — Repeated “costs exist and deserve honest mitigation” (What the Posters Don’t Count)

**Original (second instance):**

> “The posters present a zero-sum worldview: data centers = pure loss for regular people. The actual dynamic is investment with real but solvable trade-offs. The costs exist and deserve honest mitigation. The benefits — jobs, tax revenue, and massive productivity gains — are being deliberately erased from the conversation.”

**Problem type:** Repeated reassurance. This line appears verbatim in the Political Version paragraph and again here. Combined with the “some communities may rationally decide” paragraph immediately following, it creates a back-to-back cluster of reassurances at the end of the section’s strongest argumentative block. The “some communities” paragraph handles the scope caveat cleanly on its own.

**Why it weakens the argument:** Second instance adds no new information. It softens the momentum before the close and signals more uncertainty than the evidence warrants.

**Recommended action:** Cut the repeated line. Retain the “some communities” paragraph as written.

**Voice-restored replacement:**

> “The posters present a zero-sum worldview: data centers = pure loss for regular people. The actual dynamic is investment with real but solvable trade-offs — and the benefit side of that ledger is being deliberately erased from the conversation.”

-----

### Hotspot 3 — Claim 1 verdict incomplete (minor)

**Original:**

> “267% is an extreme outlier stripped of context and presented as representative. The documented impact is real and in the low single digits.”

**Problem type:** Verdict lands the fact but does not connect it to the mechanism. The gap between “low single digits” and “267%” is the entire exploit — stating it explicitly completes the point.

**Why it weakens the argument:** The reader is left to make the inferential leap themselves. A direct audit verdict should close it.

**Recommended action:** Rewrite — add the mechanism clause.

**Voice-restored replacement:**

> “267% is an extreme outlier stripped of context and presented as representative. The documented impact is real and in the low single digits — that is the entire gap the infographic exploits.”

## 7. Manual Review Warnings

**1. AI force multiplier / jobs section**
The draft asserts the historical job-creation pattern confidently (“AI is following the same pattern, only faster”). This is defensible as the mainstream economic consensus but is contested specifically for AI’s displacement-to-creation ratio. If published in a venue where empirical challenge is likely, one sentence noting that AI’s displacement-to-creation ratio is still being measured could preempt the most legitimate objection — without hedging the core argument. Author judgment required.

**2. Gallup poll citation**
The 75% / 63% / 63% figures are attributed to a March 2026 Gallup poll in the text version but appear without date in the PDF. Verify that a citable source is ready — the specificity of these numbers will attract scrutiny if challenged.
