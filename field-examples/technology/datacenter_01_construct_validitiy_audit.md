# Construct-Validity Audit

## Data Center Doom Infographics — Five Claims (Circulating Social Media, 2025–2026)

**Audit framework:** Construct-Validity Audit Prompt (14 sections)
**Verdict:** F across all five claims — extreme proxy failures. Real but narrow local signals inflated into national emergencies through omitted denominators, stripped qualifiers, and fabricated magnitude.

-----

## 1. Exact Claim Extraction

**A. Strongest claim made by the source itself**
Data centers are directly causing 267% higher power bills, a current cancer and death toll, towns going dry nationally, pure hell noise up to 2 miles, and heat islands up to 16°F hotter up to 6 miles out — all as universal, ongoing, and catastrophic features of data center construction.

**B. Weaker defensible claim actually supported by the data**
In specific utility territories, localized rate pressure exists. In arid regions during drought years, water allocation conflicts are documented. At the fence line, noise is a real nuisance. Waste heat is a genuine engineering consideration near facilities. Future grid emissions projections exist.

**C. Inflated claim made by the infographics and downstream political discourse**
That data centers are an unambiguous catastrophe for residential ratepayers, public health, water supplies, acoustic environment, and local climate — universally, nationally, and currently — with no countervailing benefits.

**Are A, B, and C the same claim?**
No. They diverge at every step — from localized to national, from projected to present, from modest to catastrophic, from qualified to absolute.

**Where does inflation occur?**
At the point of omitting the denominator (water), stripping qualifiers (health projections), extrapolating one outlier as universal (power bills), ignoring acoustic physics (noise), and citing no reputable study (heat islands). In every case the real but narrow signal is the motte; the catastrophic framing is the bailey.

## 4. Level-of-Analysis Check

**Evidence collected at:** Single utility territories, single facilities, modeled projections, fence-line measurements, near-facility microclimate readings

**Claim applied at:** National policy, universal residential ratepayer impact, current public health emergency, nationwide water crisis, regional climate catastrophe

**Assumptions required to bridge the gap (none tested):**

1. A single-territory rate outlier represents all utility territories nationally
1. A 2030 emissions projection equals a current death toll
1. A facility’s daily water draw causes municipal shortages in the absence of other contributing factors
1. Fence-line decibel levels persist at that intensity across 2 miles despite known acoustic decay
1. A localized microclimate effect of 0.5–2°F within 500 meters scales to 16°F across 6 miles

## 6. Ecological Validity Check

- **Does the measurement resemble the claimed scope?** No in every case. Single-territory or single-facility measurements are presented as universal national phenomena.
- **Does the sample resemble the population the claim is applied to?** No. One outlier utility district is presented as representative of all residential ratepayers. One facility’s water draw is presented as causing national municipal shortages.
- **Does the time scale match?** No. A 2030 projection is presented as a current death toll.

**Verdict:** Not ecologically valid for any of the five strong claims. Ecologically valid only for the narrow, qualified versions: localized rate pressure exists in some territories; water stress is real in arid regions during drought; fence-line noise is a genuine nuisance; waste heat is a real engineering consideration near facilities; future grid emissions projections exist and are worth monitoring.

-----

## 7. Decisive-Test Check

**The decisive test for each claim would be:**

- **Power bills:** Compare average residential electricity rates in high-data-center-density territories versus matched territories without them, controlling for fuel mix, regulatory environment, and baseline demand growth, tracked before and after major data center additions.
- **Health burden:** Compare actual age-adjusted cancer, respiratory, and miscarriage rates in communities surrounding large data centers versus matched control communities before and after facility opening, using real epidemiological data rather than modeled electricity-demand projections.
- **Water:** Compare total annual data center water consumption as a percentage of regional freshwater draw against all other major users in the same watershed, with before/after municipal shortage rates for communities hosting new facilities, controlling for drought cycles.
- **Noise:** Deploy calibrated sound meters at graduated distances (0.1 mi, 0.5 mi, 1 mi, 2 mi) around multiple facilities during day and night operation, comparing to local background levels and published health thresholds for chronic noise exposure.
- **Heat islands:** Deploy a network of temperature sensors at multiple distances upwind and downwind of multiple large facilities across different climates and seasons, controlling for urban heat island effects, wind patterns, and seasonal variation.

**Have these tests been run?**
Not for the purposes of supporting the infographic claims. The noise and heat island figures cited do not correspond to any published study with the stated magnitudes and distances.

## 8. Citation-Use Audit

Downstream inflation sequence for each claim:

> **Power bills:** One outlier rate case → “267% higher bills” → universal data center surcharge on all residential customers

> **Health:** 2030 emissions projection → stripped of “projected,” “marginal,” “2030” → current data-center death toll + unsourced “rare cancers & miscarriages” appended

> **Water:** Facility-level daily cooling volume → “sucks millions of gallons” (accurate) → “towns go dry” (denominator omitted, conclusion fabricated)

> **Noise:** Fence-line dB measurement → “up to 2 miles” added with no acoustic modeling → “pure hell” subjective characterization appended

> **Heat:** Near-facility microclimate signal → magnitude inflated 8–32× → distance inflated to 6 miles → no reputable study cited

**Classification:** Outlier laundering (Claim 1), qualifier stripping (Claim 2), denominator omission (Claim 3), scale inflation (Claims 4 and 5). All five deploy the same underlying mechanism: report the real numerator, erase or fabricate everything else.

## 11. Verdict

**F across all five claims — fatal proxy failures.**

Each claim takes a real but narrow signal and inflates it through one or more of the following mechanisms: outlier laundering, qualifier stripping, denominator omission, scale inflation, or unsourced addition. No claim survives contact with the decisive test. The narrower versions of each claim are real and worth monitoring as legitimate engineering, policy, and siting considerations. The infographic versions are not supported by the underlying evidence in any case.

The “gives you nothing” framing that unifies all five claims is the deepest construct failure: it is not an empirical claim at all, but a framing device that requires the systematic exclusion of all countervailing evidence — jobs, tax revenue, AI productivity effects — to appear coherent. It is the same displacement accounting failure documented in other fields the toolkit has audited.

-----

## 12. Paper-Safe Wording

**A. Neutral summary:**
A set of circulating infographics makes five empirical claims about data center impacts on residential electricity rates, public health, water supplies, noise, and local temperatures. Each claim cites or implies a real underlying signal while omitting the qualifiers, denominators, and contextual comparisons required to evaluate its scope and magnitude.

**B. Direct methodological critique:**
Each infographic claim takes a localized or projected measurement — one outlier rate case, a 2030 health burden model, a facility’s daily water volume, fence-line decibel readings, near-facility temperature measurements — and presents it as evidence of a universal, current, national catastrophe. The proxy gap between the measurement and the claim is fatal in all five cases.

**C. Strong adversarial version:**
The infographics do not make empirical arguments. They identify real but narrow signals, strip every qualifier and denominator that would reveal their limited scope, and present the result as a national emergency. The “gives you nothing” framing that ties the claims together requires the systematic exclusion of the benefit side of the ledger — which is not an analytical choice but a rhetorical one.

**D. Decisive-test sentence:**
The question of whether data centers are causing the harms described would require matched before/after studies of residential rates, epidemiological surveys of proximate communities, watershed-level water accounting, calibrated acoustic surveys at graduated distances, and multi-point thermal sensor arrays — none of which the infographics cite, because none of them would produce the magnitudes claimed.
