# Key References

## IAT Construct-Validity Audit — Annotated Bibliography

References cited across all audit documents, with role in the literature and evidence-quality notes.

-----

## Foundational Papers

**Greenwald, A. G., & Banaji, M. R. (1995).** Implicit social cognition: Attitudes, self-esteem, and stereotypes. *Psychological Review, 102*(1), 4–27.
*Role: Pre-founding theoretical scaffold. Provides the implicit cognition framework the IAT was built on. Adopted as premise rather than directly tested.*

**Greenwald, A. G., McGhee, D. E., & Schwartz, J. L. K. (1998).** Measuring individual differences in implicit cognition: The implicit association test. *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 74*(6), 1464–1480.
*Role: Foundational paper. Introduces the IAT as a laboratory RT measurement instrument. Sample sizes ~32–46 undergraduates per study. The group-level RT finding is real and replicable; the individual-diagnosis claim is not supported by the data.*

**Greenwald, A. G., Nosek, B. A., & Banaji, M. R. (2003).** Understanding and using the IAT: I. An improved scoring algorithm. *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 85*(2), 197–216.
*Role: Scoring standard. Cited in nearly every IAT methods section. Methodological standardization cited as construct validation — a structural inflation.*

-----

## Validity Meta-Analyses (Author-Run)

**Greenwald, A. G., Poehlman, T. A., Uhlmann, E. L., & Banaji, M. R. (2009).** Understanding and using the IAT: III. Meta-analysis of predictive validity. *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 97*(1), 17–41.
*Role: Main validity claim source. r ≈ .27 average (inclusive coding). Author-run meta-analysis. Overestimated due to inclusive inclusion criteria. Functions as laundering hub.*

**Greenwald, A. G., Banaji, M. R., & Nosek, B. A. (2015).** Statistically small effects of the IAT can have societally large effects. *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 108*(4), 553–561.
*Role: Main rebuttal to Oswald et al. (2013). Argues small effects matter at aggregate scale. Constitutes a claim-type switch from individual diagnosis to population-level risk factor; does not rescue individual use.*

-----

## Validity Meta-Analyses (Independent)

**Oswald, F. L., Mitchell, G., Blanton, H., Jaccard, J., & Tetlock, P. E. (2013).** Predicting ethnic and racial discrimination: A meta-analysis of IAT criterion studies. *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 105*(2), 171–192.
*Role: Primary critical challenge. Independent meta-analysis with stricter coding; r ≈ .15. Published in founding cluster’s flagship journal. Rebutted by Greenwald et al. (2015); not incorporated.*

**Kurdi, B., et al. (2019).** Relationship between the IAT and intergroup behavior: A meta-analytic review. *Psychological Bulletin, 145*(6), 600–627.
*Role: Most recent large meta-analysis. r ≈ .097–.22 range; large heterogeneity. More honest about small effects than the 2009 meta-analysis but still cited as validation.*

**Forscher, P. S., Lai, C. K., Axt, J., Ebersole, C. R., Herman, M., Devine, P. G., & Nosek, B. A. (2019).** A meta-analysis of procedures to change implicit measures. *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 117*(3), 522–559.
*Role: Most important confirmatory evidence for Link 4 failure. 492 studies, 87,418 participants. Implicit measures can be changed but changes do not mediate behavioral change. Co-authored by Nosek — an internal field concession. Null mediation finding underemphasised in public-facing claims.*

-----

## Methodological Critiques

**Blanton, H., & Jaccard, J. (2006).** Arbitrary metrics in psychology. *American Psychologist, 61*(1), 27–41.
*Role: Scoring critique. “Slight / moderate / strong” cutoffs are empirically unanchored. Challenge rebutted but not answered.*

**Blanton, H., Jaccard, J., Strauts, E., Mitchell, G., & Tetlock, P. E. (2015).** Toward a meaningful metric of implicit prejudice. *Journal of Applied Psychology, 100*(5), 1468–1481.
*Role: Extended scoring critique. Specifies what would be required to anchor feedback cutoffs. Not incorporated.*

**Schimmack, U. (2021).** Invalid claims about the validity of IATs by prisoners of the implicit social-cognition paradigm. *Perspectives on Psychological Science, 16*(3), 435–442.
*Role: Strongest structural challenge. Uses SEM to show no discriminant validity evidence — IAT and self-report measures may not be measuring separate implicit and explicit constructs. Not engaged substantively by founding cluster. Most important ignored critique in the literature.*

**Meissner, F., et al. (2019).** Predicting behavior with implicit measures: Disillusioning findings, prescient challenges, and promising futures. *Frontiers in Psychology, 10*, 2483.
*Role: Systematic review. Behavioral prediction weak; incremental validity above self-report negligible. Partially acknowledged by field; reframed rather than incorporated.*

-----

## Ecological / Aggregate Validity (Strongest Supporting Evidence)

**Payne, B. K., Vuletich, H. A., & Lundberg, K. B. (2017).** The bias of crowds: How implicit bias bridges personal and systemic prejudice. *Psychological Inquiry, 28*(4), 233–248.
*Role: Most important supporting evidence — and most important theoretical concession. Relocates IAT validity to aggregate ecological level; explicitly concedes individual unreliability. The bias-of-crowds model is internally consistent with the audit’s verdict against individual diagnosis. Strongest pro-IAT theoretical framework currently available.*

**Charlesworth, T. E. S., & Banaji, M. R. (2019).** Patterns of implicit and explicit attitudes: I. Long-term change and stability from 2007 to 2016. *Psychological Science, 30*(2), 174–192.
*Role: Longitudinal national trends. IAT scores declined nationally over a decade. Supports ecological/population-level construct sensitivity.*

**Charlesworth, T. E. S., & Banaji, M. R. (2024).** The science of implicit race bias: Evidence from the Implicit Association Test. *Daedalus, 153*(1), 21–48.
*Role: Most recent ecological evidence. Regional RA-IAT scores covary with health, education, and policing disparities. Genuine construct-relevant signal at aggregate level.*

-----

## Intervention Studies

**Lai, C. K., et al. (2016).** Reducing implicit racial preferences: II. Intervention effectiveness across time. *Journal of Experimental Psychology: General, 145*(8), 1001–1016.
*Role: Pre-registered experiment testing 17 bias-reduction strategies. Most effective interventions did not persist beyond the immediate session. Training failure documented.*

**Vuletich, H. A., & Payne, B. K. (2019).** Stability and change in implicit bias. *Psychological Science, 30*(6), 854–862.
*Role: Critical reanalysis of Lai et al. (2016). Individual scores did not return to pre-intervention baselines — campus means did. Environmental stability, not individual reversion, explains why training effects dissolve. More precise framing of the training failure.*

-----

## Cognitive / Psychometric Challenges

**Rothermund, K., & Wentura, D. (2004).** Underlying processes in the IAT: Dissociating salience from associations. *Journal of Experimental Psychology: General, 133*(2), 139–165.
*Role: Salience-asymmetry account. RT differences may reflect figure-ground perceptual salience rather than stored associations. Confound at Link 1.*

-----

## Public Synthesis

**Banaji, M. R., & Greenwald, A. G. (2013).** *Blindspot: Hidden biases of good people.* Delacorte Press.
*Role: Main public amplification hub. Converts academic hedges into popular certainty. “75% of Americans have implicit racial preferences” framing traces to public reception of this book. Primary vehicle for Level 2 and Level 3 claim inflation.*

-----

## Neglected Adjacent-Field Canonical Sources

**Messick, S. (1989).** Validity. In R. L. Linn (Ed.), *Educational Measurement* (3rd ed., pp. 13–103). American Council on Education.
*Role: Defines convergent, discriminant, and consequential validity as requirements for a valid instrument. Should have been cited in founding papers. Fatal omission for individual-use claim.*

**Schmidt, F. L., & Hunter, J. E. (1998).** The validity and utility of selection methods in personnel psychology: Practical and theoretical implications of 85 years of research findings. *Psychological Bulletin, 124*(2), 262–274.
*Role: Canonical personnel selection validity paper. Defines r-value thresholds for justified use of selection instruments; IAT falls well below them. Never cited by the founding cluster. Fatal omission for workplace policy deployment.*

**Wicker, A. W. (1969).** Attitudes versus actions: The relationship of verbal and overt behavioral responses to attitude objects. *Journal of Social Issues, 25*(4), 41–78.
*Role: Established that explicit attitudes are poor behavioral predictors. Implicit measures face an even higher bar. Should have been integrated into validity claims from the start.*

**Bertrand, M., & Mullainathan, S. (2004).** Are Emily and Greg more employable than Lakisha and Jamal? A field experiment on labor market discrimination. *American Economic Review, 94*(4), 991–1013.
*Role: Canonical labor economics audit study. Provides direct behavioral measurement of hiring discrimination without any implicit bias intermediary. If discrimination is measurable directly, the IAT’s proxy role is further diminished. Never cited in IAT validity papers.*
