# Construct-Validity Audit

## Greenwald, McGhee & Schwartz (1998) — The Implicit Association Test

**Audit framework:** Construct-Validity Audit Prompt (14 sections)
**Verdict:** C/D — Useful for a narrower claim, invalid for the stronger claim. Major proxy failure for individual diagnosis and policy deployment.

-----

## 1. Exact Claim Extraction

**A. Strongest claim made by the source itself**
The IAT measures individual differences in implicit social cognition — specifically, the relative strength of automatic associations between target concepts and evaluative attributes stored in memory. The authors claim it captures something real, stable, and personal: your implicit attitude.

**B. Weaker defensible claim actually supported by the data**
Under controlled laboratory conditions, reaction-time differences in a categorization task correlate with some attitudinal constructs and can differentiate groups. It is a reliable group-level signal in specific, controlled settings.

**C. Inflated claim made by later citations, institutions, and public discourse**
That the IAT measures your implicit racial bias specifically; that a high score means you are biased and will discriminate; that implicit bias training will reduce discriminatory behavior in the real world; and that the IAT is a valid basis for organizational policy and hiring-diversity interventions.

**Are A, B, and C the same claim?** No. They diverge significantly at each step.

**Where does inflation occur?** First between A and B (individual vs. group level), then massively between B and C (laboratory construct → policy tool). The original paper itself overclaims at the individual level; downstream inflation then compounds this into a policy instrument.

-----

## 2. Measurement Extraction

|Parameter            |Detail                                                                                            |
|---------------------|--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
|Sample               |Three studies; ~32–46 undergraduate participants each                                             |
|Population           |University students (WEIRD convenience sample)                                                    |
|Recruitment          |Convenience sample                                                                                |
|Task                 |Computerized categorization — reaction times (ms) across compatible vs. incompatible pairings     |
|Outcome variable     |Reaction-time difference score (D-score) between task blocks                                      |
|Time scale           |Single laboratory session                                                                         |
|Controls             |Block order counterbalanced; few confounds controlled                                             |
|What was NOT measured|Discriminatory behavior, real-world decisions, long-term stability, unconscious cognition directly|

**Is the measured variable direct evidence of the claimed construct?**
No. Reaction-time difference scores are a proxy for association strength, which is a proxy for “implicit attitude,” which is a proxy for discriminatory behavior. There are at minimum three inferential leaps before reaching the construct the IAT is institutionally deployed to diagnose.

## 4. Level-of-Analysis Check

**Evidence collected at:** Laboratory task, single session, undergraduate convenience sample, reaction-time measure

**Claim applied at:** Individual diagnosis of bias; population-level policy; workplace intervention design; clinical and legal settings; institutional hiring and promotion decisions

**Assumptions required to bridge the gap (none tested):**

1. RT differences reflect stable internal mental structures
1. Lab associations predict real-world behavior under professional constraints
1. Undergraduate convenience samples generalize to professional populations
1. Individual scores are reliable enough to diagnose individuals

## 6. Ecological Validity Check

- **Does the task resemble real-world discrimination?** No. Timed button-pressing under no social stakes bears no resemblance to a hiring decision, medical diagnosis, or police encounter.
- **Does the sample resemble the population the claim is applied to?** No. Undergraduates → working professionals, clinicians, police officers.
- **Does the time scale match?** No. Single session vs. repeated real-world decisions over careers.

**Verdict:** Not ecologically valid for the strong claim (individual bias diagnosis → real-world discriminatory behavior). Ecologically valid only for the narrower claim: group-level differences in association speed exist under controlled laboratory conditions.

-----

## 7. Decisive-Test Check

**The decisive test would be:** A pre-registered prospective study — IAT scores at baseline predicting real discriminatory decisions in professional populations, above self-report, with validated behavioral outcome measures, conducted by researchers without financial or institutional interest in a particular result.

**Has this test been run?** No. Not in 27 years of deployment.

-----

## 8. Citation-Use Audit

Downstream inflation sequence:

> RT lab finding → “IAT measures implicit attitudes” → “IAT measures individual bias” → “implicit bias is pervasive; training is warranted” → federal training mandates and HR deployment

**Classification: Premise laundering.** The original modest laboratory finding has been progressively inflated into an unearned foundation for institutional policy.

## 11. Verdict

**C/D — Useful for a narrower claim, invalid for the stronger claim.**

The 1998 paper validly demonstrates that reaction-time differences exist between compatible and incompatible category pairings under laboratory conditions — that is real and replicable. It is a legitimate methodological contribution to the study of automatic associations at the group level. However, the paper overclaims at the individual level from the start, and the downstream citation network has inflated a group-level laboratory effect into an individual diagnostic tool and policy instrument. The measure does not meet standard psychometric thresholds for individual-level diagnosis and has no demonstrated ability to predict who will actually discriminate in real-world settings.

-----

## 12. Paper-Safe Wording

**A. Neutral summary:**
Greenwald et al. (1998) demonstrated that reaction times differ between compatible and incompatible category pairings in a computerized task, and proposed this as a measure of implicit association strength.

**B. Direct methodological critique:**
The IAT measures reaction-time differences in a laboratory categorization task — a proxy three inferential steps removed from discriminatory behavior — and these scores lack the test-retest reliability and predictive validity required for individual-level diagnosis or policy application.

**C. Strong adversarial version:**
The IAT’s institutional deployment as a bias-detection and training tool rests on a chain of unvalidated inferences from a lab RT task with undergraduate samples, and the measure’s predictive validity for real-world discrimination has shrunk toward zero across successive meta-analyses correcting for publication bias.

**D. Decisive-test sentence:**
The question of whether IAT scores predict real-world discriminatory behavior would require a pre-registered prospective study with professional populations, behavioral outcome measures, and controls for explicit bias — a study the field has not run in 27 years of deployment.

-----

## 13. Failure-Mode Classification

- [x] **Untested premise** — RT differences reflect stable, personal implicit attitudes rather than cultural knowledge or situational noise
- [x] **Proxy problem** — RT difference scores are a proxy for association strength, which is a proxy for attitude, which is a proxy for behavior
- [x] **Weak proxy** — IAT scores explain ~1–5% of the variation in discriminatory behavior
- [x] **Fatal proxy failure** — for individual-level diagnosis and policy deployment
- [x] **Level-of-analysis error** — lab task with undergraduates → institutional policy for professionals
- [x] **Ecological-validity failure** — timed button-pressing under no stakes does not resemble professional decision-making
- [x] **Omitted confound** — cultural familiarity with stereotypes vs. personal endorsement is never disentangled
- [x] **Citation laundering** — RT lab finding laundered into proof that bias is pervasive and trainable
- [x] **Premise laundering** — “IAT measures implicit bias” treated as established fact in downstream policy literature
- [x] **Ignored adjacent literature** — psychometric reliability standards, personnel selection validity, attitude-behavior gap
- [x] **Missing decisive test** — no prospective behavioral study with professional samples has been run
- [x] **Intervention ratchet** — training industry continues expanding despite null behavioral effects
