# Field Diagnostic: 44 Questions

## Field: Implicit Bias Research / Implicit Social Cognition

**Foundational paper:** Greenwald, McGhee & Schwartz (1998)
**Central contested claim:** IAT scores measure individual implicit bias and predict discriminatory behavior in real-world professional settings, justifying mandatory training interventions at scale.

**Section A verdict:** All failure-mode clusters confirmed present.
**Section B verdict:** Challenge is runnable with appropriate collaborators.
**Section C verdict:** Green flags across all five calibration questions. Challenge is methodological, not motivated.

-----

## Section A: Does the Failure Mode Exist?

### On the Core Claim

**1. What is the field’s central claim?**

Individuals carry implicit biases — automatic, unconscious associations between social groups and evaluative attributes — that can be measured by the IAT, predict discriminatory behavior in consequential professional settings, and can be reduced through awareness-based training, thereby reducing discriminatory outcomes.

-----

**2. Is this claim presented as an empirical finding or a theoretical framework? Is there a meaningful difference between how it is stated and how it is treated?**

Stated as an empirical finding. Treated as a theoretical framework with policy authority.

The meaningful difference: the original 1998 paper framed the IAT as a laboratory instrument for measuring differential association strength with specific scope conditions. By the time the claim reached federal training programs, HR departments, medical education, and legal proceedings, it had been reframed as an established fact about individual psychology with direct behavioral implications. The gap between “this RT difference exists in undergraduates under controlled conditions” and “this score tells you whether you will discriminate against patients / job applicants / defendants” was never closed empirically. It was closed rhetorically, through citation repetition and institutional adoption.

-----

**3. When was this claim first established? Original study? Sample size? Population?**

Original paper: Greenwald, McGhee & Schwartz (1998), *JPSP*, 74(6).

Three laboratory studies. Sample sizes: approximately 32–46 undergraduate participants per study. Population: university students — WEIRD convenience samples with no professional context, no stakes, and no accountability.

The foundational individual-prediction claim was established on samples of roughly 35–46 undergraduates in single laboratory sessions. The gap between this population and the populations the claim was subsequently applied to — hiring managers, clinicians, law enforcement officers, judges — was never addressed in the original paper and has not been closed by subsequent research.

-----

**4. Has the original claim been directly replicated by independent researchers using pre-registered methods?**

The RT effect — that reaction times differ between compatible and incompatible pairings — replicates reliably. That narrow finding is not contested.

The individual-prediction claim has not been confirmed by pre-registered prospective replication at the appropriate level of analysis. The relevant sequence:

- Greenwald et al. (2009) author-run meta-analysis: r ≈ .27 (inclusive coding)
- Oswald et al. (2013) independent meta-analysis, stricter coding: r ≈ .15
- Kurdi et al. (2019) largest meta-analysis: r ≈ .097–.15 for the race IAT
- Forscher et al. (2019) 492-study pre-registered meta-analysis: no behavioral mediation

Direction of travel: as independent teams apply stricter methods and publication-bias corrections, the effect shrinks. No pre-registered prospective study in professional populations has confirmed the individual behavioral prediction claim.

-----

**5. Is there a version that is well-supported and a version that is not? Does the field distinguish between them?**

**Well-supported:** Regional and national aggregate IAT scores track social change over time and covary with measurable disparities in policing, health, and education (Payne et al. 2017; Charlesworth & Banaji 2019, 2024).

**Not supported:** Individual IAT scores diagnose personal implicit bias, predict whether a specific person will discriminate, and justify mandatory individual-feedback training as a behavioral intervention.

**Does the field distinguish?** Inconsistently. The bias-of-crowds model (Payne et al. 2017) makes this distinction correctly and has been partially incorporated into academic literature. It has not been incorporated into Project Implicit’s individual feedback interface, federal training programs, HR curricula, or introductory textbooks.

-----

### On Empirical Status

**6. Does the field treat this claim as settled or as an active research question? What would it take to publish a challenge?**

The founding cluster treats it as substantially settled. Challenges are publishable — Oswald et al. (2013) in flagship *JPSP*, Schimmack in *Perspectives on Psychological Science* — but the pattern of engagement is rebuttal rather than incorporation. For a challenge to materially change institutional behavior, it would need to either come from within the founding cluster or accumulate sufficient independent replication failures to force a reckoning. Forscher et al. (2019), co-authored by Nosek, is the closest thing to an internal concession; it has not changed the Project Implicit feedback interface or the training industry’s practices.

-----

**7. Has the field specified what evidence would falsify the central claim?**

No. The founding cluster has never specified a predictive validity threshold below which the IAT would be declared invalid for individual diagnostic use. Blanton and Jaccard (2006, 2009) explicitly challenged the field to specify what magnitude of RT difference corresponds to what magnitude of discriminatory tendency. This challenge was rebutted but not answered. The absence of a falsification criterion is itself a diagnostic finding.

-----

**8. Has the field’s main prediction been tested against a natural experiment or long-running intervention? Did the model update?**

**Training interventions tested:** UK EHRC reviewed 18 studies — no evidence of improved workplace equality outcomes. Lai et al. (2016) pre-registered — effects did not persist beyond the immediate session. Forscher et al. (2019) 492-study meta-analysis — no behavioral mediation. The training prediction has been tested repeatedly and consistently failed.

**Model update:** No. The training industry has expanded. When training studies show null results, the field’s response is “more research is needed” or “effects require longer intervention periods” — not model revision. This is the intervention ratchet in operation.

-----

### On the Proxy

**10. What does the field actually measure?**

A reaction-time difference score (D-score) between two computerized categorization task conditions. Taken in a single session, typically online or in a laboratory, with no behavioral stakes.

**11. What does the field claim to explain?**

Individual implicit bias — the personal, automatic, unconscious evaluative associations a person holds toward social groups — and its causal role in producing discriminatory behavior in consequential real-world professional settings.

**12. Are the measurement instruments and the target construct the same thing?**

No. Four inferential steps, each contested:

1. RT difference → association strength: Confounded by salience asymmetry, recoding, block order effects
1. Association strength → implicit attitude: Test-retest r ≈ .34–.56; scores shift with context, priming, repetition
1. Implicit attitude → individual discrimination propensity: r ≈ .097–.15; incremental validity above self-report 1–5%
1. Discrimination propensity → actual discriminatory outcome in professional context: Never directly tested

**13. What confounds are routinely present that the field does not control for?**

|Confound                                                               |Controlled?|Practitioner-obvious?         |
|-----------------------------------------------------------------------|-----------|------------------------------|
|Cultural familiarity with stereotypes vs. personal endorsement         |No         |Yes                           |
|Recoding (participants mentally recategorize)                          |No         |Yes to cognitive psychologists|
|Block order effects                                                    |Partially  |Yes to psychometricians       |
|Situational priming                                                    |No         |Yes                           |
|Publication bias in validity studies                                   |No         |Yes to meta-analysts          |
|Environmental stability vs. individual attitude (Vuletich & Payne 2019)|No         |Newer finding                 |

**14. Has anyone formally challenged the validity of the primary instrument?**

Multiple challenges, consistent non-uptake pattern:

- Blanton & Jaccard (2006, 2009): Scoring cutoffs unanchored. Rebutted. Not incorporated.
- Oswald et al. (2013): r ≈ .15. Counter-meta-analysis published. Neither side conceded. Field did not update.
- Schimmack (2019, 2021): No discriminant validity. Not engaged substantively by founding cluster.
- Meissner et al. (2019): Weak prediction, negligible incremental validity. Partially acknowledged; reframed.
- Forscher et al. (2019): No behavioral mediation across 492 studies. Present in literature; null finding underemphasised.

### On the Citation Network

**15. Top 5–10 most-cited papers — do they cite each other or primary experimental evidence?**

Top 5: Greenwald & Banaji 1995 (~14k), Greenwald et al. 1998 (~22k), Greenwald et al. 2003 (~9k), Greenwald et al. 2009 (~4.5k), Nosek et al. 2007 (~2.5k). These papers primarily cite each other. The 2003 scoring paper cites the 1998 paper to establish the instrument it is standardizing. The 2009 meta-analysis was conducted by the same authors who created the instrument. This is internal refinement cited as external validation.

**16. Small founding cluster? Were their claims directly tested or adopted as premises?**

Yes. Founding cluster: Greenwald & Banaji 1995 → Greenwald et al. 1998 → Greenwald et al. 2003. Claims adopted as premises, not directly tested. No independent group has run a pre-registered study asking: does this instrument, applied to a new professional population, predict their actual discriminatory behavior?

**17. What disciplines are in the dominant network? What are absent?**

Represented: Social psychology, cognitive psychology (selectively), personality psychology.

Absent with relevant expertise: Psychometrics, personnel selection / I-O psychology, labor economics, behavioral genetics, epidemiology / ecological statistics.

**18. Papers that challenged the consensus but are rarely cited or only dismissed?**

- **Schimmack (2019, 2021):** Most technically rigorous challenge; rarely cited by dominant cluster; not engaged substantively
- **Blanton & Jaccard (2006–2015):** Cited regularly but only to be rebutted; substantive point never answered
- **Oswald et al. (2013):** Cited primarily in context of Greenwald et al.’s rebuttal; “Greenwald–Oswald debate” treated as evidence of contestation rather than as a null finding
- **Schmidt & Hunter (1998):** Not cited at all

### On Extrapolation

**19. At what population level were foundational studies conducted?**

Undergraduate convenience samples. Single laboratory sessions. No stakes. No professional context. No real consequences. Sample sizes 32–46.

**20. At what population level does the field make its claims?**

Hiring managers. Clinicians. Law enforcement officers. Judges. Teachers. All adults who have taken the test — forty million people told whether they have “slight,” “moderate,” or “strong” implicit preferences.

**21. What is the gap? What assumptions are required to bridge them? Are they stated?**

The gap is categorical. Bridging assumptions (all untested, almost all implicit):

1. RT differences in undergraduate samples generalize to professional populations
1. Single-session scores predict stable individual traits
1. Implicit associations predict behavior under professional accountability and institutional constraints
1. Individual-level measure sensitivity is sufficient for personalized feedback

**22. Are there contexts where the field’s model predicts convergence that has not materialized?**

Yes. The training prediction. The model predicts: identify implicit bias → raise awareness → reduce discriminatory behavior. Tested at scale across multiple countries and industries. The behavioral outcome has not materialized. The field’s explanation has not been “the model is wrong” but “more intervention is needed” — an unfalsifiable adjustment.

-----

### On the Ratchet

**23. What interventions? At what scale?**

Implicit bias training deployed at: US DOJ (28,000+ employees), US federal judiciary, NHS and UK medical education, academic hiring, corporate HR globally, law schools, medical schools, police departments. Scale: millions of person-hours, hundreds of millions of dollars annually.

**24. What metrics improved? What did not?**

Improved: IAT scores immediately post-training (the metric the training targets). Did not improve: Representation gaps; pay disparities; documented racial disparities in clinical treatment; law enforcement outcomes; audit-study measures of discriminatory behavior.

**25. Was the primary outcome measure independent of the intervention?**

No. The primary outcome measure in most training evaluation studies is the post-training IAT score — the instrument the training is designed to move. Circular: training people to be aware of IAT scores produces lower IAT scores, cited as evidence the training works. When discriminatory behavior is measured as an outcome, no effect is found.

**26. Has the intervention been reversed? How did the field respond?**

Several institutional adoptions have been quietly scaled back after null evaluations. The field’s response has generally been silence rather than acknowledgment.

**27. When the intervention fails, what does the field prescribe next?**

More intervention, at a structural level. The current evolution is a shift from individual implicit bias training to “structural” and “systemic” bias interventions — changing hiring processes, promotion criteria, institutional policies. Presented as a refinement of the original model, not a falsification. The logic is preserved; the intervention ratchets upward.

## Section B: Can the Challenge Be Run?

**28. Direct experience with the phenomenon?**

The challenge is constructed from the published literature. The relevant expertise is methodological — construct validity, citation network analysis, psychometric standards. A collaborator with direct professional experience in one of the deployment contexts (HR, clinical medicine, law enforcement) would strengthen the challenge by providing practitioner-level confound identification.

**29. Specific instances where the field’s model doesn’t match observed practice?**

From the published literature: (1) Audit studies consistently find racial discrimination in hiring without IAT data — the phenomenon is measurable and real without the proxy. (2) Training programs evaluated with behavioral outcomes consistently find null effects. (3) Project Implicit tells users they have “strong automatic preference for European Americans” based on a few hundred milliseconds of RT difference — a claim the psychometric literature does not support.

**30. Confounds the field ignores that you have encountered directly?**

Cultural knowledge vs. personal endorsement — the IAT cannot distinguish between a person who knows their culture stereotypes a group and a person who personally endorses that stereotype. This confound is obvious to any researcher familiar with attitude literature and has not been systematically addressed.

**31. Can you name the proxy problem in terms the field would recognize?**

Yes, and it has been named in the literature by established researchers: the IAT measures a differential RT that is presented as evidence of implicit attitude, which is presented as evidence of individual discrimination propensity. Each inferential step requires assumptions that have not been validated at the level at which the instrument is deployed. The test-retest reliability is insufficient for individual classification under standard psychometric criteria. The predictive validity for behavioral outcomes is below the threshold required to justify selection or diagnostic use under personnel selection standards.

**32. What evidence would directly test the central claim?**

A pre-registered prospective study recruiting professional populations in consequential decision-making roles, measuring IAT and matched explicit attitudes at baseline, tracking actual discriminatory decisions longitudinally with behavioral outcome measures validated independently of the IAT, testing whether IAT scores predict those outcomes above explicit self-report, conducted by researchers without financial or institutional interest in a particular result.

**33. Does this data exist?**

Not in the required form. Some organizational datasets where both IAT scores and hiring decisions are recorded exist but are proprietary and have not been published as prospective validation studies.

**34. What would it take to collect it?**

Partnership with a large organization willing to administer baseline IAT and explicit attitude measures to decision-makers and track actual decisions over 12–24 months. Most rigorous design would use audit methods (blind résumé review, standardized clinical vignettes) rather than naturalistic observation, to control for behavioral moderation by observed participants.

**35. Would it be publishable?**

If supportive of IAT: yes, without question. If null: yes with pre-registration (Oswald et al. 2013 in *JPSP* establishes precedent) but with substantial gatekeeping risk. Pre-registration is the key protection against post-hoc reframing.

**36–39. Position questions**

These calibration questions address the researcher’s personal position and are for the human investigator to answer.

## Section C: Calibration Questions

**40. Is there a version of the field’s central claim you accept as valid?**

Yes. The IAT is a legitimate ecological instrument for studying population-level associative patterns. Regional and national aggregate IAT scores track social change over time and covary with measurable disparities in policing, health outcomes, and education. The field has produced genuinely important evidence that implicit associations exist at the aggregate level and correlate with structural inequalities.

The overreach begins at the step from aggregate ecological signal to individual diagnosis.

**41. Can you describe the strongest version of the opposing argument?**

The bias-of-crowds model (Payne, Vuletich & Lundberg 2017):

> “Individual IAT scores are environmentally sensitive rather than individually stable — they reflect the associative environment the person inhabits, not a fixed personal trait. This is a feature, not a bug: it means aggregate IAT scores are valid indicators of the discriminatory environment that groups of decision-makers collectively create. Even if no individual score reliably predicts individual behavior, the aggregate of scores across a hiring committee, a clinical department, or a police precinct is a valid measure of the implicit bias environment those groups will produce. Structural interventions — blind evaluation, standardized criteria, accountability mechanisms — are warranted not because individual bias is reliably measurable but because aggregate environments produce discriminatory outcomes.”

This version concedes individual diagnostic invalidity, preserves aggregate ecological validity, and reframes the intervention target from individuals to environments. It is the most defensible position the field can currently occupy.

**42. If the decisive test came back in the field’s favor, would you update?**

Yes. If a well-powered, pre-registered prospective study in professional populations found that individual IAT scores predict consequential discriminatory decisions above self-report at r ≥ .20–.25, the case for individual deployment would require serious reassessment. The audit’s verdict is not that implicit associations don’t influence behavior; it is that the IAT has not been validated as an individual diagnostic at the level required for its deployed use.

**43. Are there aspects of the field’s work you would cite approvingly?**

Yes:

1. The 1998 methodological innovation is genuine — the IAT is a clever instrument and its group-level RT finding is real
1. Payne, Vuletich & Lundberg (2017) ecological reframing is theoretically sound and empirically grounded
1. Charlesworth & Banaji’s longitudinal work showing national decline in IAT scores over time is important and well-designed
1. Forscher et al. (2019) is methodologically rigorous and represents exactly the kind of large-scale, pre-registered synthesis the field needs more of
1. The finding that implicit associations exist and correlate at the aggregate level with structural disparities is supported and important

**44. Is the failure mode a property of the field’s institutional structure, or a claim about individual researchers’ motives?**

It is a property of the field’s institutional structure. The documented features are:

- Same three researchers control founding papers, scoring standard, primary validity meta-analysis, main rebuttals, and institutional delivery vehicle (Project Implicit) simultaneously
- Citation network is functionally self-referential: >70–80% of IAT papers cite founding cluster
- Critique enters the network, receives rebuttal from the same authors, field moves on — this is structural, not a coordination problem
- Decisive test not run in 27 years — not because individuals are blocking it, but because the field’s incentive structure does not reward running a test that could retire its primary instrument
- Institutional adoption occurred before validation — an institutional sequence problem, not a motive problem

The motive for any individual researcher is irrelevant to the audit’s findings. The structural conditions are documented and sufficient to explain the observed pattern of non-correction.

## Final Diagnostic Verdict

**The failure mode is present, the challenge is runnable, and the calibration is clean.**

The field has a genuine and important ecological finding at the aggregate level. It has an institutional deployment infrastructure that has extended that finding to individual diagnosis, behavioral prediction, and mandatory training without the validation those uses require. The citation network is structurally self-referential, the decisive test has not been run, the training prediction has failed every serious test, and the founding cluster has not updated its core claims in response to 27 years of critique.

The most defensible position for a challenger is the one the field’s own best theorists have now largely conceded: the IAT is a valid population-level ecological instrument. It is not a validated individual diagnostic. The infrastructure built on the latter claim has not earned its deployment. Running the decisive test is the productive next step — and the field’s failure to run it after 27 years is the finding that most needs explaining.
