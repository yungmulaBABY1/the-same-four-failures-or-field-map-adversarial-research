## Summary of Findings

### What was measured

A reaction-time difference score (the D-score) between compatible and incompatible category pairings in a computerized laboratory task administered to undergraduate convenience samples in single sessions. Sample sizes in the founding studies: approximately 32–46 participants.

### What was claimed

That the measure captures individual implicit attitudes toward social groups, predicts discriminatory behavior in consequential professional settings, and justifies mandatory awareness-based training at institutional scale.

### Where the proxy chain fails

The inferential chain has four links, each contested:

1. **RT difference → association strength** — confounded by salience asymmetry, recoding, and block order effects
1. **Association strength → implicit attitude** — test-retest r ≈ .34–.56, insufficient for individual classification (threshold: r ≥ .80)
1. **Implicit attitude → discriminatory behavior** — meta-analytic r ≈ .097–.15; incremental validity above self-report: 1–5%
1. **IAT score reduction → behavior change via training** — directly falsified by Forscher et al. (2019): 492 studies, 87,418 participants, no behavioral mediation

### What the ecological evidence does support

Regional and national aggregate IAT scores track social change over time (Charlesworth & Banaji 2019) and covary with measurable disparities in policing, health, and education (Payne, Vuletich & Lundberg 2017; Charlesworth & Banaji 2024). The IAT is a legitimate population-level ecological instrument. The bias-of-crowds model (Payne et al. 2017) explicitly concedes individual unreliability and relocates validity to the aggregate — making the strongest pro-IAT theoretical framework internally consistent with the audit’s verdict against individual diagnosis.

### The decisive test (not yet run)

A pre-registered prospective study recruiting professional populations in consequential decision-making roles, measuring IAT scores and matched explicit attitudes at baseline, tracking actual discriminatory decisions longitudinally, and testing whether IAT scores predict those outcomes above self-report. This study has not been conducted in 27 years of deployment.

### Citation network structure

- **Founding cluster:** Greenwald & Banaji (1995) → Greenwald, McGhee & Schwartz (1998) → Greenwald, Nosek & Banaji (2003)
- **Same three authors** dominate the founding papers, the scoring standard, the primary validity meta-analysis, the main rebuttals to critics, and the institutional delivery vehicle (Project Implicit) simultaneously
- **>70–80%** of IAT papers cite the founding cluster
- **Critiques** by Oswald (2013), Schimmack (2019/2021), and Meissner (2019) were published but not incorporated
- **Adjacent fields** with direct expertise — psychometrics, personnel selection, labor economics, ecological epidemiology — are structurally excluded

## Calibration

This audit accepts the following claims as valid:

- The IAT RT effect is real and replicable under laboratory conditions
- Aggregate regional IAT scores covary with measurable structural disparities
- The field has produced important evidence about population-level implicit associations
- The bias-of-crowds model (Payne et al. 2017) is theoretically sound

The overreach begins at the step from aggregate ecological signal to individual diagnosis.

The strongest opposing argument — the bias-of-crowds ecological model — has been engaged directly. It does not rescue individual diagnosis; it concedes it.

## Diagnostic Flags

**Red flags present:**

- No falsification criterion specified
- Training prediction failed; ratchet response is structural expansion, not model revision
- Decisive test not run in 27 years
- Founding cluster controls measure, meta-analyses, rebuttals, and institutional delivery simultaneously
- Adjacent fields with directly relevant expertise are structurally excluded

**Green flags present:**

- Valid version of claim identified and accepted (ecological aggregate use)
- Strongest opposing argument describable and engaged (bias-of-crowds model)
- Would update on positive decisive test (specified threshold: r ≥ .20–.25 above self-report)
- Field’s work cited approvingly in five specific instances
