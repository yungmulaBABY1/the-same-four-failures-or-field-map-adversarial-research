OPPOSITIONAL CITATION & NEGLECTED EVIDENCE COUNTER-AUDIT PROMPT

Purpose:
You are now running an adversarial counter-audit against a previously generated citation-network audit, construct-validity audit, thesis paragraph, or short paper.

The goal is not to find more evidence for the audit.
The goal is to find the strongest evidence that opposes, complicates, narrows, weakens, or potentially overturns the audit.

This prompt is designed to prevent motivated reasoning. It forces the user to ask:

- What did I miss?
- What does the other side have?
- What evidence would change my conclusion?
- Did the field actually engage its critics?
- Did I overstate the claim?
- Are there adjacent fields with better evidence or better methods?

Run this audit rigorously and without softening. Prioritize direct, high-quality opposition over weak or tangential material. Do not produce flat lists — rank findings by how much they threaten the original claim.

-----

ANTI-HEDGING RULE

Do not default to false balance. If the opposition is weak, say so. If it is strong, say so. Do not soften the verdict to appear thorough or even-handed. A prompt that finds strong opposition and calls it “mixed” is a failure. A prompt that finds weak opposition and calls the audit “strengthened” is a success — but only if the opposition was genuinely tested.

-----

DATA-HONESTY RULE

For every paper, study, report, or source listed, state how it was found:

- cites foundational paper directly
- cites citation hub directly
- cited by foundational paper
- cited by citation hub
- appeared in “related papers”
- found through keyword search
- found through adjacent-field search
- supplied by user
- model memory / needs verification
- unverified source lead

If you cannot verify a citation relationship, label it: “Unverified citation relationship.”

Do not invent citation links.
Do not claim that a paper cites another paper unless the citation relationship is verified.
Do not treat a paper as oppositional merely because it is thematically different.
Do not count weak, tangential, or speculative criticism as strong opposition.
Do not omit strong evidence that supports the original field’s position.

-----

EVIDENCE-QUALITY SCORING

For every source listed, assign an evidence-quality score:

0 = weak / speculative / rhetorical
1 = relevant but limited
2 = strong methodological critique
3 = direct empirical contradiction or strong empirical support
4 = decisive or near-decisive evidence

Also label source type:

- primary empirical study
- replication / failed replication
- meta-analysis / systematic review
- methodological critique
- natural experiment
- policy evaluation
- adjacent-field canonical paper
- practitioner / domain-expert evidence
- public reporting / journalism
- commentary / editorial
- theoretical paper
- model estimate / needs verification

-----

RELATIONSHIP TO CENTRAL CLAIM

For each source, classify its relationship to the central claim:

- directly contradicts
- weakens
- narrows
- complicates
- supports a weaker version
- supports the original claim
- irrelevant after inspection
- unclear / needs verification

Do not overstate opposition. Some evidence narrows a claim without refuting it. Some evidence criticizes one use of a construct while supporting another. Mark this clearly.

List the 5–10 most important papers that cite the foundational work or citation hub but criticize, weaken, narrow, or undermine it. Rank by strength of opposition — most threatening first.

For each source, provide:

- full citation and year
- source type and how it was found
- what foundational paper or hub it cites
- main criticism
- evidence-quality score (0–4)
- relationship to central claim
- strength of opposition (weak / moderate / strong / severe / potentially decisive)
- whether the dominant field engaged it substantively
- whether the critique changed later citation behavior

B. NON-CITING OPPOSITION PAPERS

List papers, studies, reports, or literatures that oppose, weaken, or complicate the central claim but do not cite the foundational paper or citation hub. Rank by relevance and evidence quality.

These matter because a field can appear internally coherent while contrary evidence exists outside the citation network.

For each source, provide:

- full citation and year
- source type and how it was found
- main argument or finding
- why it matters
- why it may have been outside the citation network
- evidence-quality score (0–4)
- relationship to central claim
- whether the field should have engaged it

C. NEGLECTED REFERENCES THE HUB SHOULD HAVE CITED

List important papers, studies, reports, or literatures that the foundational authors and/or citation hub should have referenced but neglected. These are not necessarily oppositional in intent — they may contain relevant methods, confounds, measurement standards, or findings that would have constrained the claim.

For each:

- full citation and year
- source type and relevance to the foundational claim
- what method, confound, or evidence it contributes
- whether the foundational paper cited it
- whether the hub cited it
- effect of omission (minor context loss / moderate weakness / major evidentiary gap / fatal omission for the strong claim)
- evidence-quality score (0–4)

-----

D. IGNORED ADJACENT FIELDS

List 4–6 adjacent fields or literatures that are directly relevant but were ignored, excluded, or minimally engaged.

For each adjacent field, provide:

- field name and key papers/authors
- why it is relevant
- what evidence it provides against, beneath, or adjacent to the claim
- what better measurement instrument or method it offers
- what decisive test it suggests
- whether the target field adopted that method
- what the target field would have had to confront if it engaged this literature
- effect of neglect (minor / moderate / major / fatal for strong claim)

|Adjacent field|Key sources|Why relevant|Better method / measure|Decisive test suggested|Engagement by target field|Effect of neglect|

-----

E. STRONGEST EVIDENCE SUPPORTING THE ORIGINAL CLAIM

Do not only search for evidence that supports the audit. Identify the strongest evidence that supports the foundational paper, citation hub, or mainstream claim.

For each source:

- full citation and year
- source type
- what part of the original claim it supports
- whether it directly answers the construct-validity problem
- whether it runs or approximates the decisive test
- whether it supports the strong claim or only a narrower version
- whether it changes the audit verdict
- evidence-quality score (0–4)

Classify impact on audit:

- no effect
- narrows the critique
- weakens the critique
- seriously challenges the critique
- overturns the critique

Required table:

|Supporting source|Year|What it supports|Answers construct problem?|Runs decisive test?|Strong or narrow claim?|Impact on audit|
|-----------------|---:|----------------|--------------------------|-------------------|-----------------------|---------------|

Be honest. If the strongest support for the original claim is genuinely strong, say so. If it only supports a narrower version, say that clearly.

-----

F. CITATION-NETWORK UPTAKE OF OPPOSITION

For each strong oppositional paper or neglected source identified above, answer:

- Did the dominant citation hub cite it?
- Did later papers cite it?
- Was it engaged substantively, dismissed, minimized, or ignored?
- Did the field update after it appeared?
- Did the opposition remain peripheral while the original claim continued spreading?
- Did later reviews preserve the critique or bury it?

Required table:

|Opposition / neglected source|Cited by hub?|Later uptake?|Engagement quality|Did field update?|Network implication|
|-----------------------------|-------------|-------------|------------------|-----------------|-------------------|

Engagement quality options:

- not cited
- cited in passing
- cited but dismissed
- cited and reframed
- substantively engaged
- incorporated into model revision
- unclear / needs verification

Network implication options:

- healthy uptake
- partial uptake
- critique contained
- critique marginalized
- evidence ignored
- field updated
- field did not update

-----

H. REVISED THESIS IMPACT

Classify how the counter-audit affects the original thesis. Do not choose a weaker category when a stronger one is accurate.

A. Thesis strengthened — opposition confirms ignored evidence / critique non-uptake.
B. Thesis narrowed — original critique remains valid but needs more precise scope.
C. Thesis softened — some claims were overstated.
D. Thesis seriously weakened — decisive contrary evidence exists.
E. Thesis overturned — audit’s central claim is wrong.
F. Mixed — thesis strengthened in one area and narrowed in another.

Then answer each question directly. Do not hedge:

1. Which claims should be removed?
1. Which claims should be narrowed?
1. Which claims should be strengthened?
1. Which claims require better sourcing?
1. Which claims survived the adversarial review unchanged?
1. Which evidence most improves the thesis?
1. Which evidence most threatens the thesis?

-----

I. REVISED THESIS PARAGRAPH

Write a revised, sharper thesis paragraph that incorporates the strongest counter-evidence.

Rules:

- Preserve the strongest valid critique.
- Do not soften merely to appease institutional readers.
- Do narrow claims that were too broad.
- Do distinguish direct evidence, inference, and speculation.
- Do acknowledge the strongest evidence for the original claim if it materially changes the argument.
- Do not use vague balancing phrases unless they do real evidentiary work.

The revised thesis should include:

- what was measured
- what was claimed
- where the proxy or construct gap is
- what evidence opposed the claim
- what evidence supports a narrower version
- what the decisive test would look like
- how the citation network handled opposition

-----

K. FINAL ONE-PARAGRAPH SUMMARY

Write one paragraph summarizing:

- the strongest counter-evidence found
- whether it weakens or strengthens the original audit
- whether the original thesis survives
- whether the field engaged or ignored the opposition
- what the most defensible revised position is

This paragraph should be direct, evidence-based, and free of unnecessary reassurance language. State the verdict plainly. Do not hedge.

-----

REMINDER

This prompt is not for finding more ammunition.
It is for testing whether the audit survives contact with the strongest opposing evidence.

A good audit becomes sharper after this process.
A weak audit becomes narrower.
A bad audit should be abandoned.

You may begin with suspicion.
You may not end with suspicion.

Start wherever the anomaly appears.
Finish with receipts.
