OFF-LABEL USE AUDIT PROMPT

Purpose:
This prompt audits the evidence base for a drug, intervention, device, or clinical protocol being used outside its approved or originally validated indication, population, or duration. It determines whether the off-label practice rests on solid evidence or has been normalized through weaker mechanisms — institutional momentum, marketing, guideline capture, or extrapolation from studies conducted under structurally different conditions.

This prompt is part of the adversarial research toolkit. It applies the same core question the toolkit asks in every domain: does the evidence actually support the claim being made, or has the claim outpaced the measurement?

Run this prompt standalone when conducting a deep audit of a specific off-label use. Run it alongside the Construct Validity Audit and the Internal Validity & Replication Audit for a complete three-layer assessment. The Off-Label Use Flag in the Construct Validity prompt will route you here automatically when two or more triggers fire.

The most important concept in this prompt is the evidence transfer problem: the study was conducted in population A under conditions B; the use is being justified for population C under conditions D. The gap between those is where most off-label harm hides.

-----

DATA-HONESTY RULE

For every claim about evidence quality, approval status, or normalization pathway, state how it was determined:

- Reported in the source itself
- Found in regulatory documentation (FDA, EMA, TGA, Health Canada, etc.)
- Found in independent systematic review or meta-analysis
- Found in replication literature
- Found in methodological critique or post-market surveillance
- Model memory / needs verification
- Unknown / not reported

Do not infer regulatory compliance or evidence adequacy from institutional adoption. Widespread use is not evidence of evidence.

-----

INPUT TEMPLATE

Drug / intervention / device:
[NAME AND APPROVED INDICATION(S)]

Off-label use being audited:
[SPECIFIC USE, POPULATION, DURATION BEING EXAMINED]

Source(s) cited to justify the off-label use:
[PASTE CITATIONS, ABSTRACTS, OR KEY CLAIMS]

Normalization pathway (if known):
[HOW DID THIS USE BECOME WIDESPREAD? GUIDELINES, MARKETING, CLINICAL MOMENTUM, ACTIVIST PRESSURE, ETC.]

Domain-expert concern:
[WHAT SMELLS WRONG? WHAT IS THE SUSPECTED EVIDENCE TRANSFER PROBLEM?]

-----

STEP 1: REGULATORY BASELINE

1. What is the drug / intervention officially approved for?
- Approved indication(s)
- Approved population (age, sex, diagnosis, comorbidity profile)
- Approved duration of use
- Approved dose range
- Regulatory jurisdiction(s) where approval applies
1. What is the off-label use being examined?
- Specific indication being treated
- Population receiving the off-label use
- Duration of use in practice
- Dose used off-label vs. approved dose
1. Is the off-label use common, growing, or already treated as standard of care in practice?
- Estimate of prevalence if available
- Is it endorsed by professional guidelines? On what evidence basis?
- Is it reimbursed by insurers or public health systems?

Required table:

|Approved indication|Approved population|Approved duration|Off-label use|Off-label population|Off-label duration|Gap magnitude|
|-------------------|-------------------|-----------------|-------------|--------------------|------------------|-------------|

Gap magnitude options: Minimal / Moderate / Large / Extreme

-----

STEP 2: EVIDENCE AUDIT

Answer each question clearly. Do not soften findings for comfort.

2.1 EVIDENCE QUALITY

What is the highest quality evidence that directly supports this specific off-label use in the relevant population?

- Randomized controlled trial(s) in the target population: [yes / no / partial]
- If yes: sample size, pre-registration status, replication status, effect size, follow-up duration
- If no: what evidence exists instead? (observational studies, case series, expert opinion, extrapolation)
- Is the existing evidence pre-registered and independently replicated?

Required table:

|Evidence type|Sample size|Population match|Pre-registered|Replicated|Follow-up duration|Evidence quality rating|
|-------------|-----------|----------------|--------------|----------|------------------|-----------------------|

Evidence quality ratings: Strong / Acceptable / Weak / Very weak / No direct evidence

2.2 EVIDENCE TRANSFER PROBLEM

Is the justification primarily based on extrapolating evidence from the approved indication or a different population?

- What population was the supporting evidence collected in?
- What population is the off-label use applied to?
- What biological, developmental, pharmacokinetic, or clinical differences exist between the two populations?
- Are those differences large enough to invalidate the extrapolation?
- Has the extrapolation been explicitly tested or is it assumed?
- Would a domain expert or regulatory body accept this extrapolation as valid?

State the evidence transfer gap in one sentence:
“The evidence was collected in [POPULATION A] under [CONDITIONS B] and is being used to justify treatment of [POPULATION C] under [CONDITIONS D].”

2.3 DECISIVE TEST

Has a well-powered, pre-registered RCT (or equivalent gold standard) ever been conducted specifically for this off-label use in the relevant population?

- If yes: what did it find? Is the finding replicated?
- If no: why not?
  - Ethical barriers to randomization?
  - Commercial disincentive (off-patent drug, no profit motive)?
  - Institutional resistance to testing an already-normalized practice?
  - Activist or advocacy pressure against trials?

State the decisive test in one sentence:
“The decisive test would be: [SPECIFIC TRIAL DESIGN, POPULATION, OUTCOME, AND DURATION].”

2.4 RISK-BENEFIT IN THE OFF-LABEL POPULATION

Are there meaningful differences in risk profile, comorbidities, developmental stage, or long-term outcomes between the approved and off-label populations?

- What adverse effects are documented in the approved population?
- Are those adverse effects more or less likely in the off-label population?
- Are there adverse effects specific to the off-label population that may not appear in the approved population (e.g., developmental effects in pediatric use, reproductive effects, long-term hormonal effects)?
- What is the follow-up duration of existing studies relative to the duration of real-world use?
- Are the effects reversible if the intervention is discontinued?

Reversibility assessment:

- Fully reversible
- Partially reversible
- Irreversible in the off-label population
- Unknown / inadequately studied

2.5 NORMALIZATION PATHWAY

How did this off-label use become widespread?

Map the pathway:

- Was adoption driven by strong evidence → clinical uptake → guideline endorsement?
- Or by: advocacy pressure → guideline endorsement → clinical adoption → retrospective evidence collection?
- Did pharmaceutical or device company marketing play a role?
- Did a small cluster of authors, institutions, or advocacy organizations drive normalization?
- Was there a specific event, campaign, or policy decision that accelerated adoption?
- Did guidelines endorse the use before decisive trial evidence existed?

Normalization pathway verdict:

- Evidence-driven: RCT evidence preceded widespread adoption
- Mixed: some evidence, significant institutional/advocacy momentum
- Momentum-driven: widespread adoption preceded or substituted for decisive evidence
- Advocacy-captured: normalization driven primarily by advocacy, activism, or ideological pressure

2.6 CITATION PATTERNS

Does the supporting literature rely on a small cluster of authors, institutions, or studies?

- Are the same authors producing the foundational papers, the validity meta-analyses, and the guideline recommendations?
- Are weak studies being cited as if they are robust?
- Are study limitations dropped as findings travel through citation chains?
- Has the foundational evidence been independently replicated by researchers without financial or ideological ties to the intervention?

Citation pattern verdict:

- Diverse independent literature
- Moderately concentrated
- Highly concentrated — cartel risk
- Circular — same cluster producing and validating its own evidence

2.7 INFORMED CONSENT AND LIABILITY

- Are patients being clearly informed that the use is off-label?
- Are the specific evidence limitations being communicated, not just the general off-label status?
- Are there regulatory, legal, or liability concerns that are being downplayed by practitioners or institutions?
- In pediatric or other vulnerable populations: are consent and assent processes adequate given the evidence uncertainty?

2.8 HARM SIGNALS

Have harm signals specific to the off-label use emerged?

- What adverse events have been reported in post-market surveillance, case reports, or observational studies?
- Are harm signals being tracked prospectively or only identified retrospectively?
- Are the harms reversible or irreversible?
- Have regulatory agencies in any jurisdiction issued warnings, restrictions, or reversals based on harm signals?
- Have independent researchers raised concerns that were dismissed or ignored by the field?

-----

STEP 3: INSTITUTIONAL AND INCENTIVE AUDIT

3.1 Financial incentives

- Do pharmaceutical companies, device manufacturers, clinics, or providers benefit financially from expanded off-label use?
- Are the researchers producing the supporting evidence funded by parties with financial interest in the outcome?
- Are there commercial reasons why a decisive RCT has not been conducted (e.g., off-patent drug with no profit motive, or on-patent drug where a negative trial would be costly)?

3.2 Guideline and institutional endorsement

- Have professional guidelines endorsed the off-label use?
- On what evidence basis were those guidelines issued?
- Who authored the guidelines? Do they have conflicts of interest?
- Have guidelines been updated in response to new evidence, or have they remained static despite accumulating concerns?

3.3 Regulatory and advocacy environment

- Is there evidence of regulatory capture — regulators adopting the perspective of the industry or advocacy groups they oversee?
- Has activist or advocacy pressure influenced the pace of adoption, the suppression of concerns, or the framing of evidence?
- Have researchers who raised methodological concerns been professionally penalized, deplatformed, or dismissed as motivated by bias rather than engaged on the substance of their findings?
- Have any jurisdictions paused, restricted, or reversed the off-label use after systematic review? What was the stated basis?

-----

STEP 4: OVERALL VERDICT

4.1 Evidence strength
Rate the evidence base for this specific off-label use:

- Strong: well-powered RCTs in the target population, independently replicated, consistent harm profile, adequate follow-up
- Acceptable: reasonable evidence with acknowledged limitations, extrapolation is defensible
- Weak: primarily extrapolated, observational, or underpowered; decisive test not run
- Very weak: case series, expert opinion, or advocacy-driven; no RCT evidence in target population
- Harmful: evidence of net harm in the off-label population, or irreversible effects without adequate informed consent

4.2 Use vs. evidence alignment
Is the current level of use justified by the evidence, or has it outpaced the evidence?

- Justified: evidence and use are appropriately matched
- Ahead of evidence: use has expanded faster than supporting evidence
- Evidence-free: widespread use exists without meaningful direct evidence in the target population
- Evidence-contradicted: use continues despite evidence of inadequate benefit or active harm

4.3 Decisive test
State what is missing:
“The decisive test that has not been run is: [SPECIFIC TRIAL DESIGN, POPULATION, OUTCOME MEASURES, AND FOLLOW-UP DURATION].”

4.4 Population-specific risks
Are there population-specific risks that are being inadequately addressed?
List each risk, its evidence basis, and whether it is being communicated to patients and practitioners.

-----

SUMMARY OUTPUT

Start with a short summary (2-4 sentences) stating:

- What the off-label use is
- What the highest quality direct evidence shows
- Whether use has outpaced evidence
- What the decisive missing test is

Then provide the full Step 1-4 assessment with clear headings.

End with a direct verdict:

[ ] Evidence-based off-label use — strong direct evidence in the target population, adequate follow-up, harm profile established
[ ] Weakly justified — extrapolated from approved indication without decisive test in target population; use may be reasonable but evidence is insufficient to confirm
[ ] Normalized without evidence — widespread use driven by institutional momentum, advocacy, or guideline capture rather than RCT evidence in the target population
[ ] Potentially harmful — harm signals present, effects inadequately studied, irreversible effects in off-label population, or evidence-contradicted use continuing

Then state the single most important finding in one sentence.

-----

INTEGRATION WITH MAIN TOOLKIT

This prompt covers the deployment gap: what was studied versus what is being done in practice.

The Construct Validity Audit covers the internal claims of the supporting studies.
The Internal Validity & Replication Audit covers whether the foundational findings are real and stable.
This prompt covers whether the study population and conditions match the real-world use case.

All three failures can compound:

- The study may not measure what it claims (construct validity failure)
- The finding may not be real (internal validity failure)
- The population and conditions may not match the actual use (evidence transfer failure)

A practice that fails all three is triply unjustified. State this directly in the verdict.

-----

CANDIDATE APPLICATIONS

The following domains are strong candidates for this audit given documented evidence transfer problems, normalization without decisive trial evidence, or emerging harm signals:

- Puberty blockers and cross-sex hormones in minors with gender dysphoria
- GLP-1 agonists (semaglutide, tirzepatide) for non-diabetic weight loss, particularly long-term use and pediatric use
- SSRIs in adolescents and children, particularly long-term use and discontinuation effects
- Benzodiazepines for chronic anxiety management beyond approved short-term duration
- Ketamine / esketamine for treatment-resistant depression outside controlled clinical settings
- Stimulant medications for adult ADHD diagnosed without childhood documentation
- Off-label antipsychotic use in dementia patients
- Testosterone therapy in non-hypogonadal men and women

This list is not exhaustive. Apply the prompt wherever the evidence transfer problem is suspected.

-----

REMINDER

Widespread use is not evidence of evidence.
Guideline endorsement is not evidence of evidence.
Institutional adoption is not evidence of evidence.

A practice normalized through momentum rather than measurement carries the same risk as a practice normalized through fraud — it just has more respectable company.

You may begin with vibes.
You may not end with vibes.

Start wherever the anomaly appears.
Finish with receipts.
