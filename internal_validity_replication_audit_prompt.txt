INTERNAL VALIDITY & REPLICATION AUDIT PROMPT

Purpose:
This prompt audits the internal validity of a specific study or finding — whether the result itself is likely to be real, replicable, and free of analytic artifacts — before assessing what it claims or how it has been cited.

Citation laundering and proxy problems operate on the output of a study. P-hacking, HARKing (Hypothesizing After Results are Known), selective reporting, and underpowered designs corrupt the finding at the source. A finding that fails internal validity cannot support the claims built on it regardless of how many papers cite it.

Run this audit before or alongside the construct validity audit. If the foundational finding fails internal validity, note this in the thesis. The claim is doubly weak: the study may not measure what it claims, and the finding may not be real.

-----

INPUTS

Provide the following:

1. Foundational paper(s):
   [Paste citation, abstract, methods, results, and statistical reporting where available.]
1. Central empirical finding:
   [State the finding in one sentence: what effect was found, in what population, measured how, of what magnitude.]
1. Claimed construct and downstream use:
   [What is this finding used to prove in subsequent literature or policy?]
1. Known replication attempts (if any):
   [Paste citations and outcomes if known. If none known, state “unknown.”]
1. Pre-registration status (if known):
   [Was the study pre-registered? At what stage? Did the published analysis match the pre-registered plan?]

-----

DATA-HONESTY RULE

For every claim about study design, analysis choices, or replication status, state how it was determined:

- reported in the paper itself
- reported in supplementary materials
- found in pre-registration record (OSF, ClinicalTrials, AsPredicted, etc.)
- found in replication literature
- found in methodological critique
- model memory / needs verification
- unknown / not reported

Do not infer pre-registration compliance from the absence of information. If pre-registration status is unknown, state it clearly.

-----

A. PRE-REGISTRATION AND ANALYSIS PLAN

1. Was the study pre-registered before data collection began?
- If yes: where, when, and what was registered?
- If no: was any aspect of the design registered after data collection but before analysis?
- If unknown: note as unknown
1. Did the published analysis match the pre-registered plan?
- Were primary outcomes changed?
- Were additional outcomes added post-hoc?
- Were subgroup analyses pre-specified or exploratory?
- Were exclusion criteria changed after data collection?
1. If not pre-registered: how many outcomes were tested in the study?
- How many are reported?
- What is the probability that at least one would reach p < .05 by chance given the number tested?

Required table:

|Pre-registration status|Analysis match|Outcomes tested|Outcomes reported|Post-hoc subgroups|P-hacking risk|
|-----------------------|--------------|---------------|-----------------|------------------|--------------|

P-hacking risk options: Low / Moderate / High / Severe / Cannot assess

-----

B. EFFECT SIZE AND STATISTICAL POWER

1. What is the reported effect size? (r, d, OR, RR, η², etc.)
1. How does this effect size compare to:
- The field’s typical effect sizes for similar constructs?
- The minimum effect size that would be practically or clinically meaningful?
- Later corrected meta-analyses or larger independent replications?
1. What was the sample size?
1. What statistical power did the study have to detect the reported effect at the reported size?
- Was the sample size determined in advance by power calculation?
- If not, at what power level was the study operating?
1. Winner’s curse assessment: studies that barely achieve p < .05 in underpowered designs systematically overestimate effect sizes. Is the reported effect size likely inflated?

Required table:

|Reported effect size|Field typical range|Corrected meta-analysis estimate|Sample size|Estimated power|Winner’s curse risk|
|--------------------|-------------------|--------------------------------|-----------|---------------|-------------------|

Winner’s curse risk options: Low / Moderate / High / Cannot assess

-----

C. HARKING (HYPOTHESIZING AFTER RESULTS ARE KNOWN)

1. Is the central finding presented as confirmatory (testing a pre-specified hypothesis) or exploratory?
1. Does the introduction describe a specific directional hypothesis that matches the result?
- If yes: was this hypothesis pre-registered before data collection?
- If no: was the hypothesis direction specified in any pre-study documentation?
1. Are there signs of post-hoc hypothesis specification?
- Vague or multiple hypotheses in the introduction that could accommodate various outcomes?
- Subgroup findings presented as primary results without pre-specification?
- Effect found in unexpected direction presented as theoretically predicted?
1. Does the discussion present exploratory findings as if they were confirmatory?

Required table:

|Hypothesis type|Pre-registered?|HARKing indicators|HARKing risk|
|---------------|---------------|------------------|------------|

HARKing risk options: Low / Moderate / High / Cannot assess

-----

D. SELECTIVE REPORTING

1. Are all pre-registered outcomes reported?
- If not: which outcomes are missing?
- Is there a plausible explanation for their omission?
1. Are there signs of selective subgroup reporting?
- Is the reported effect found in the full sample or only in a subgroup?
- Was the subgroup pre-specified?
1. Are there signs of selective time-point reporting?
- Is the reported effect found at all time points or only at one?
- Was the reported time point pre-specified?
1. Publication bias assessment:
- Is this a published positive result in a high-impact journal?
- Are there known null or negative replication attempts?
- Is the effect size unusually large compared to later replications (consistent with publication bias)?

Required table:

|Outcomes registered|Outcomes reported|Missing outcomes|Subgroup selectivity|Publication bias indicators|
|-------------------|-----------------|----------------|--------------------|---------------------------|

-----

E. REPLICATION STATUS

1. Has the finding been directly replicated?
- How many direct replication attempts exist?
- What proportion succeeded?
- Were the replications pre-registered and adequately powered?
- Were the replications conducted by independent labs?
1. Has the finding been conceptually replicated?
- Do meta-analyses support the effect?
- Are meta-analyses author-run or independent?
- Do meta-analyses show evidence of publication bias (funnel plot asymmetry, p-curve skew)?
1. Effect size trajectory:
- Has the reported effect size shrunk across successive replications?
- What is the current best estimate of the true effect size?
- Does the current best estimate support the downstream claims built on the original finding?

Required table:

|Direct replications|Success rate|Pre-registered reps|Independent reps|Meta-analytic estimate|Effect shrinkage|
|-------------------|------------|-------------------|----------------|----------------------|----------------|

-----

F. DATA AND CODE AVAILABILITY

1. Were the data made publicly available?
1. Was the analysis code made publicly available?
1. Has anyone attempted a reanalysis of the original data?
- If yes: what did the reanalysis find?
1. Are there known data irregularities, anomalies, or concerns?
- Has the paper been flagged on PubPeer or by post-publication peer review?
- Have any authors issued corrections or expressions of concern?

Required table:

|Data available|Code available|Reanalysis attempted|Reanalysis result|PubPeer flags|Corrections issued|
|--------------|--------------|--------------------|-----------------|-------------|------------------|

H. INTERNAL VALIDITY VERDICT

Classify the finding using this scale:

A. Strong — pre-registered, adequately powered, replicated by independent labs, effect stable across replications, data available
B. Acceptable — minor design limitations, replicated in spirit if not directly, effect size plausible
C. Weak — underpowered or unreplicated, effect size possibly inflated, design concerns present
D. Suspect — multiple internal validity problems, non-replicated or failed replication, effect size likely inflated
E. Invalid — failed replication in adequately powered independent studies, p-hacking or HARKing evidence, finding likely artifact of analytic choices
F. Cannot assess — insufficient reporting to evaluate

Then answer:

1. Is the foundational finding likely to be real?
1. If it is real, is its magnitude sufficient to support the downstream claims built on it?
1. What is the best current estimate of the true effect size?
1. What pre-registration and replication evidence would upgrade this verdict?
1. What is the decisive test that has not been run?

-----

I. INTEGRATION WITH CONSTRUCT VALIDITY AUDIT

After completing this audit, note:

1. If the finding is suspect or invalid (D/E), the construct validity problem is secondary — the foundational claim is doubly weak.
1. If the finding is acceptable or strong but the construct validity fails, the study may be internally valid but externally overclaimed.
1. If both internal validity and construct validity fail, note the compounding failure: the study may not measure what it claims AND the finding may not be real.

The decisive-test sentence should integrate both:

“The foundational finding [is/is not] likely to be real based on current replication evidence, AND the construct being measured [does/does not] validly represent the downstream claim — meaning the policy/institutional deployment rests on [a real but overclaimed finding / an unreliable finding being overclaimed / both].”

-----

J. FINAL ONE-PARAGRAPH SUMMARY

Write one paragraph summarizing:

- Whether the foundational finding is likely real
- The current best estimate of the true effect size
- The replication status
- The internal validity verdict
- How internal validity failures compound or modify the construct validity assessment
- What the decisive test would look like

This paragraph should be direct, evidence-based, and free of unnecessary reassurance language.

-----

REMINDER

This prompt audits the finding itself — whether the result is real and stable — before asking what it proves.

A finding that replicates cleanly but is overclaimed has one problem.
A finding that doesn’t replicate has a different problem.
A finding that doesn’t replicate AND is overclaimed is doubly invalid.

The toolkit now covers both.

You may begin with suspicion.
You may not end with suspicion.

Start wherever the anomaly appears.
Finish with receipts.
