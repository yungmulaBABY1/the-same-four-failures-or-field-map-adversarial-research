Field Examples — Detailed / Semi-Detailed

1. Nutrition Science

Core Claim
Dietary saturated fat causes cardiovascular disease, and low-fat/high-carbohydrate diets are optimal for population health.

The Four Failures

1. Untested Premise Recycled Through Citation
    The low-fat dietary model became institutional consensus before long-term controlled outcome evidence fully established the causal chain from saturated fat reduction to reduced cardiovascular events.
2. Proxy Problem
    LDL cholesterol is used as a proxy for cardiovascular event risk. LDL is measurable and clinically relevant, but it is not itself a heart attack, stroke, or death. The seed-oil debate has a related proxy issue: linoleic acid intake is measured more easily than downstream oxidative stress or inflammatory mechanisms.
3. Level-of-Analysis Error
    Population-level epidemiological associations are treated as causal mechanisms for individuals. Confounds such as sugar intake, ultra-processed food, sedentary behavior, and broader lifestyle patterns are difficult to isolate.
4. Ratchet Mechanism
    Dietary guidelines reshape the food industry; low-fat processed foods proliferate; obesity and metabolic disease rise; then the original dietary model is defended while alternative explanations are dismissed as contrarian or industry-funded.

Domain Experts Who Could Challenge It
Clinical nutritionists, metabolic researchers, physicians with large patient populations, and dietitians observing patient outcomes that diverge from guideline predictions.

Decisive Test
Long-term randomized trials comparing high-saturated-fat and low-saturated-fat diets using actual cardiovascular events as outcomes, while properly controlling carbohydrate and sugar intake.

Source Status
High confidence in the existence of the dispute; partly tested, but many trials use surrogate endpoints or confound multiple dietary variables.

2. Implicit Bias / DEI Research

Core Claim
Implicit bias, as measured by the IAT, predicts real-world discriminatory behavior, and implicit bias training reduces discrimination.

The Four Failures

1. Untested Premise Recycled Through Citation
    The assumption that IAT scores meaningfully predict discriminatory behavior became foundational before being established at the behavioral level.
2. Proxy Problem
    The IAT measures reaction-time differences in categorization tasks. It does not directly measure hiring, policing, medical treatment, promotion, or classroom decisions.
3. Level-of-Analysis Error
    Brief lab tasks are exported into claims about real-world institutional behavior under very different conditions: accountability, incentives, deliberation, feedback, and legal constraints.
4. Ratchet Mechanism
    Implicit bias training becomes mandatory across corporations, schools, police departments, and government agencies. Weak or null behavioral outcome findings do not reverse the intervention; instead, newer DEI variants replace older ones.

Domain Experts Who Could Challenge It
HR professionals, organizational psychologists, managers, social psychologists familiar with IAT validity debates, and people who have actually implemented hiring/promotion systems.

Decisive Test
Large-scale pre-registered RCTs of implicit bias training with actual behavioral outcomes measured over 12+ months: hiring decisions, promotions, discipline, evaluations, patient outcomes, or police decisions.

Source Status
High confidence that the proxy-validity critique exists in mainstream psychology; the contested question is how much predictive value remains and whether training changes behavior.

3. Education Psychology — Reading Wars / Learning Styles / Growth Mindset

A. Reading Wars: Whole Language vs. Phonics

Core Claim
Children learn to read naturally through exposure to meaningful text; explicit phonics instruction is unnecessary or harmful.

Failures

1. Untested Premise
    Whole-language assumptions became pedagogical doctrine despite weak evidence against explicit decoding instruction.
2. Proxy Problem
    Reading comprehension scores are used as proxies for the mechanism of reading acquisition.
3. Level-of-Analysis Error
    Observations about fluent readers recognizing whole words are extrapolated backward into claims about how beginners learn to read.
4. Ratchet
    Whole-language and balanced-literacy curricula persist even after phonics evidence becomes strong.

Decisive Test
This one has largely been run: systematic phonics produces better outcomes.

B. Learning Styles

Core Claim
Students learn better when instruction matches their preferred visual, auditory, or kinesthetic learning style.

Failures

1. Untested Premise
    Preference is treated as learning mechanism.
2. Proxy Problem
    Self-reported preference is treated as a proxy for differential learning outcome.
3. Level-of-Analysis Error
    Individual comfort or engagement is treated as evidence of improved learning.
4. Ratchet
    Teacher training and curriculum materials adopt learning-styles frameworks even after tests fail to support them.

Decisive Test
The test has been run: matching instruction to preferred learning style does not reliably improve learning.

4. Gender Medicine / Youth Gender Care

Core Claim
Social transition and medical intervention for gender-dysphoric youth improve long-term mental-health outcomes compared with watchful waiting, and puberty blockers are fully reversible.

The Four Failures

1. Untested Premise Recycled Through Citation
    Clinical confidence developed around protocols before long-term evidence could establish comparative outcomes for adolescent-onset gender dysphoria.
2. Proxy Problem
    Short-term self-reported wellbeing is used as proxy for long-term psychiatric outcomes, persistence/desistance, detransition, physical health, fertility, sexual function, and overall adult functioning.
3. Level-of-Analysis Error
    Adult gender-dysphoria data and older early-onset cohorts are applied to newer adolescent-onset populations with different sex ratios, comorbidity profiles, and developmental histories.
4. Ratchet Mechanism
    Guidelines are issued, adopted by institutions, and scaled. European evidence reviews and restrictions appear; some US institutions maintain the previous protocol and characterize reversals as political.

Domain Experts Who Could Challenge It
Pediatricians, psychiatrists, therapists, detransitioners, clinicians in European systems, and researchers doing long-term outcome follow-up.

Decisive Test
Prospective long-term comparison of medical intervention versus watchful waiting, measuring psychiatric and functional outcomes at 5, 10, and 20 years. RCTs raise genuine ethical issues, but the absence of long-term comparative data limits confidence.

Source Status
High confidence that the Cass Review and European reversals represent a major methodological challenge; contested by WPATH and major US medical associations.

5. Criminal Justice / Implicit Bias in Policing

Core Claim
Racial disparities in police use of force are primarily explained by officer implicit racial bias, and reducing bias through training will reduce disparate outcomes.

The Four Failures

1. Untested Premise Recycled Through Citation
    Disparity is often treated as evidence of officer-level bias before the encounter-level benchmark problem is solved.
2. Proxy Problem
    Stop rates, arrest rates, and use-of-force rates are used as proxies for bias. These are confounded by crime rates, reporting rates, deployment patterns, call-for-service patterns, and encounter rates.
3. Level-of-Analysis Error
    Population-level disparity is exported into claims about individual officer decision-making.
4. Ratchet Mechanism
    Implicit-bias training and policy reforms are mandated. When outcomes remain contested, the response is often more training or deeper policy intervention rather than decisive measurement.

Domain Experts Who Could Challenge It
Police officers, detectives, criminologists, statisticians, prosecutors, and people familiar with encounter-level data.

Decisive Test
Controlled encounter simulations with matched suspect behavior, plus field comparisons using proper benchmarks for exposure, crime context, and encounter conditions.

Source Status
Medium confidence; the benchmark problem is real and published, but the literature is contested.

6. Social Psychology — Replication Crisis

Core Claim
Many findings that shaped textbooks, public policy, and organizational training were assumed to be robust before pre-registered replication tested them.

The Four Failures

1. Untested Premise Recycled Through Citation
    Novel, counterintuitive findings were rewarded and cited before independent replication.
2. Proxy Problem
    Small lab effects in undergraduate samples are treated as proxies for real-world behavioral effects.
3. Level-of-Analysis Error
    One-session priming or attitude effects are extrapolated to durable behavior, professional outcomes, or population-scale policy.
4. Ratchet Mechanism
    Findings enter textbooks, training programs, and policy frameworks before replication. Failed replications often do not remove downstream interventions.

Domain Experts Who Could Challenge It
Statisticians, replication researchers, adjacent-field researchers, practitioners who implemented psychology-derived interventions and observed no effect.

Decisive Test
Pre-registered replications of claims that still support active interventions, using behavioral outcomes at 6–12 months rather than attitude surveys or lab tasks.

Source Status
High confidence; the field itself has documented the replication crisis.

7. Housing Economics / Urban Policy

Core Claim
Rent control protects tenants and improves affordability; restrictive zoning is the primary cause of housing shortages; homelessness is primarily caused by housing cost rather than mental illness, addiction, or behavioral factors.

The Four Failures

1. Untested Premise Recycled Through Citation
    Politically useful causal narratives are treated as settled even where mechanisms vary by city, population, and policy design.
2. Proxy Problem
    Short-term tenant stability is used as proxy for long-term affordability. Housing-unit counts are used as proxy for regional market dynamics. Twelve-month housing placement is used as proxy for long-term homelessness recovery.
3. Level-of-Analysis Error
    Benefits to current rent-controlled tenants are extrapolated to the whole housing market. Individual homelessness cases are extrapolated to population-level causation.
4. Ratchet Mechanism
    Rent control and Housing First models expand even where supply effects, mobility effects, or long-term recovery outcomes are mixed.

Domain Experts Who Could Challenge It
Real estate professionals, urban planners, shelter operators, social workers, economists, and people tracking local housing markets over time.

Decisive Test
Quasi-experimental housing-market comparisons with supply, mobility, affordability, and displacement outcomes; for homelessness, long-term trials comparing Housing First and treatment-first models over 3–10 years.

Source Status
High confidence for rent-control supply effects; medium/contested for Housing First long-term outcomes.

8. Macroeconomics — Specific Empirical Claims

Core Claim
Certain macroeconomic claims are treated as policy-grade despite depending heavily on model assumptions: minimum wage effects, fiscal multipliers, and inequality dynamics.

The Four Failures

1. Untested Premise Recycled Through Citation
    Claims such as “minimum wage increases above a threshold do not reduce employment” or “fiscal multipliers are consistently above 1” can become consensus through model-dependent literature.
2. Proxy Problem
    Natural experiments between adjacent jurisdictions are treated as substitutes for controlled experiments. Model-derived multiplier estimates are presented as empirical findings.
3. Level-of-Analysis Error
    Findings from specific historical or geographic contexts are generalized into universal policy claims.
4. Ratchet Mechanism
    Minimum-wage and stimulus policies scale on uncertain empirical claims, while uncertainty ranges are often undercommunicated.

Domain Experts Who Could Challenge It
Labor economists, business owners, HR professionals, fiscal-policy practitioners.

Decisive Test
True randomized minimum-wage assignment across jurisdictions is structurally impossible, but better natural experiments and longer employment tracking can narrow the question.

Source Status
Medium confidence; harder outsider challenge because domain expertise is often academic.

9. Sports Science — Specific Claims

Core Claim
Long-term CTE risk from repeated subconcussive impacts is well-established and quantifiable at population level; RED-S criteria identify a specific physiological syndrome with predictable outcomes.

The Four Failures

1. Untested Premise Recycled Through Citation
    Highly selected clinical or brain-donation findings are treated as population-risk evidence.
2. Proxy Problem
    Brain-donation samples are used as proxies for population prevalence. Self-reported energy availability is used as proxy for measured physiological RED-S markers.
3. Level-of-Analysis Error
    Findings from symptomatic donated brains are generalized to all contact-sport athletes.
4. Ratchet Mechanism
    Concussion protocols expand, contact sports are restructured, and youth participation changes based on risk estimates that may be inflated by selection bias.

Domain Experts Who Could Challenge It
Sports medicine physicians, neurologists, athletes, coaches, long-term cohort researchers.

Decisive Test
Prospective longitudinal cohorts of contact-sport athletes with matched controls and pre-specified neurological outcomes, not reliant on post-mortem brain donation.

Source Status
Medium confidence; important but specialized.

10. Developmental Psychology — Screen Time / ACE Scores

Core Claim
Smartphone use is a primary causal driver of the adolescent mental-health crisis; ACE scores are reliable universal predictors of adult health and behavior across populations.

The Four Failures

1. Untested Premise Recycled Through Citation
    Correlations between screen time and mental health, or ACE counts and adult outcomes, become causal policy narratives.
2. Proxy Problem
    “Screen time” is too crude: it does not distinguish social comparison, sleep disruption, algorithmic exposure, displacement of in-person interaction, pornography, bullying, or passive entertainment. ACE score count ignores severity, duration, timing, and context.
3. Level-of-Analysis Error
    Cross-sectional survey correlations are exported into causal claims. Kaiser Permanente ACE findings are generalized to diverse populations and cultural contexts.
4. Ratchet Mechanism
    Screen-time limits and ACE screening enter schools, pediatrics, and public-health practice before mechanisms are decisively specified.

Domain Experts Who Could Challenge It
Child psychiatrists, pediatricians, developmental psychologists, school counselors, researchers familiar with the Haidt vs. Orben/Przybylski debate.

Decisive Test
Longitudinal or randomized smartphone-access studies tracking adolescent outcomes over 2+ years; ACE validation across demographically diverse populations with pre-specified outcomes.

Source Status
Medium confidence; contested and highly tractable.

31. Sexual Violence Research and Male Victimization

Possible Failure Mode
The dominant framework was built around male-perpetrator/female-victim assumptions. Crime statistics are shaped by sex-differentiated reporting rates, while population surveys with methodologically equivalent measures show higher female perpetration and male victimization than official data suggests.

Potential Decisive Test
Population-level surveys using symmetrical measurement for victimization and perpetration across sex, combined with reporting-rate correction and institutional response data.

Named Leads in README
CDC NISVS; Anderson et al. 2021 “Millennial Shift”; Johnson et al. on male sexual victimization among college students; Hines & Saudino on multinational coercion predictors.
