#### 1. Nutrition Science

**The untested premise:** Dietary saturated fat causes cardiovascular disease. Low-fat, high-carbohydrate diets are optimal for population health.

**The proxy problem:** LDL cholesterol as a proxy for cardiovascular event risk. LDL is easy to measure; actual cardiovascular outcomes require long-term trials. The two are not equivalent. Seed oil debate: linoleic acid consumption measured; oxidative stress and inflammatory mechanisms not directly measured.

**The level-of-analysis error:** Epidemiological associations (populations that eat more saturated fat have higher rates of X) treated as causal mechanisms for individuals. Confounding variables (sugar consumption, ultra-processed food, sedentary behavior) routinely inadequately controlled.

**The ratchet:** Dietary guidelines issued, food industry restructures around them, low-fat processed foods proliferate, obesity and metabolic disease rates rise, the guidelines are not updated, alternative explanations are dismissed as industry-funded contrarianism.

**What a domain expert brings:** Clinical experience with patients whose outcomes diverge from guideline predictions. Knowledge of which confounds are routinely ignored. Familiarity with the specific studies the consensus rests on and their methodological limits.

**The decisive test that hasn’t been run:** Long-term randomized controlled trials comparing high-saturated-fat vs. low-saturated-fat diets with actual cardiovascular event outcomes, properly controlling for carbohydrate and sugar consumption. Most existing RCTs are short-term, use surrogate endpoints, or confound multiple dietary variables.

**Domain experts who could run this:** Clinical nutritionists, metabolic researchers, physicians with large patient populations, dietitians who have observed guideline-divergent outcomes.

|Source status               |                                                                                                                                                       |
|----------------------------|-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
|Confidence level            |High — multiple systematic reviews and the 2010 Siri-Tarino et al. meta-analysis document the weak link between saturated fat and cardiovascular events|
|Claim type                  |Documented; proxy critique is published in peer-reviewed literature                                                                                    |
|Best known contrary evidence|Some RCTs do show LDL reduction from saturated fat reduction; the dispute is whether LDL reduction translates to reduced cardiovascular events         |
|Decisive test status        |Partly run — PREDIMED and other trials exist but confound multiple dietary variables                                                                   |
|Source audit needed         |Specific guidelines citations; Ancel Keys Seven Countries Study methodology critique                                                                   |

#### 2. Implicit Bias / DEI Research

**The untested premise:** Implicit bias (as measured by the IAT) predicts discriminatory behavior in real-world settings. Implicit bias training reduces discrimination. Diversity mandates improve organizational performance.

**The proxy problem:** The IAT measures reaction time differences in categorization tasks. It is not a measure of discriminatory behavior. The correlation between IAT scores and actual discriminatory behavior in field settings is weak to negligible in meta-analyses (Oswald et al., 2015; Forscher et al., 2019). The proxy (IAT score) is routinely treated as equivalent to the construct (discriminatory behavior).

**The level-of-analysis error:** Lab findings (brief reaction time tasks) extrapolated to real-world hiring, policing, medical, and educational decisions. The contexts differ in stakes, deliberation time, accountability, and feedback — all of which affect whether bias operates.

**The ratchet:** Implicit bias training mandated at scale in corporations, government agencies, and schools. Meta-analyses showing no effect on behavior are published but do not reverse the mandates. The training industry expands. New variants (anti-racism training, DEI consulting) replace discredited versions without new evidence.

**What a domain expert brings:** HR professionals who have observed training effects (or lack thereof). Psychologists familiar with the IAT literature’s limitations. Organizational researchers who have studied diversity mandate outcomes. Managers who have run hiring processes.

**The decisive test that hasn’t been run:** Randomized controlled trials of implicit bias training with behavioral outcomes (actual hiring decisions, promotion rates, performance evaluations) measured at 12+ months. The existing literature is dominated by self-report and short-term lab measures.

|Source status               |                                                                                                                                     |
|----------------------------|-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
|Confidence level            |High — Oswald et al. (2015) meta-analysis and Forscher et al. (2019) are published peer-reviewed critiques of IAT predictive validity|
|Claim type                  |Documented; the IAT validity critique is mainstream within psychology                                                                |
|Best known contrary evidence|Greenwald et al. defend IAT predictive validity; some meta-analyses show small but nonzero correlations with behavior                |
|Decisive test status        |Not run — no large-scale pre-registered RCT of implicit bias training with behavioral outcome measures at 12+ months                 |
|Source audit needed         |Specific training program RCT literature; organizational HR outcome studies                                                          |

#### 3. Education Psychology — Reading Wars / Learning Styles / Growth Mindset

**Three sub-cases with the same structure:**

**Reading Wars (whole language vs. phonics):**

- Untested premise: children learn to read naturally through exposure to meaningful text; explicit phonics instruction is unnecessary or harmful.
- Proxy problem: reading comprehension scores on low-stakes assessments used as proxy for reading acquisition mechanism.
- Level-of-analysis error: observations of fluent readers (who do use whole-word recognition) extrapolated backward to explain how reading is acquired.
- Decisive test: the science of reading is actually settled — systematic phonics instruction produces better outcomes. The ratchet ran anyway: whole language became curricula, outcomes declined, the curricula persisted for decades.
- Domain experts: reading specialists, speech-language pathologists, neurologists familiar with reading acquisition research, teachers who noticed the gap between method and outcome.

**Learning Styles (VAK — Visual, Auditory, Kinesthetic):**

- Untested premise: students learn better when instruction matches their preferred learning style.
- Proxy problem: student self-report of preference used as proxy for differential learning outcome.
- The decisive test: has been run. Learning styles instruction does not produce better outcomes than mixed-modality instruction. The premise failed its test. The curriculum persisted anyway.
- Domain experts: classroom teachers who matched styles and observed no differential effect.

**Growth Mindset:**

- Untested premise: teaching students that intelligence is malleable (growth mindset) produces durable academic improvement.
- Proxy problem: self-report mindset measures used as proxy for academic outcome change.
- Level-of-analysis error: lab studies with brief interventions extrapolated to durable classroom effects.
- The decisive test: large-scale replications (including a pre-registered study of 36 schools) showed much smaller effects than original research. The intervention is still widely implemented.
- Domain experts: teachers who ran mindset programs and observed no durable effect. School psychologists familiar with the replication literature.

|Source status               |                                                                                                                                                                 |
|----------------------------|-----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
|Confidence level            |High for phonics (National Reading Panel 2000; settled); high for learning styles debunking (Pashler et al. 2008); medium for growth mindset replication failures|
|Claim type                  |Documented — all three sub-cases have published critiques or replication failures                                                                                |
|Best known contrary evidence|Growth mindset proponents argue implementation fidelity explains replication failures; some reading researchers still defend balanced literacy                   |
|Decisive test status        |Run for phonics and learning styles; growth mindset large-scale replications show substantially smaller effects than original (Sisk et al. 2018)                 |
|Source audit needed         |District-level whole language adoption persistence; specific growth mindset curriculum RCT citations                                                             |

#### 4. Gender Medicine / Youth Gender Care

**The untested premise:** Social transition and medical intervention (puberty blockers, cross-sex hormones) for gender-dysphoric youth produce better long-term mental health outcomes than watchful waiting. Puberty blockers are fully reversible.

**The proxy problem:** Short-term self-reported wellbeing used as proxy for long-term mental health outcomes. Gender dysphoria resolution rates, detransition rates, and long-term psychiatric outcomes rarely measured in the studies that informed clinical guidelines.

**The level-of-analysis error:** Studies of adult gender dysphoria (different population, different comorbidity profile, different persistence rates) used to inform treatment of adolescent-onset gender dysphoria. Historical patient populations (more male, earlier onset, higher persistence) used to justify treatment of a new, demographically different patient population (more female, adolescent onset, high psychiatric comorbidity).

**The ratchet:** WPATH guidelines issued based on weak evidence base, adopted by medical organizations, implemented at scale. European countries (Sweden, Finland, Denmark, UK — Cass Review) conducted systematic evidence reviews and reversed or restricted the protocols. US medical institutions maintained the protocols and characterized the reversals as politically motivated.

**The Cass Review (2024):** The most thorough systematic evidence review conducted. Found the evidence base for pediatric gender medicine “remarkably weak.” This is the equivalent of your Paper 2 — a methodological critique showing that what the literature claims to have established is not supported by the evidence it cites.

**What a domain expert brings:** Pediatricians, psychiatrists, and therapists who have treated this population and observed outcomes that diverge from the literature. Detransitioners whose experience the literature systematically undercounts. Clinicians in European systems that reversed course.

**The decisive test that hasn’t been run:** Prospective long-term RCTs comparing medical intervention vs. watchful waiting with psychiatric outcomes measured at 5, 10, and 20 years. The ethical objections to RCTs in this space are real, but the absence of the trial has not prevented confident clinical recommendations.

|Source status               |                                                                                                                                                       |
|----------------------------|-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
|Confidence level            |High — the Cass Review (2024) is a systematic evidence review commissioned by NHS England; Swedish, Finnish, Danish reversals are documented           |
|Claim type                  |Documented; the Cass Review is the strongest systematic evidence audit available                                                                       |
|Best known contrary evidence|WPATH and major US medical associations maintain that the evidence base supports current protocols; some studies show short-term wellbeing improvements|
|Decisive test status        |Not run — no long-term RCT exists; ethical objections are genuine but do not justify the confidence of current clinical recommendations                |
|Source audit needed         |Specific detransition rate studies; long-term follow-up data from Dutch protocol cohorts                                                               |

#### 5. Criminal Justice / Implicit Bias in Policing

**The untested premise:** Racial disparities in police use of force are primarily explained by officer implicit racial bias. Reducing bias through training will reduce disparate outcomes.

**The proxy problem:** Arrest rates, stop rates, and use-of-force rates used as proxies for bias. These rates are confounded by crime rates, reporting rates, deployment patterns, and encounter rates — none of which are equivalent to bias.

**The level-of-analysis error:** Population-level demographic disparities in outcomes treated as evidence of officer-level bias in individual encounters. The benchmark problem: what is the appropriate comparison population for measuring disparity? Different benchmark choices produce dramatically different disparity estimates.

**The ratchet:** Implicit bias training mandated for police departments. Use-of-force policies revised. Departments reduced in some jurisdictions. Crime rates rose in several cities following policy changes. The causal attribution is contested; the policy reversal was slow.

**What a domain expert brings:** Police officers and detectives who understand encounter dynamics. Criminologists familiar with the benchmark problem. Statisticians who have worked with crime data and understand the confound structure. Former prosecutors who have observed case selection.

**The decisive test that hasn’t been run:** Studies that measure officer-level bias in controlled encounter simulations with matched suspects and compare to actual field outcomes, with proper benchmarking. The existing literature has significant disagreement on findings depending on methodology.

|Source status               |                                                                                                                                                |
|----------------------------|------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
|Confidence level            |Medium — benchmark problem is documented (Knox et al. vs. Fryer et al. disagreement is published); training effectiveness critique is documented|
|Claim type                  |Contested — genuine methodological dispute in published literature; not author inference                                                        |
|Best known contrary evidence|Fryer (2016) found racial bias in non-lethal force but not lethal force; Knox et al. (2020) disputed the methodology                            |
|Decisive test status        |Partly run — natural experiments exist but benchmark choice determines findings; no consensus on correct benchmark                              |
|Source audit needed         |Post-2020 crime rate studies and causal attribution to policy changes; specific training RCT literature                                         |

#### 6. Social Psychology — The Replication Crisis

**The untested premise:** A large fraction of findings that built careers, textbooks, and policy recommendations were assumed to be robust without pre-registered replication. The incentive structure — rewarding novel, counterintuitive findings over replication — produced a literature where effect sizes were systematically inflated and many foundational results were not reproducible.

**The proxy problem:** Lab-based effect sizes on convenience samples (university undergraduates, brief tasks, low-stakes conditions) treated as proxies for real-world behavioral effects at population scale. Sample sizes too small to detect true effect sizes meant that published findings were often statistical artifacts of flexible analysis rather than genuine signals.

**The level-of-analysis error:** Short-term lab findings extrapolated to durable real-world behavior. Priming effects demonstrated in one session extrapolated to chronic attitudinal change. Stereotype threat effects in brief, explicit-priming lab tasks extrapolated to career-long performance suppression in professional environments.

**The ratchet:** Failed findings became textbook content, policy justifications, and training program foundations before replication was attempted. When the Reproducibility Project (2015) found fewer than 40% of 100 psychology studies replicated, some reform followed — but the textbooks, training programs, and policy frameworks built on failed findings largely remained. New variants of the same interventions replaced discredited versions without new evidence.

**What a domain expert brings:** Researchers in adjacent fields who noticed findings didn’t match observations. Statisticians who identified analytical flexibility and p-hacking. Psychologists who attempted independent replication and failed. Practitioners who implemented interventions derived from failed findings and observed no effect.

**The decisive test that hasn’t been run:** Systematic pre-registered replication of the specific findings that currently underpin active policy interventions — implicit bias training, growth mindset curricula, stereotype threat interventions — with behavioral outcome measures at 6 and 12 months. Not attitude surveys. Not lab tasks. Actual behavior in actual professional or educational environments.

|Source status               |                                                                                                                                    |
|----------------------------|------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
|Confidence level            |High — Reproducibility Project (2015), Many Labs replication studies, and individual high-profile replication failures are published|
|Claim type                  |Documented; this is the field’s own internal crisis, extensively published                                                          |
|Best known contrary evidence|Some original authors argue methodological differences explain replication failures; some findings have replicated                  |
|Decisive test status        |Run — the Reproducibility Project is the decisive test for the general claim; specific intervention replications remain incomplete  |
|Source audit needed         |Current textbook and policy citation status of failed findings; specific training program adoption records                          |

#### 7. Housing Economics / Urban Policy

**The untested premise:** Rent control protects tenants and improves housing affordability. Restrictive zoning is the primary cause of housing shortages. Homelessness is primarily caused by housing cost rather than mental illness, addiction, or behavioral factors.

**The proxy problem:** Short-term tenant stability used as proxy for long-term housing affordability. Local housing unit counts used as proxy for housing market dynamics that operate regionally.

**The level-of-analysis error:** Effects of rent control on current tenants extrapolated to effects on the housing market overall. Individual case studies of homelessness extrapolated to population-level causal claims.

**The ratchet:** Rent control expanded in multiple jurisdictions. Evidence of reduced housing supply and long-term affordability damage is documented but does not reverse the policies. Homelessness interventions organized around Housing First (provide housing unconditionally) show mixed long-term outcomes but the model expands.

**What a domain expert brings:** Real estate professionals who have observed market dynamics. Urban planners who have implemented zoning reform. Social workers and shelter operators who have observed the population and outcomes. Economists who have studied the natural experiments.

**The decisive test that hasn’t been run:** Longitudinal randomized or quasi-experimental studies comparing housing market outcomes (supply, affordability, mobility) in jurisdictions with and without rent control, with proper controls for underlying demand. For homelessness: randomized controlled trials comparing Housing First with treatment-first models, measuring long-term housing stability, mental health outcomes, and substance use at 3, 5, and 10 years — not just 12-month placement rates.

|Source status               |                                                                                                                                       |
|----------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
|Confidence level            |High for rent control supply effects (Diamond et al. 2019 Stanford study is well-regarded); medium for Housing First long-term outcomes|
|Claim type                  |Documented for rent control; contested for Housing First long-term claims                                                              |
|Best known contrary evidence|Some Housing First studies show sustained housing stability at 2 years; rent control advocates cite tenant stability benefits          |
|Decisive test status        |Partly run — Diamond et al. is close to a natural experiment; Housing First RCTs exist but long-term outcome data is limited           |
|Source audit needed         |Homelessness causation research — behavioral vs. housing cost — requires careful sourcing                                              |

#### 8. Macroeconomics — Specific Empirical Claims

**The untested premise(s):** Minimum wage increases above a threshold do not reduce employment. Fiscal multipliers are consistently above 1 in most economic conditions. Rising wealth inequality is primarily driven by returns to capital exceeding growth rates as a universal law.

**The proxy problem:** Natural experiment comparisons between adjacent jurisdictions used as proxies for controlled experiments — but jurisdictions differ in ways that confound the comparison. Model-derived multiplier estimates presented as empirical findings rather than model outputs sensitive to assumed parameters.

**The level-of-analysis error:** Findings from specific historical episodes or geographic contexts extrapolated to general policy claims. Piketty’s r > g derived from specific historical periods and presented as a near-universal economic law.

**The ratchet:** Minimum wage policy expanded at federal and state levels based on contested empirical claims. Fiscal stimulus programs scaled on multiplier estimates with wide uncertainty ranges not communicated to policymakers.

**What a domain expert brings:** Economists working in labor markets who have observed employment responses. Business owners and HR professionals who have made employment decisions in response to minimum wage changes. Practitioners in fiscal policy who have observed multiplier failures.

**The decisive test that hasn’t been run:** True randomized assignment of minimum wage levels across jurisdictions with matched controls and employment outcome tracking at 2 and 5 years — structurally impossible at scale, but better natural experiments exist and have not been fully exploited.

**Note:** Macroeconomics is harder to challenge from outside because the domain expertise required is itself largely academic. The failure modes are present but the outsider advantage is smaller than in clinical or domain-practice fields.

#### 9. Sports Science — Specific Claims

**The untested premise(s):** Long-term CTE risk from repeated subconcussive impacts is well-established and quantifiable at the population level. Relative energy deficiency in sport (RED-S) diagnostic criteria accurately identify a specific physiological syndrome with predictable clinical outcomes.

**The proxy problem:** CTE prevalence in brain donation studies — a heavily selected sample of individuals with behavioral symptoms — used as proxy for CTE prevalence in the broader population of contact sport athletes. Self-report of energy availability used as proxy for measured physiological markers of RED-S.

**The level-of-analysis error:** Selection bias in brain donation studies (families donate brains specifically because of behavioral changes) produces prevalence estimates that cannot be generalized to the full population of contact sport athletes. Clinical case series extrapolated to population risk estimates.

**The ratchet:** Concussion protocols expanded, contact sports restructured, youth participation declined — based on risk estimates that may be substantially inflated by selection bias in the foundational research.

**What a domain expert brings:** Sports medicine physicians who have tracked long-term outcomes in large patient populations. Neurologists who understand the methodological limits of the brain donation study design. Athletes and coaches who have observed protocol effects.

**The decisive test that hasn’t been run:** Prospective longitudinal cohort studies of contact sport athletes with matched non-athlete controls, tracking neurological outcomes with pre-specified measures, without reliance on post-mortem brain donation samples.

#### 10. Developmental Psychology — Screen Time / ACE Scores

**The untested premise(s):** Smartphone use is a primary causal driver of the adolescent mental health crisis. ACE (Adverse Childhood Experiences) scores are reliable universal predictors of adult health and behavioral outcomes across diverse populations.

**The proxy problem:** Screen time (hours per day) used as proxy for the specific mechanisms — social comparison, sleep disruption, displacement of in-person interaction, algorithmic content exposure — that might actually drive harm. ACE score (count of adverse events) used as proxy for the severity, duration, and developmental timing of adversity, which are the variables that actually predict outcomes.

**The level-of-analysis error:** Correlation between screen time and mental health symptoms in cross-sectional surveys extrapolated to causal claims. ACE study conducted on Kaiser Permanente patients (predominantly white, middle-class, insured adults) extrapolated to universal developmental predictions across diverse populations and cultural contexts.

**The ratchet:** Screen time limits mandated in schools and pediatric guidance issued based on effect sizes Orben and Przybylski showed were comparable to wearing eyeglasses or eating potatoes. ACE screening embedded in pediatric and public health practice based on population-specific findings applied universally.

**What a domain expert brings:** Child and adolescent psychiatrists who have observed the mental health crisis and its correlates. Pediatricians who have implemented ACE screening and observed its predictive validity in their patient population. Researchers familiar with the Orben/Przybylski reanalysis and the Haidt vs. Przybylski debate.

**The decisive test that hasn’t been run:** Randomized controlled trials assigning adolescents to different levels of smartphone access with mental health outcomes tracked over 2+ years. Validation of ACE predictive utility across demographically diverse populations with pre-specified outcome measures.

## Extended Inquiry List: 20 Additional Fields Worth Investigating

*These fields show similar failure mode signatures but have not been fully analyzed here. They are starting points for domain experts who recognize their own area in the generalized chain. No full structure is provided — that is the work of the domain expert who knows the field from the inside.*

*For each field: ask whether the untested premise, proxy problem, level-of-analysis error, and ratchet are present. Use the diagnostic prompt in Section 8. Run the analysis with Grok first.*

-----

- **Climate Policy Modeling and Net-Zero Mandates** — Integrated assessment models (IAMs) with wide uncertainty ranges presented as policy-grade precision; damage function assumptions embedded as inputs rather than derived as findings; cost-benefit analyses that are highly sensitive to discount rate choices rarely disclosed to policymakers.
- **Foreign Aid Effectiveness Research** — Macro-level correlations between aid flows and development outcomes used to justify specific program designs; limited RCT evidence generalized to non-RCT contexts; institutional capture of evaluation research by organizations whose funding depends on positive findings.
- **Corporate ESG Scoring** — Proprietary scoring methodologies with low inter-rater reliability treated as objective measures of environmental and social performance; correlation between ESG score and financial performance used to claim causation; greenwashing detection capability overstated.
- **Vaccine Hesitancy and Public Health Messaging** — Survey-based hesitancy measures used as proxies for actual vaccination behavior; messaging intervention effects in lab settings extrapolated to population-level uptake; trust erosion dynamics systematically understudied relative to compliance promotion.
- **Gender Pay Gap Explanations** — Raw pay gap (unadjusted for occupation, hours, experience, role) used as the headline figure in policy advocacy; adjusted gap findings published in the economics literature rarely reaching the same policy prominence; self-selection, risk tolerance, and negotiation behavior understudied relative to discrimination.
- **Affirmative Action in Employment and Promotions** — Representation metrics used as proxies for discrimination; mismatch effects (placing candidates in roles for which they are underprepared) understudied; long-term career outcome data for beneficiaries rarely tracked against counterfactual.
- **Universal Basic Income Pilot Studies** — Short-term pilot findings (12-24 months, volunteer or lottery populations) extrapolated to long-term macro effects of universal implementation; labor supply effects in pilots confounded by pilot-specific psychology (knowing the program will end); general equilibrium effects impossible to capture in small pilots.
- **Mindfulness and Corporate Wellness Programs** — Short-term self-report wellbeing improvements used as proxies for durable mental health, productivity, and retention outcomes; effect sizes in controlled trials substantially larger than observed effects in organizational rollouts; billion-dollar industry built on evidence base that does not support enterprise-scale ROI claims.
- **Social Media Addiction and Mental Health Claims** — Platform engagement metrics used as proxies for addiction (a clinical construct with specific diagnostic criteria); correlation between heavy use and poor mental health outcomes does not establish causation or direction; alternative explanations (pre-existing mental health conditions driving heavy use) understudied.
- **Cancel Culture / Deplatforming Effectiveness** — Anecdotal case studies used to make general claims about prevalence and impact; no agreed measurement framework for what constitutes cancellation; claimed chilling effects on speech unmeasured at population level; claimed harm reduction from deplatforming unmeasured against counterfactual.
- **Restorative Justice Programs in Criminal Justice** — Recidivism outcomes in small, well-resourced pilots extrapolated to scaled implementation; selection effects (programs administered to lower-risk offenders) understudied; victim satisfaction as primary outcome measure rather than victim safety or recidivism.
- **School Choice and Charter School Outcome Research** — Highly politicized literature on both sides; selection effects on both student population and school quality difficult to disentangle; lottery-based RCTs exist but are not generalizable to all charter contexts; long-term outcomes understudied relative to test score gains.
- **Pharmaceutical Opioid Crisis Policy Responses** — Prescription monitoring and restriction policies evaluated on prescription rates (proxy) rather than on overdose rates (actual outcome); substitution to illicit opioids understudied in early policy evaluation; crackdown-and-harm-shift dynamic well-documented but slow to affect policy design.
- **Central Bank Monetary Policy Effectiveness Claims** — Structural VAR models used to identify causal effects of monetary policy with identification assumptions that are themselves contested; forward guidance effectiveness claims based on models that assume rational expectations; distributional effects of quantitative easing understudied relative to aggregate claims.
- **Cryptocurrency and Decentralized Finance Regulation** — Financial stability risk estimates for crypto markets extrapolated from traditional finance models that do not account for crypto-specific dynamics; consumer harm claims based on anecdote and complaint data rather than representative sampling; innovation cost of regulation systematically unquantified.
- **AI Ethics and Algorithmic Bias Research** — Bias defined inconsistently across papers (statistical parity vs. equal opportunity vs. calibration — mathematically incompatible); audit findings on specific systems extrapolated to AI systems generally; disparate impact findings used to infer discriminatory intent without evidence of mechanism.
- **Pandemic Lockdown and School Closure Studies** — Observational studies with no counterfactual for infection trajectory; learning loss estimates based on test score proxies rather than long-term educational outcome measures; mental health costs of closures systematically excluded from cost-benefit analyses that informed policy.
- **Diversity Quotas on Corporate Boards** — Board diversity correlation with firm performance treated as causal in policy advocacy; endogeneity (better-performing firms more likely to adopt diversity initiatives) poorly controlled; mechanism (how diversity produces performance improvement) rarely specified or tested.
- **Reparations Policy Research** — Preliminary economic estimates of reparations cost and impact based on models with wide assumption ranges presented as policy-grade projections; historical harm quantification methodologically contested; long-term behavioral and social effects entirely speculative but cited as established.
- **Pronoun Policy and Workplace Language Mandates** — Self-report wellbeing improvements in affirming environments used as proxies for measurable mental health outcomes; no RCT evidence for mandatory policy vs. voluntary cultural change; compliance cost and productivity effects in organizational settings unmeasured; legal and free-speech tradeoffs excluded from framing of research questions.

-----

- **Sexual Violence Research and Male Victimization** — The dominant research infrastructure was built around a foundational premise (sexual violence is primarily male perpetration / female victimization) that population surveys with methodologically equivalent measurement of both directions have substantially complicated. Official crime statistics capture reported incidents heavily skewed by reporting rates that differ by sex of victim — male victims report at dramatically lower rates for documented social and institutional reasons. Population survey data (CDC NISVS; Millennial Shift studies — Anderson et al. 2021, Journal of Sex Research) finds substantially higher rates of female perpetration and male victimization than criminal justice data suggests, including a documented generational convergence in sexual coercion rates. The institutional framework built around the original premise — Violence Against Women Act, dedicated funding structures, mandatory reporting frameworks — does not measure male victimization equivalently. Researchers who study male victimization report significant career friction. The decisive test — population surveys with pre-specified, methodologically equivalent measurement of perpetration and victimization across both sexes — has been partially run and produces results that diverge sharply from the criminal justice baseline. Key sources: APA (2014) men’s victimization study; Struckman-Johnson et al. on male sexual victimization among college students; Hines & Saudino multinational coercion predictors study; Anderson et al. (2021) Millennial Shift replication.
