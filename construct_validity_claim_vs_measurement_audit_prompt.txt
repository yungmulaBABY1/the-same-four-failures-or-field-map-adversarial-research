CONSTRUCT-VALIDITY AUDIT PROMPT:

CLAIM VS. MEASUREMENT

Purpose:

Use this prompt to audit one paper, study, report, policy document, or institutional claim before making any broad claim about a field.

Do not evaluate whether the authors are good people.

Do not infer private motive.

Do not summarize the source politely.

Do not defer to prestige, peer review, journal status, citation count, institutional authority, or the social acceptability of the conclusion.

Your task is narrower:

Compare what the source actually measured to what the source claims, and to what later citations, institutions, or public arguments use it to prove.

The point is not to decide whether the field is good or bad.

The point is to test whether the measurement supports the claim.

-----

HOW TO USE THIS PROMPT

Paste the relevant source material below:

- Abstract
- Methods
- Measures / variables
- Sample / population
- Results
- Conclusion
- Any key tables or quoted claims
- If available: how later papers, institutions, or public commentators cite this source

Then provide:

1. The contested claim this source is being used to support.
1. The field or debate where the source is being used.
1. Any domain-specific concern you want tested.

AUDIT INSTRUCTIONS

You are auditing construct validity.

Do not begin by balancing the source with generic praise.

Do not add reassurance clauses unless they are doing real evidentiary work.

Do not use phrases like “it is important to acknowledge” or “this does not invalidate” unless the qualification is necessary to prevent factual overstatement.

Do not soften “invalid proxy” into “limited proxy” if the measurement cannot support the claim.

Do not treat peer review, citation count, or institutional adoption as evidence that the construct was measured correctly.

Answer the following sections.

-----

1. EXACT CLAIM EXTRACTION

Identify three levels of claim:

A. Strongest claim made by the source itself:

[Extract the strongest claim the authors make.]

B. Weaker defensible claim actually supported by the data:

[State the narrower claim the study may support.]

C. Inflated claim made by later citations, institutions, media, or public discourse:

[State how the source is being used beyond its evidence.]

Then answer:

- Are A, B, and C the same claim?
- Where does inflation occur?
- Does the source itself overclaim, or is the overclaim mainly downstream?

-----

1. MEASUREMENT EXTRACTION

State exactly what was measured.

Include:

- Sample size
- Population studied
- Recruitment method
- Task, instrument, proxy, survey, administrative measure, or dataset used
- Time scale
- Outcome variable
- Control variables
- Exclusions
- What was not measured

Then answer:

- Is the measured variable direct evidence of the claimed construct?
- Or is it a proxy?
- If it is a proxy, how close is the proxy to the target construct?

-----

1. CLAIM VS. MEASUREMENT TABLE

Create a table with these columns:

Measured variable | Claimed construct | Are they the same? | Gap | Severity | Notes

Severity options:

- No meaningful gap
- Minor limitation
- Moderate proxy gap
- Major construct mismatch
- Fatal proxy failure for the claim being made

Use the strongest severity justified by the evidence.

Do not understate the severity to sound balanced.

-----

1. LEVEL-OF-ANALYSIS CHECK

At what level was the evidence collected?

Examples:

- Laboratory task
- Survey respondents
- Self-report
- Administrative records
- Clinical sample
- Amateur population
- Intermediate-skill population
- Elite population
- Institutional metric
- Real-world field outcome
- Longitudinal behavioral data
- Natural experiment

At what level is the claim being applied?

Examples:

- Elite performance
- Professional behavior
- Population-level policy
- Clinical guideline
- Institutional intervention
- Group outcome gap
- Long-term social outcome
- Mechanistic causal claim

Then answer:

- What assumptions are required to bridge the evidence level to the claim level?
- Are those assumptions tested?
- Are they merely implied?
- Would a domain expert accept the bridge as valid?

-----

1. CONFOUND AUDIT

Identify obvious confounds.

Ask:

- What variables could explain the measured outcome besides the proposed explanation?
- What confounds would a practitioner, clinician, performer, or domain expert immediately notice?
- Did the authors control for these confounds?
- Did the authors acknowledge them?
- Did later citations forget the confounds?
- Would the omitted confounds change the interpretation?

Classify each confound:

Confound | Domain obvious? | Controlled? | Acknowledged? | Impact if omitted

Impact options:

- Low
- Moderate
- High
- Fatal for the strong claim

-----

1. ECOLOGICAL VALIDITY CHECK

Ask:

- Does the task resemble the real-world phenomenon being explained?
- Does the sample resemble the population the claim is applied to?
- Does the time scale match the real process?
- Does the measured behavior occur under realistic pressure, incentives, constraints, or selection?
- Is the study measuring performance, stated belief, compliance, perception, or institutional output?
- Are those being conflated?

Verdict:

- High ecological validity
- Moderate ecological validity
- Low ecological validity
- Ecologically valid only for a narrower claim
- Not ecologically valid for the claim being made

-----

1. DECISIVE-TEST CHECK

What test would directly measure the claimed construct?

Answer:

- What would the decisive test measure?
- What population would it study?
- What controls would be required?
- What outcome would support the source’s claim?
- What outcome would weaken or falsify the source’s claim?
- Does the current source run that test?
- If not, does the field acknowledge that the decisive test has not been run?

Then state:

“The decisive test would be: [ONE-SENTENCE TEST SPECIFICATION].”

-----

1. CROSS-REANALYSIS OBJECT CHECK

If two or more independent reanalyses, replications, or critiques reach different conclusions about the source, before treating them as agreeing or conflicting, check what each one actually tested.

Ask:

- Did each reanalysis test the same object — the full result, a single component, a specific subgroup, a specific time window?
- Or did they test different objects that only sound like the same dispute?
- Is apparent disagreement actually a scope mismatch rather than a genuine unresolved question?
- Is apparent convergence masking that the harder or more specific version of the question was never independently re-tested?

State plainly: for each pair of reanalyses in tension, are they actually contradicting each other, or talking past each other?

Do not report a dispute as “resolved” or “settled” if the reanalyses that appear to resolve it tested a narrower or different claim than the one in question. Do not report a dispute as “ongoing” if it is actually two non-competing findings about different objects.

-----

1. CITATION-USE AUDIT

If later papers, institutions, or commentators cite this source:

Ask:

- Are they citing the actual finding?
- Are they citing an inflated version of the finding?
- Are they using the source as evidence, or as a premise?
- Does the source become stronger through repetition than it was in the original methods?
- Does the citation network preserve the source’s limitations?
- Does the network drop the limitations and retain only the conclusion?

Classify the downstream use:

- Accurate citation
- Slightly inflated citation
- Major inflation
- Citation laundering
- Premise laundering
- Unsupported consensus construction

-----

1. IGNORED-LITERATURE CHECK

Ask:

- What adjacent literatures should have been cited?
- What domain-specific literatures are directly relevant?
- What practitioner knowledge would identify obvious confounds?
- Did the source cite those literatures?
- Did later reviews or hubs cite them?
- Are there contrary findings that were available but structurally ignored?

Create a table:

Ignored literature / evidence | Why it matters | Was it cited? | Effect of omission

Effect options:

- Minor context loss
- Moderate weakness
- Major evidentiary gap
- Fatal omission for the strong claim

-----

1. SOURCE-STATUS LABELS

Assign labels to each type of evidence in the source.

Use these labels:

- Primary empirical evidence
- Secondary synthesis
- Systematic review / meta-analysis
- Policy document / guideline
- Proxy evidence
- Weak proxy
- Conceptual / theoretical claim
- Citation-laundered premise
- Practitioner / domain observation
- Public reporting / journalism
- Community observation / anecdotal lead
- Author inference
- Unverified source lead

Then state:

- Which claims are directly measured?
- Which claims are inferred?
- Which claims are speculative?
- Which claims require a source audit before publication?

-----

1. VERDICT

Choose one primary verdict:

A. Valid evidence for the claim

B. Partial evidence with clear limits

C. Useful for a narrower claim, invalid for the stronger claim

D. Major proxy failure

E. Fatal construct-validity failure for the claim being made

F. Citation-laundered premise: weak original evidence inflated into consensus

G. Insufficient information to judge without the full source

Then explain the verdict in 3-6 sentences.

Do not hedge unless the evidence requires it.

Do not soften the verdict for comfort.

If the study is useful for a narrower claim, say so.

If it fails for the strong claim, say that directly.

If your confidence level or dispute-resolution language differs between this Verdict section and any earlier table or note, resolve the discrepancy here and state which is correct.

-----

1. INTERNAL CONSISTENCY CHECK

Before finalizing the verdict, reread this audit’s own sections for contradictory resolution language about the same dispute, finding, or reanalysis — for example, calling something “resolved” in one section and “contested” or “disputed” in another.

If found:

- State which characterization is accurate.
- State why the inconsistent phrasing occurred (e.g., borrowing the framing of one source without checking it against another section of this same audit).

Do not let a downstream section (e.g., a confound table entry or citation-use note) carry a more confident or more hedged resolution status than the Decisive-Test Check and Verdict sections actually support. All resolution-status language in this audit should agree with itself.

-----

REMINDER

Suspicion, practitioner frustration, political disagreement, and anecdotes can be valid discovery tools. They are often how domain experts notice that a literature does not match reality.

But they are not final evidence.

You may begin with vibes.

You may not end with vibes.

Start wherever the anomaly appears.

Finish with receipts.
