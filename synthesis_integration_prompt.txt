SYNTHESIS & INTEGRATION PROMPT

Purpose:
Use this prompt after completing two or more individual audits on the same paper, claim, or field. Its job is to integrate findings across prompts into one clear, prioritized, non-hedged overall assessment.

The individual prompts break things down. This prompt puts them back together — but as a compounding failure analysis, not a summary. The goal is a publishable verdict, not a balanced overview.

Run after: Construct Validity Audit, Internal Validity & Replication Audit, Citation Network Audit, Oppositional Counter-Audit, or any combination of these. The more prompts completed, the more compounding the synthesis can identify.

-----

ANTI-HEDGING RULE

Do not default to hedging or false balance.
Do not use phrases like “it’s complicated,” “both sides have merit,” “more research is needed,” or “the jury is still out” unless the evidence genuinely requires that level of uncertainty.
Prioritize analytical clarity over appearing neutral.
If the evidence is weak, say so plainly.
If the claim fails on multiple dimensions simultaneously, say so directly and explain how the failures compound.
Focus on what the audits actually revealed. Do not introduce new speculation.

SYNTHESIS INSTRUCTIONS

Work through the following steps. Be specific at each step. Do not produce vague generalizations.

-----

STEP 1: IDENTIFY THE MOST IMPORTANT FINDINGS

List the 2–4 strongest methodological or evidentiary problems identified across all audits. Do not treat every issue as equally significant. Prioritize by:

- Severity: does this problem fatally undermine the claim, or only weaken it?
- Centrality: does this problem affect the core claim or only a peripheral aspect?
- Irreparability: could the problem be fixed with more data, or is it structural?

For each problem, state:

- What it is
- Which audit identified it
- Why it matters for the core claim specifically

-----

STEP 2: HOW THESE PROBLEMS COMPOUND

Explain how the identified problems interact and reinforce each other. Be specific about the compounding mechanism.

Examples of compounding:

- A proxy failure means the study didn’t measure the right thing, AND the finding doesn’t replicate, meaning the wrong thing was measured unreliably — doubly invalid.
- A concentrated citation network means the weak foundational finding has been recycled without independent verification, AND the foundational study’s limitations are dropped as the claim travels downstream — the problem is structurally self-reinforcing.
- An off-label evidence transfer problem means the study was conducted in the wrong population, AND the normalization pathway was advocacy-driven rather than evidence-driven, AND the decisive test hasn’t been run — triply unjustified.

State the compounding in one or two sentences: “The [X failure] compounds the [Y failure] because…”

-----

STEP 3: SOURCE-STATUS ASSESSMENT

After reviewing all audit findings, identify what tier of evidence the load-bearing claims actually rest on.

Use the source-status hierarchy:

- Tier 1: Primary data
- Tier 2: High-quality pre-registered empirical research
- Tier 3: Systematic reviews / meta-analyses
- Tier 4: Individual peer-reviewed studies
- Tier 5: Guidelines and policy documents
- Tier 6: Practitioner evidence
- Tier 7: Journalism and secondary reporting
- Tier 8: Anecdotal / informal reports
- Tier 9: Author inference / speculation

State: “The load-bearing claims rest primarily on Tier [X] evidence. The institutional deployment requires Tier [Y] evidence. The gap is [small / moderate / large / fatal].”

Flag any tier laundering: where a low-tier claim has accumulated unearned authority by passing through progressively higher-tier citations without the original weakness being preserved.

-----

STEP 4: STRENGTH OF THE CORE CLAIM

State directly how well-supported the main claim is after considering all identified problems and the source-status assessment.

Answer these questions:

- Does the claim survive serious scrutiny?
- Which parts of the claim (if any) are adequately supported?
- Which parts fail?
- Is the failure a matter of degree (overclaimed but not baseless) or kind (the measurement doesn’t address the claim at all)?

Be direct. Do not hedge unless the evidence genuinely requires it.

-----

STEP 5: OVERALL VERDICT

Choose one of the following and justify the choice in 3–5 sentences:

A. The claim is well-supported by the evidence.
[The measurement matches the claim, the finding is reliable, and the downstream use is proportionate to the evidence.]

B. The claim is partially supported but overstated or weakly evidenced.
[Some evidence supports a narrower version of the claim, but the stronger version deployed in policy or public argument exceeds what the evidence supports.]

C. The claim lacks adequate support and relies on significant methodological weaknesses.
[The foundational measurement has serious proxy problems, replication failures, or citation laundering that prevent the claim from being treated as established.]

D. The claim is not supported and appears to rest on flawed foundations.
[The measurement does not address the claimed construct, the finding does not replicate, or the evidence base is circular and self-reinforcing rather than independently validated.]

Do not choose B when D is accurate. Do not soften the verdict to appear balanced.

-----

STEP 6: CONCISE CHALLENGE STATEMENT

Write one or two sentences that could be used in a published critique, a formal response, or a public thread. This should be:

- Direct and specific
- Suitable for formal or public use
- Free of hedging language
- Grounded in what the audits actually found

Format: “[The claim] rests on [specific methodological problem]. The decisive test — [what would actually settle the question] — has not been run.”

Or: “[Source] is cited as evidence that [claim]. The study actually shows [what it measured], which does not support [claim] because [specific gap].”

-----

STEP 7: WHAT WOULD CHANGE THIS VERDICT

State specifically what evidence would upgrade the verdict. Be precise — not “more research” but “a pre-registered prospective study in [population] measuring [outcome] over [timeframe] with [controls], replicated by independent labs.”

This is the decisive test specification. If no such test is conceivable, note that the claim may be unfalsifiable by design — which is itself a finding.

-----

REMINDER

You are integrating, not summarizing.
The individual prompts identified problems. This prompt asks which problems matter most, how they interact, and what the honest overall conclusion is.

A finding that replicates cleanly but is overclaimed has one problem.
A finding built on flawed data has a second problem.
A finding that doesn’t replicate has a third problem.
A claim that fails on construct validity, internal validity, and citation integrity simultaneously is not “complicated” — it is multiply invalid.

Say so directly.

You may begin with suspicion.
You may not end with suspicion.

Start wherever the anomaly appears.
Finish with receipts.
