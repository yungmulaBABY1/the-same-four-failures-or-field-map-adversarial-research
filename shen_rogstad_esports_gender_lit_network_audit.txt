EXECUTIVE VERDICT

Best formal label:
Citation-laundering network / premise-laundering hub, with high cartel-risk.

Not because every author secretly coordinated. The problem is structural: Shen’s MMO leveling-speed paper becomes a load-bearing premise, Rogstad becomes the review/amplification hub, and later papers continue citing the cluster while the directly relevant performance-science and MMO-social-dynamics literatures remain largely outside the main explanatory frame.

Core failure:

MMO character advancement speed
→ “no gender performance gap in gaming”
→ “physical attributes do not matter in esports”
→ cultural/gatekeeping explanations treated as sufficient for elite-tail absence.

That is a major claim mutation.

4. PROXY VERDICT ON SHEN

Primary verdict:
Fatal construct-validity failure for the strong downstream claim.

Shen et al. is not useless. It may say something about MMO progression under certain controls. But it does not measure:

- adversarial mechanical skill;
- reaction time under cognitive load;
- aiming or tracking;
- elite ladder performance;
- PvP output;
- high-rank competitive selection;
- role-specific professional output;
- sustained Tier-1 open performance;
- psychomotor skill under pressure;
- gamesense under adversarial conditions;
- tournament stability;
- open-bracket performance.

The study measures MMO progression speed. That is not the same construct as elite competitive gaming performance.

A metric can be behavioral and objective while still measuring the wrong construct.

-----

5. ADJACENT-FIELD NEGLECT

A. Ignored MMO social dynamics

The neglected MMO literature matters because Shen’s metric is socially mediated. MMO advancement is not a clean individual-performance measure if players can receive:

- routing help;
- group support;
- guild help;
- gear transfers;
- quest assistance;
- dungeon carries;
- raid carries;
- escorting through dangerous areas;
- crafting support;
- differential attention;
- courtship-driven help;
- generosity toward visible female players or female avatars.

Relevant available literature includes:
- Yee (2006): MMORPG demographics, relationships, motivations, gendered social patterns.
- Brehm (2013): gendered play and sexism in World of Warcraft.
- Fortim & de Moura Grando (2013): female MMO players, attention, courtship, and gendered treatment.
- Patrizio (2001): early EverQuest reporting on female avatars receiving more help, invitations, and generosity.
- Shen’s own prior MMO social-network work: directly relevant to the social architecture of MMOs.

Audit conclusion:
If a metric is socially mediated, and the social mediation is non-random by sex/avatar presentation, then raw leveling speed cannot be treated as clean individual performance.

B. Ignored performance science

The adjacent performance-science omission is even more damaging for elite esports claims. A literature claiming to explain sustained Tier-1 open performance should engage:

- spatial ability / mental rotation;
- reaction time;
- reaction time under cognitive load;
- dynamic visuomotor tracking;
- multiple-object tracking;
- temporal precision;
- deliberate practice;
- desirable difficulties;
- sports psychology;
- failure tolerance;
- competitive self-selection;
- elite-tail selection;
- skill acquisition under high-feedback conditions.

Relevant adjacent sources include:
- Voyer et al. (1995): sex differences in spatial ability / mental rotation.
- Der & Deary (2006): reaction time.
- Silverman (2006): reaction time meta-analysis.
- Mathew et al. (2020): dynamic visuomotor tracking accuracy.
- Trick et al. (2005): multiple-object tracking.
- Gur et al. (2012): neural connectivity architecture.
- Ericsson & Pool: deliberate practice framework.
- Bjork & Bjork: desirable difficulties / learning science.

Audit conclusion:
This adjacent literature does not prove the full biological-plus-funnel model by itself. But it absolutely means a literature claiming to explain elite esports absence cannot treat psychomotor, sports-science, and learning-science literatures as peripheral.

6. RECENT-PAPER BEHAVIOR

Recent papers still show the field leaning on the same cultural frame rather than running the decisive elite-performance test.

Observed recent behavior:
- Continued citation of Rogstad-style framing.
- Continued emphasis on women’s representation, harassment, identity, gendered participation, and esports culture.
- Little evidence of matched-rank telemetry, matched practice-volume analysis, or elite-tail psychomotor testing.
- Continued use of cultural explanations without direct measurement of the performance constructs that separate high-ranked amateurs from sustained Tier-1 open professionals.

Key downstream pattern:
A narrow MMO progression result gets cited or paraphrased as evidence for much broader claims, including claims that physical attributes or biological substrates do not matter for high performance in esports.

That is precisely the laundering move:
Shen measured MMO advancement speed.
Downstream claims treat it as evidence about gaming performance generally or esports high performance specifically.

9. MAIN MISSING DECISIVE TEST

The decisive test would be:

Sex-stratified telemetry at matched rank, matched role, matched practice volume, matched competitive intent, and matched genre, measuring reaction time under load, tracking accuracy, input precision, gamesense-relevant state updates, performance stability, and progression into sustained open competitive output.

Shen did not run that test.
Rogstad did not synthesize that test.
The downstream literature does not appear to have run it either.

10. DOMAIN-EXPERT RECEPTION FAILURE

One of the strongest points for the Shen-specific paper:

The player communities identified the core methodological objection immediately. They understood that MMO leveling speed is not a clean individual-performance metric because MMO progression is socially mediated.

Domain experts recognized:
- leveling speed is not performance;
- leveling speed can reflect route efficiency, social support, and assistance;
- play objectives vary massively;
- players can idle, craft, fish, socialize, raid, quest, or PvP;
- guild help and carries matter;
- visible female players and female avatars often receive non-random attention and support.

The academic network did not uptake this critique.

Why the critique failed to enter the network:
The paper appeared during the peak GamerGate / post-GamerGate period. Criticism from gamer communities was easily coded as reactionary hostility rather than domain-valid methodological critique.

Key reception failure:
The people with the ecological knowledge needed to identify the proxy problem were treated as morally suspect, while the academic literature absorbed the study as settled evidence.

Core line:
The field did not merely miss the confound. It dismissed the people who knew the confound existed.

Sharper line:
The field did not merely overlook domain expertise. It pathologized the population that possessed it.

-----

11. MALE-TO-FEMALE MMO BOOSTING AS OBVIOUS CONFOUND

The most obvious confound is the longstanding, widely recognized pattern in MMO ecology:

visible female player / female avatar
→ more attention
→ more whispers
→ more guild invites
→ more quest help
→ more gear help
→ more dungeon carries
→ faster progression.

This directly contaminates leveling speed.

This is not an obscure edge case. It is part of the social structure of MMOs:
- guilds;
- parties;
- gear economies;
- carries;
- dungeon groups;
- raids;
- quest routing;
- escorting;
- crafting assistance;
- courtship;
- status dynamics;
- male-to-female boosting.

Paper-safe wording:
The player critique was not crude backlash. It identified the central construct-validity problem. In MMOs, character advancement is not a clean individual-performance metric. Progression is socially mediated through guild access, dungeon groups, quest routing, gear transfers, escorting, crafting help, and informal power-leveling. These forms of assistance are not randomly distributed. Visible female players and female avatars have long received differential attention, courtship, generosity, and unsolicited help in MMO environments. Any study treating raw leveling speed as individual performance without measuring or controlling for those assistance pathways is not merely missing a minor covariate. It is measuring a socially contaminated progression outcome and calling it performance.

Sharper wording:
The irony is that the gamer communities dismissed as reactionary saw the problem immediately because they understood the medium. MMO leveling is not a solo psychometric test. It is embedded in guilds, groups, social attention, gear economies, quest routing, and status dynamics. Male-to-female boosting was not an edge case; it was one of the most obvious social facts of the genre. A study that failed to account for it did not simply overlook a variable. It mistook the social environment for an individual-performance measure.

SHORTEST POSSIBLE SUMMARY

Shen et al. (2016) measured MMO leveling speed, a socially mediated progression metric, and the field treated it as evidence about gaming performance generally. Rogstad then functioned as the amplification hub that stabilized the premise inside the esports gender literature. The central failure is not merely that Shen was weak. It is that a weak proxy became a load-bearing premise through citation laundering while the directly relevant MMO social-dynamics literature, performance-science literature, and domain-expert critique remained outside the citation network.
