FIELD DIAGNOSTIC: 44 QUESTIONS TO RUN AGAINST YOUR FIELD
A companion to “The Same Four Failures, Different Fields”
GitHub: https://github.com/yungmulaBABY1/the-same-four-failures-or-field-map-adversarial-research
Archive: DOI 10.5281/zenodo.20201226

-----

HOW TO USE THIS PROMPT

Option A — With an AI system (recommended first pass):
Paste this entire document into Grok or Claude. Add a one-paragraph description of your field
and the contested claim you are investigating. Ask the model to answer each question as
accurately as possible based on the published literature. Then compare the model’s answers
to your own domain knowledge. The gaps between what the model says and what you observe
are your entry points.

Use Grok for initial mapping — it engages contested empirical territory more directly.
Use Claude to structure and synthesize the findings into a coherent challenge.
Use ChatGPT only after the argument is stable — for adversarial stress-testing and
generating the strongest objections to your challenge. Do not use ChatGPT for the
initial diagnostic pass on politically sensitive fields (see the Critical Warning in
the main document).

Option B — With yourself:
Work through the questions honestly before investing significant time in building a challenge.
If you cannot answer yes to most of Section A and yes to most of Section B, the challenge
may not be right for your position or your field at this time.

-----

SECTION A: DOES THE FAILURE MODE EXIST?

If you answer yes to most of these questions, the failure mode is likely present.

ON THE CORE CLAIM

1. What is the field’s central claim? State it in one sentence.
2. Is this claim presented as an empirical finding or as a theoretical framework?
   Is there a meaningful difference between how it is stated and how it is treated?
3. When was this claim first established? What was the original study or paper?
   How large was the sample? What population was studied?
4. Has the original claim been directly replicated by independent researchers using
   pre-registered methods? What happened?
5. Is there a version of the claim that is well-supported (e.g. historical discrimination
   is real) and a version that is not (e.g. current outcome gaps are primarily caused by
   current discrimination)? Does the field distinguish between them?

ON EMPIRICAL STATUS

6. Does the field treat this claim as settled or as an active research question?
   What would it take for a researcher in this field to publish a paper challenging the claim?
7. Has the field specified what evidence would falsify the central claim?
   Is there a pre-specified disconfirmation condition?
8. Has the field’s main prediction been tested against a natural experiment or a
   long-running intervention? What was the result? Did the model update?
9. Are there adjacent fields or literatures that contradict or complicate the central
   claim? Does the dominant literature engage with them?

ON THE PROXY

10. What does the field actually measure? Describe the most common data sources
    and measurement instruments.
11. What does the field claim to explain? Name the target construct.
12. Are the measurement instruments and the target construct the same thing?
    If not, what is the gap between them?
13. What confounds are routinely present in the measurement that the field does not
    control for? Would a practitioner in this domain recognize those confounds immediately?
14. Has anyone in the field formally challenged the validity of the primary measurement
    instrument? What happened to that challenge?

ON THE CITATION NETWORK

15. Name the 5-10 most-cited papers in this subfield. Do they primarily cite each other
    or do they cite primary experimental evidence?
16. Is there a small founding cluster of papers that everything else cites?
    Were those papers directly tested or were their claims adopted as premises?
17. What disciplines are represented in the dominant citation network?
    What disciplines with relevant expertise are absent?
18. Are there papers that challenged the consensus that are rarely cited or cited only
    to be dismissed? What were their arguments?

ON EXTRAPOLATION

19. At what population level were the foundational studies conducted?
    (Lab subjects? Survey respondents? Intermediate-skill practitioners? Amateur populations?)
20. At what population level does the field make its claims?
    (Elite performers? Real-world professional behavior? Population-level policy outcomes?)
21. What is the gap between those two levels? What assumptions are required to bridge them?
    Are those assumptions stated or implicit?
22. Are there contexts where the field’s model predicts convergence or improvement that
    have not materialized? How does the field explain those failures?

ON THE RATCHET

23. What interventions has the field prescribed? At what scale have they been implemented?
24. What metrics improved after implementation? What metrics did not?
25. Was the primary outcome measure independent of the intervention — i.e., did it
    measure the underlying construct, or did it measure compliance with the intervention?
26. Has the intervention been reversed or substantially modified in any jurisdiction or
    institution? What happened? How did the field respond?
27. When the intervention fails on its own terms, what does the field prescribe next?

-----

SECTION B: CAN THE CHALLENGE BE RUN?

If you answer yes to most of these questions, you have the raw material to run the challenge.

ON YOUR DOMAIN KNOWLEDGE

28. Do you have direct experience with the phenomenon the field claims to explain?
    At what level — amateur, professional, elite?
29. Can you identify specific instances where the field’s model predicts something that
    does not match what you observe in practice?
30. Do you know which confounds the field routinely ignores because you have
    encountered them directly?
31. Can you name the proxy problem — the specific gap between what the field measures
    and what it claims to explain — in terms that someone in the field would recognize
    as a legitimate methodological objection?

ON THE DECISIVE TEST

32. What evidence would directly test the central claim at the appropriate level of analysis?
33. Does this data exist? Who has it? Has it been collected and not published,
    or has it genuinely not been collected?
34. If the data does not exist, what would it take to collect it?
    Is there a natural experiment or an existing dataset that approximates it?
35. If you had the decisive test result, would it be publishable? Or would the field’s
    gatekeeping prevent it from reaching the literature regardless of the finding?

ON YOUR POSITION

36. Do you have something to lose by running this challenge?
    (Tenure, funding, professional relationships, institutional standing)
37. Do you have something to gain that is not career-dependent?
    (Intellectual satisfaction, correction of a harm you have observed,
    contribution to a field you care about)
38. Are you willing to publish under your own name with receipts?
39. Are you willing to specify what evidence would change your own conclusion?

-----

SECTION C: CALIBRATION QUESTIONS

These questions tell you whether your challenge is methodological or motivated.
Answer them before you start writing.

40. Is there a version of the field’s central claim that you accept as valid?
    What is it? Where does the field’s overreach begin?
41. Can you describe the strongest version of the opposing argument — the best case
    the field could make for its claims — before dismantling it?
42. If the decisive test came back in the field’s favor, would you update?
    What would that update look like?
43. Are there aspects of the field’s work you would cite approvingly in your challenge?
    (If the answer is no, your challenge may be advocacy rather than methodology.)
44. Is the failure mode you are identifying a property of the field’s institutional
    structure, or are you making a claim about the motives of individual researchers?
    (The former is documentable. The latter requires stronger evidence.)

-----

RED FLAGS — stop and reconsider if:

- You cannot describe a version of the field’s claim you accept (Q40)
  → you may be writing a polemic, not a methodological challenge
- You cannot specify what evidence would change your conclusion (Q42)
  → your challenge is not falsifiable
- Your challenge requires attributing motive rather than documenting method (Q44)
  → you need more evidence before publishing
- You answered no to most of Section B
  → domain expertise is necessary but not sufficient

GREEN FLAGS — proceed if:

- You can name the proxy problem in terms the field would recognize as legitimate
- You know which confounds are ignored because you have encountered them directly
- The decisive test is specifiable and the data either exists or is collectible
- You can describe the strongest opposing argument before dismantling it
- You are willing to publish under your own name with receipts
- You can answer Q40-44 honestly without becoming defensive

-----

WHAT TO DO WITH YOUR ANSWERS

If Section A is mostly yes and Section B is mostly yes:
You have the raw material. Use the methodology in the main document to operationalize it.
The how-to guide (8 steps) is the production infrastructure. The citation spine prompt
in Step 2 is your first tool.

If Section A is mostly yes but Section B is weak:
The failure mode exists but you may not be the right person to run this challenge in
this field, or the decisive test may be genuinely unavailable. Consider whether you
can partner with someone who has the missing domain knowledge or institutional access.

If Section C reveals motivated reasoning:
Run the calibration questions again after writing a steelman of the field’s position.
A methodological challenge that can survive steelmanning is publishable.
One that cannot is a polemic — which has its place, but is a different project.
