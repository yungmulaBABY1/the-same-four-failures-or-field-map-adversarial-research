CITATION NETWORK AUDIT PROMPT:
DETECTING FUNCTIONAL CITATION CARTELS AND ADJACENT FIELD NEGLECT

Companion to: The Same Four Failures, Different Fields
GitHub: https://github.com/yungmulaBABY1/the-same-four-failures-or-field-map-adversarial-research
Archive: DOI 10.5281/zenodo.20201226

-----

PURPOSE

Use this prompt to audit whether a field, subfield, or contested claim is supported by a healthy evidence base or by a functionally closed citation network.

The goal is not to prove that individual researchers acted in bad faith.
The goal is to test whether the literature’s apparent consensus is produced by converging evidence, or by repeated citation of the same premise, same proxies, same review hubs, same author clusters, and same neglected adjacent literatures.

This prompt is designed for fields where a domain expert suspects that:
- a small founding cluster dominates the literature;
- weak proxy studies are being cited as if they directly measured the target construct;
- review papers are laundering a premise into “settled science”;
- adjacent fields with direct expertise are ignored;
- recent papers continue recycling old claims rather than testing the decisive question.

-----

IMPORTANT DEFINITION

In this prompt, “citation cartel” does not require proof of secret coordination, illegal collusion, or private bad faith.

It means a functional citation structure:
a small group of papers, authors, journals, theoretical frameworks, or institutional networks repeatedly cite one another, recycle the same load-bearing premise, neglect adjacent literatures, and convert repetition into apparent consensus.

Use “cartel” as a structural diagnosis, not a motive claim.

Preferred formal terms:
- functionally closed citation network
- self-referential citation cluster
- citation laundering pathway
- premise-laundering hub
- adjacent-field neglect
- unsupported consensus construction

Use “citation cartel” only when the evidence supports closure, premise recycling, and systematic exclusion of relevant outside evidence.

-----

HOW TO USE THIS PROMPT

Paste this entire prompt into Grok or Claude.

Then add:
1. A one-paragraph description of the field or subfield.
2. The central contested claim.
3. Any founding papers or review papers you already know.
4. Any adjacent fields you suspect were ignored.
5. Any databases or citation sources available to you.

Recommended model routing:
- Use Grok for initial mapping and willingness to identify contested failure modes directly.
- Use Claude to synthesize network analysis into tables, diagrams, and structured prose.
- Use ChatGPT only after the core argument is stable, as a hostile reviewer and source-risk checker.

Do not rely on any model to invent exact citation metrics. If exact citation data are unavailable, require the model to label numbers as rough estimates or source leads.

SECTION A: CITATION NETWORK STRUCTURE AND CARTEL DETECTION

1. What are the top 50 most-cited papers on the central claim?
List at least the top 10-15 with:
- citation count
- year
- authors
- paper type
- source of citation count
- role in the literature

2. Of the top 50, what percentage of citations appear internal to this set?
That is: how many papers in the top 50 cite other papers in the top 50?

3. Calculate the mutual citation rate:
How many papers in the top 50 cite at least 3-5 other papers from the same top-50 cluster?

4. Identify the founding cluster:
Is there a small founding cluster, typically 3-8 papers, that dominates later citations?

5. What percentage of total citations in the network still flow back to this founding cluster?

6. In the last 5 years, what are the most-cited new papers on the claim?

7. Do recent papers primarily cite:
- the old founding cluster;
- the main review/hub papers;
- new independent empirical evidence;
- adjacent-field evidence;
- policy/guideline documents;
- commentary or theory papers?

8. Do the last 5 years show continued self-reinforcement of the original cluster, or genuine model updating?

9. Is there one main review paper or synthesis paper functioning as an amplification hub?

10. If yes, what does the hub do?
- accurately preserve the limits of original studies;
- simplify the original claims;
- overstate the original claims;
- convert weak evidence into a settled premise;
- export the claim into policy, public discourse, or other fields.

-----

SECTION B: EVIDENCE TYPE CODING

For the top 50 papers, code each paper by evidence type:

- primary empirical test of the central claim
- proxy study
- weak proxy study
- secondary analysis
- review article
- systematic review / meta-analysis
- theoretical / conceptual paper
- qualitative study
- survey study
- policy / guideline document
- commentary / editorial
- public-facing synthesis
- unclear / needs source audit

11. What percentage of the network’s authority rests on direct empirical tests of the central claim?

12. What percentage rests on reviews, proxies, theory papers, qualitative studies, or commentary?

13. How many papers directly measure the target construct at the relevant level of analysis?

14. How many papers use proxy measures?

15. How many papers are cited as if they answer the central claim despite measuring something narrower?

16. Is the network evidence-rich or citation-rich?

-----

SECTION C: CITATION POLARITY AND CLAIM USE

A citation is not automatically supportive. For each major citation, classify citation polarity:

- supportive citation
- neutral/background citation
- critical citation
- perfunctory citation
- inflated citation
- used as premise
- unclear without full text

17. For the top 15 papers, classify how they cite the founding cluster.

18. What percentage of citations to the founding cluster are supportive or premise-building rather than critical?

19. What percentage are merely background citations?

20. Are there any serious critical citations inside the dominant network?

21. If critiques exist, are they engaged substantively or cited and dismissed?

22. Does the network preserve the original limitations of key studies, or drop the limitations and retain only the conclusion?

-----

SECTION D: CLAIM MUTATION TRACKING

Citation laundering often occurs because claims mutate as they move downstream.

Track the main claim across the citation chain:

Original study claim -> first review restatement -> downstream paper claim -> public/policy claim

23. What did the original source actually claim?

24. What did the first major review or hub paper say the original source showed?

25. How do later papers restate the review?

26. Does a narrow finding become generalized?

27. Does “evidence consistent with X” become “X is established”?

28. Does “no effect in one proxy task” become “no meaningful difference in the real-world construct”?

29. Do caveats disappear after one or two citation steps?

30. Does the claim become stronger through repetition than it was in the original methods?

Create a claim-mutation table:

Original source | Original measured claim | Hub-paper restatement | Downstream restatement | Mutation severity | Notes

Mutation severity:
- none
- minor simplification
- moderate inflation
- major inflation
- premise laundering

-----

SECTION E: ADJACENT FIELD ENGAGEMENT / NEGLECT

31. Identify the most relevant adjacent fields or literatures with direct expertise on the same phenomenon.

Examples:
- psychometrics
- sports science
- neuroscience
- cognitive psychology
- behavioral genetics
- economics
- clinical medicine
- epidemiology
- education measurement
- labor economics
- criminology
- statistics / measurement theory
- domain-specific practitioner literature

32. What are the most important findings from those adjacent fields?

33. Do those findings support, complicate, or contradict the central claim?

34. What percentage of papers in the dominant cluster cite these adjacent literatures?

35. What percentage engage them substantively rather than mentioning them in passing?

36. Are there obvious canonical adjacent-field sources that should appear but do not?

37. Does the dominant literature cite adjacent fields selectively, only when they support the preferred claim?

38. Does the dominant literature treat adjacent-field evidence as irrelevant without explaining why?

39. Are there practitioners or domain experts whose knowledge would identify obvious confounds that the literature ignores?

Create an adjacent-literature neglect table:

Adjacent field | Canonical sources | Why relevant | Cited by dominant cluster? | Engagement quality | Effect of omission

Engagement quality:
- not cited
- cited in passing
- selectively cited
- substantively engaged
- directly tested against
- dismissed without engagement

Effect of omission:
- minor context loss
- moderate weakness
- major evidentiary gap
- fatal omission for the strong claim

-----

SECTION F: HUB DEPENDENCY AND REVIEW-PAPER LAUNDERING

40. Identify the main hub paper or review article.

41. What percentage of recent papers cite the hub?

42. What percentage cite the original primary studies directly?

43. What percentage cite the hub instead of inspecting the underlying evidence?

44. Does the hub accurately represent the original studies’ methods and limits?

45. Does the hub simplify or overstate the original evidence?

46. Does the hub cite adjacent literatures?

47. Does the hub become the field’s substitute for direct empirical evidence?

Calculate a hub-dependency summary:

Hub paper | Recent papers citing hub | Recent papers citing primary evidence directly | Adjacent-field citation rate | Hub accuracy | Hub risk

Hub risk:
- low
- moderate
- high
- laundering hub

-----

SECTION G: PATTERN RECOGNITION AND RATCHET BEHAVIOR

48. Does the literature treat proxy measures as equivalent to the actual construct?

49. Does it export findings from low-stakes, lab, amateur, convenience, or administrative settings to explain elite, real-world, professional, or long-term outcomes?

50. Has the field ignored natural experiments or long-running interventions that test its predictions?

51. When predictions fail, does the field update the model?

52. Or does the field expand the intervention and reinterpret failure as evidence that more intervention is needed?

53. Does the field specify falsification criteria?

54. Does the field identify what evidence would change its conclusion?

55. Does the intervention improve the measured metric while leaving the underlying construct unmeasured or unimproved?

56. Does the citation network reward papers that expand the premise more than papers that test it?

-----

SECTION H: IGNORED CRITIQUE AND NON-UPTAKE

57. Identify serious critiques, failed replications, natural experiments, practitioner objections, or contrary reviews.

58. Are these critiques cited by the dominant cluster?

59. If cited, are they:
- engaged;
- dismissed;
- minimized;
- reframed as ideological;
- ignored after citation;
- treated as settled against despite unresolved issues?

60. Did the citation network update after the critique appeared?

61. Did recent papers incorporate the critique into methods or continue citing the old premise unchanged?

62. Are practitioner or domain-expert warnings visible in forums, trade publications, professional debates, or community spaces?

63. If yes, did those warnings affect the academic citation network?

Create a critique-uptake table:

Critique / contrary evidence | Year | Source type | Main objection | Cited by dominant cluster? | Uptake status | Effect

Uptake status:
- ignored
- cited but not engaged
- dismissed
- partially incorporated
- materially changed the field
- unknown

-----

SECTION I: AUTHOR, INSTITUTION, JOURNAL, AND FUNDING CLUSTERING

64. Are the same authors or coauthors recurring across the founding cluster?

65. Are the same journals, conferences, departments, labs, or institutions recurring?

66. Are the same funding sources, NGOs, advocacy organizations, government agencies, or policy bodies recurring?

67. Does the intellectual network overlap with a social, institutional, funding, or policy network?

68. Are dissenting papers published in the same venues, or pushed to peripheral venues?

69. Does gatekeeping appear explicit, or is the closure functional?

70. Are there signs of ideological or policy alignment that shape what gets cited?

Classify closure type:
- normal small-field clustering
- author-network clustering
- journal/conference clustering
- funding/policy clustering
- theoretical-framework clustering
- advocacy-network clustering
- functional citation closure
- explicit gatekeeping
- insufficient evidence

-----

SECTION J: FALSE-POSITIVE AND BASELINE CHECK

Dense citation is not always a cartel. Some fields are small. Some topics naturally have a few canonical papers.

71. Is this field small enough that dense internal citation would be expected?

72. Compare the target network with a healthier adjacent subfield of similar size.
Is the internal citation rate unusually high?

73. Does the target field show less engagement with adjacent expertise than comparable fields?

74. Are researchers citing each other because they are the only people studying the question, or because they are excluding relevant outside evidence?

75. Does the founding cluster rest on strong primary empirical evidence?

76. Are the central claims directly tested repeatedly by independent groups?

77. Are limitations preserved downstream?

78. Are failed predictions acknowledged?

79. Are critics cited and engaged?

80. Does the field have a clear mechanism for updating?

If the answer to these is mostly yes, do not call it a cartel.
Call it a small but active literature.

SECTION K: CARTEL-RISK SCORING

Score each dimension from 0 to 3.

0 = no concern
1 = mild concern
2 = serious concern
3 = severe concern

Dimensions:

1. Founding-cluster dominance:
0 / 1 / 2 / 3

2. Internal citation density:
0 / 1 / 2 / 3

3. Recent-paper dependence on old cluster:
0 / 1 / 2 / 3

4. Review-hub laundering:
0 / 1 / 2 / 3

5. Proxy-to-construct mismatch:
0 / 1 / 2 / 3

6. Claim mutation downstream:
0 / 1 / 2 / 3

7. Adjacent-field neglect:
0 / 1 / 2 / 3

8. Lack of decisive tests:
0 / 1 / 2 / 3

9. Poor critique uptake:
0 / 1 / 2 / 3

10. Intervention ratchet / model non-updating:
0 / 1 / 2 / 3

Total score:
[0-30]

Interpretation:
0-6 = healthy or low-risk citation network
7-12 = normal field clustering with some weaknesses
13-18 = self-referential literature with significant blind spots
19-24 = high-risk citation laundering network
25-30 = functional citation cartel / structurally closed consensus

Do not use the final label without explaining which dimensions drive the score.

-----

RED FLAGS

Strong evidence of a functionally closed citation network:

- Top 50 papers show >60-70% internal citation density.
- Recent papers continue to heavily cite the same small founding cluster.
- Main review/hub paper is cited more often than original primary evidence.
- Adjacent fields with relevant expertise are cited at very low rates, especially below 10-15%.
- Contradictory evidence from adjacent fields is ignored or dismissed without engagement.
- Original limitations disappear downstream.
- A weak or narrow finding becomes stronger through repetition.
- The original premise is treated as settled fact rather than a testable hypothesis.
- The decisive test has not been run.
- Failed predictions do not trigger model revision.
- Critics are pathologized or ignored rather than engaged.

-----

GREEN FLAGS

Signs of a healthier field:

- Diverse citation sources beyond a small cluster.
- Regular substantive engagement with adjacent literatures.
- New papers build on fresh evidence rather than recycling the founding cluster.
- Primary empirical tests outnumber reviews/theory papers in load-bearing positions.
- Limitations are preserved downstream.
- Critical papers are cited and engaged.
- Falsification criteria are specified.
- Natural experiments and failed predictions cause model updating.
- Interventions are evaluated against the underlying construct, not only the preferred metric.

-----

REMINDER

Do not call something a cartel merely because researchers cite each other.
Do not call something a cartel merely because the field is small.
Do not call something a cartel because you disagree politically with the conclusion.

The strongest accusation is not:
“They cite each other.”

The strongest accusation is:
“A small cluster repeatedly cites itself supportively, preserves an untested premise, drops original limitations, neglects adjacent fields with direct expertise, fails to run the decisive test, and continues to shape recent papers despite contrary evidence.”

That is the cartel signature.

Start with suspicion if that is where the anomaly appeared.
Finish with receipts.
