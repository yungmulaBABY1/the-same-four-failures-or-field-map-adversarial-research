CONSTRUCT-VALIDITY AUDIT PROMPT — CLIMATE SCIENCE EDITION

CLAIM VS. MEASUREMENT

Purpose:

Use this prompt to audit one paper, dataset, model output, IPCC chapter/SPM statement, or institutional claim before making any broad claim about the state of climate science on a given question.

Do not evaluate whether the authors, the IPCC, or a given research group are acting in good faith.

Do not infer private motive (funding source, political affiliation) as a substitute for methodological analysis.

Do not summarize the source politely.

Do not defer to prestige, peer review, journal status, citation count, IPCC confidence language, model intercomparison consensus, or the political salience of the conclusion.

Do not treat “the models agree” as evidence that the underlying physical measurement is sound — model agreement can reflect shared structural assumptions (shared parameterizations, shared tuning targets, shared forcing datasets) rather than independent confirmation.

Your task is narrower:

Compare what the source actually measured or modeled to what the source claims, and to what later citations, assessment reports, media, or advocacy use it to prove.

The point is not to decide whether climate change is real or whether the field is corrupt.

The point is to test whether the specific measurement, proxy, or model output supports the specific claim being made with it.

-----

HOW TO USE THIS PROMPT

Paste the relevant source material below:

- Abstract
- Methods (instrumentation, proxy type, model configuration, reanalysis product, statistical method)
- Data / forcing inputs used
- Spatial and temporal domain
- Results
- Conclusion / Summary for Policymakers language if applicable
- Any key figures or quoted confidence statements
- If available: how later papers, IPCC assessment chapters, media, or advocacy groups cite this source

Then provide:

1. The contested claim this source is being used to support.
1. The sub-field or debate where the source is being used (e.g., attribution, paleoclimate reconstruction, regional impact projection, tipping-point risk, mitigation cost-benefit).
1. Any domain-specific concern you want tested.

AUDIT INSTRUCTIONS

You are auditing construct validity.

Do not begin by balancing the source with generic praise for climate science as an enterprise.

Do not add reassurance clauses unless they are doing real evidentiary work.

Do not use phrases like “it is important to acknowledge the scientific consensus” or “this does not undermine the broader picture” unless the qualification is necessary to prevent factual overstatement about THIS source’s specific measurement.

Do not soften “invalid proxy” or “circular tuning” into “minor limitation” if the measurement cannot support the claim as stated.

Do not treat IPCC confidence labels (likely, very likely, virtually certain), peer review, or multi-model agreement as evidence that a specific construct was measured correctly — evaluate the underlying measurement on its own terms.

Do not treat “climate change is real and anthropogenic” as a proxy for “this specific claim, in this specific paper, using this specific method, is well-supported.” Global-scale attribution of warming to greenhouse gases is a different, much more robust claim than most of the specific downstream claims (regional impacts, single-event attribution, tipping-point timing, damage-function magnitude) this audit will actually be applied to. Keep these separate.

Answer the following sections.

-----

1. EXACT CLAIM EXTRACTION

Identify three levels of claim:

A. Strongest claim made by the source itself:

[Extract the strongest claim the authors make, including any hedging language in the original — e.g., “likely,” “consistent with,” “cannot rule out.”]

B. Weaker defensible claim actually supported by the data:

[State the narrower claim the study/dataset/model run may support.]

C. Inflated claim made by later citations, IPCC summary text, media, or advocacy:

[State how the source is being used beyond its evidence — e.g., a “likely” attribution finding restated as certain, a single-model regional projection restated as “the science says.”]

Then answer:

- Are A, B, and C the same claim?
- Where does inflation occur — at the paper’s own abstract/press release, at the assessment-report synthesis stage, or at the media/advocacy stage?
- Does the source itself overclaim, or is the overclaim mainly downstream?

-----

1. MEASUREMENT EXTRACTION

State exactly what was measured or modeled.

Include:

- Data type: instrumental record, satellite record, reanalysis product, proxy (ice core, tree ring, coral, sediment, speleothem), GCM/ESM output, statistical/empirical model, economic damage function
- Spatial coverage vs. claimed spatial scope (station-level, regional, global)
- Temporal coverage, resolution, and calibration period vs. reconstruction/projection period
- Model configuration: which model(s), which forcing scenario(s) (RCP/SSP), ensemble size, single-realization vs. ensemble-mean
- Homogenization, splicing, bias-correction, or detrending method applied
- Control variables / confounding forcings accounted for (solar, volcanic, ENSO, aerosols, land-use)
- Exclusions (stations dropped, proxy series excluded, model runs discarded)
- What was not measured (e.g., a temperature reconstruction does not measure impact; a GCM temperature projection does not measure regional precipitation with the same skill)

Then answer:

- Is the measured/modeled variable direct evidence of the claimed construct?
- Or is it a proxy or model-derived estimate?
- If it is a proxy or model output, how close is it to the target construct, and what is the known uncertainty/error structure (proxy calibration error, model structural uncertainty, internal variability)?

1. LEVEL-OF-ANALYSIS CHECK

At what level was the evidence collected or generated?

Examples:

- Single proxy site / single station
- Regional proxy network / regional station network
- Global instrumental composite
- Single model, single realization
- Multi-model ensemble mean
- Multi-model ensemble spread (structural uncertainty)
- Reanalysis product (model-assimilated, not pure observation)
- Downscaled regional projection
- Economic/impact model driven by climate model output

At what level is the claim being applied?

Examples:

- Global mean temperature trend
- Regional/local climate impact
- Single extreme-weather event attribution
- Tipping-point timing or probability
- Economic damage or GDP-loss projection
- Policy-relevant “business as usual” scenario framing
- Species/ecosystem-level impact claim

Then answer:

- What assumptions are required to bridge the evidence level to the claim level (e.g., from global ensemble mean to a specific regional/local outcome)?
- Are those assumptions tested, or merely implied by using the model output as if it were a direct observation?
- Would a domain expert (e.g., a regional climatologist, an actuary, an agronomist) accept the bridge as valid for the specific claim?
- If the claim rests on a single emissions scenario (e.g., RCP8.5), is that scenario being presented as a likely/central trajectory or as a high-end bound? Is that distinction preserved downstream?

-----

1. CONFOUND AUDIT

Identify obvious confounds.

Ask:

- What variables could explain the measured/modeled outcome besides the proposed explanation (e.g., urban heat island contamination in station records, ENSO/volcanic noise misattributed to secular trend, land-use change conflated with radiative forcing, instrument/platform changes in a spliced record)?
- What confounds would a practitioner immediately notice (e.g., a station-network expert flagging siting changes, a dendroclimatologist flagging the divergence problem, a modeler flagging that the model was tuned to reproduce the 20th-century trend it is now being used to “predict”)?
- Did the authors control for or explicitly test these confounds?
- Did the authors acknowledge them?
- Did later citations (assessment chapters, media, advocacy) drop the acknowledged confounds and retain only the headline result?
- Would the omitted confounds change the interpretation or the confidence level assigned?

Classify each confound:

Confound | Domain obvious? | Controlled? | Acknowledged? | Impact if omitted

Impact options:

- Low
- Moderate
- High
- Fatal for the strong claim

-----

1. ECOLOGICAL / OBSERVATIONAL VALIDITY CHECK

Ask:

- Does the model or proxy resemble the real-world physical process being explained, at the spatial/temporal scale claimed?
- Does the calibration period/population resemble the period/domain the claim is applied to (e.g., is a proxy calibrated against a 20th-century instrumental period being extrapolated across a much longer or climatically different paleo interval)?
- Does the temporal resolution match the claimed process (e.g., decadal model output used to make claims about single-event attribution)?
- Is the study measuring a physical quantity, a model-derived estimate, an economic projection built on a model-derived estimate, or public/media framing of risk — and are these being conflated?
- Is internal (natural) variability distinguished from forced trend, and is that distinction preserved in the claim?

Verdict:

- High observational/ecological validity
- Moderate observational/ecological validity
- Low observational/ecological validity
- Valid only for a narrower claim (e.g., valid for global mean trend, not valid for regional/local claim)
- Not valid for the claim being made

-----

1. DECISIVE-TEST CHECK

What test would directly measure the claimed construct?

Answer:

- What would the decisive test measure (e.g., an independent proxy network with a different physical basis, an out-of-sample model hindcast not used in tuning, a longer instrumental baseline, an independent reanalysis product)?
- What spatial/temporal domain would it cover?
- What controls would be required (confounding forcings isolated, internal variability characterized)?
- What outcome would support the source’s claim?
- What outcome would weaken or falsify it?
- Does the current source run that test?
- If not, does the field (via assessment reports or review literature) acknowledge that the decisive test has not been run, or is the gap papered over with confidence language?

Then state:

“The decisive test would be: [ONE-SENTENCE TEST SPECIFICATION].”

-----

1. CITATION-USE AUDIT

If later papers, IPCC assessment chapters, SPM text, media, or advocacy cite this source:

Ask:

- Are they citing the actual finding, with its original hedging language intact?
- Are they citing an inflated version (e.g., “likely” become “will,” a single-model regional result become “the science shows,” an RCP8.5 scenario become “business as usual”)?
- Are they using the source as evidence, or as a rhetorical premise?
- Does the source become stronger through repetition across assessment cycles than it was in the original methods (citation accretion)?
- Does the citation chain preserve the uncertainty bounds and caveats present in the underlying chapter, or does the SPM/media layer strip them?
- Is model ensemble spread being reported as a statistical confidence interval, when it is actually structural/scenario uncertainty across different models and assumptions?

Classify the downstream use:

- Accurate citation
- Slightly inflated citation
- Major inflation
- Citation laundering
- Premise laundering (scenario framing, e.g., RCP8.5-as-BAU, presented as settled input rather than contested assumption)
- Unsupported consensus construction

-----

1. IGNORED-LITERATURE CHECK

Ask:

- What adjacent literatures should have been cited (e.g., proxy divergence-problem literature, independent reanalysis comparisons, structural-uncertainty literature across CMIP generations, critiques of specific homogenization algorithms)?
- What contrary or complicating findings were available but structurally underweighted (e.g., satellite vs. surface record discrepancies, papers questioning a specific widely-used reconstruction, papers showing scenario probability weighting for RCP8.5)?
- Did the source cite those literatures?
- Did later assessment-report syntheses cite them, or smooth over the disagreement?
- Are there legitimate methodological critiques being conflated with denialist rhetoric, such that raising them is treated as illegitimate rather than evaluated on the merits?

Create a table:

Ignored literature / evidence | Why it matters | Was it cited? | Effect of omission

Effect options:

- Minor context loss
- Moderate weakness
- Major evidentiary gap
- Fatal omission for the strong claim

REMINDER

Suspicion, practitioner frustration, and cross-checking against a different data source are valid discovery tools. They are often how domain experts (station-network technicians, dendroclimatologists, actuaries, regional modelers) notice that a headline claim has outrun its underlying measurement.

But they are not final evidence, and they are not a license to treat the entire field as illegitimate.

The physical basis for anthropogenic greenhouse warming at the global scale is a different, more robust claim than most of what this audit will actually be applied to (regional impacts, single-event attribution, tipping-point timing, damage functions, scenario framing). Keep that distinction explicit in every verdict.
